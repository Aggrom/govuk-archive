Whistleblowing, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Whistleblowing

The official name for whistleblowing is ‘making a disclosure in the public interest’.

You can do this when there’s something wrong at work, like someone breaking the law or dangerous conditions. You could be protected from losing your job and being treated unfairly by your employer.

How to do it

  1. Check your employment contract or ask human resources/personnel if your company has a whistleblowing procedure.

  2. If you feel you can, contact your employer about the issue you want to report.

  3. If you can’t tell your employer, contact a ‘prescribed person or body’.

Free advice

You can get free advice from Public Concern at Work.

Protection you’ll have

Your employer can’t fire you or treat you unfairly (eg by not offering you a promotion you might otherwise have got).

You can take your employer to an Employment Tribunal if you’re treated unfairly.

Whistleblowing abroad

You’re protected from unfair treatment even when you blow the whistle on something that happened abroad. This includes when a different country’s law has been or will be broken.

Part 2: What you can blow the whistle on

You can report things that aren’t right, are illegal or if anyone at work is neglecting their duties, including:

  • someone’s health and safety is in danger
  • damage to the environment
  • a criminal offence
  • the company isn’t obeying the law (like not having the right insurance)
  • covering up wrongdoing

You can report something that’s happening now, has happened in the past or may happen in the future.

Part 3: Who to whistleblow to

If you can’t tell your employer, you can tell the ‘prescribed person or body’ responsible for the area you’re worried about.

Download ‘list of prescribed people or bodies’ (PDF, 83KB)

You can only tell the prescribed person or body if you think your employer:

  • will cover it up
  • would treat you unfairly if you complained
  • hasn’t sorted it out and you’ve already told them

Example: Laura knows the director of her bank is stealing clients’ money. She reports it to the Financial Services Authority.

Public sector whistleblowing

If you’re a public sector worker you can whistleblow to a minister, or a member of the Scottish Executive.

Part 4: Who's protected

You’re only protected if you’re:

  • an employee
  • an agency worker
  • not employed but in training with an employer
  • self-employed if you’re supervised or work off-site

You’re eligible for protection if you honestly think what you’re reporting is true and you think you’re telling the right person.

You’re not protected if:

  • you break the law when you report something (eg you signed the Official Secrets Act)
  • you found out about it when someone wanted legal advice (‘legal professional privilege’) - eg you’re a solicitor
