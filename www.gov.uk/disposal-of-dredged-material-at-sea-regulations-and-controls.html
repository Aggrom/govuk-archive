Disposal of dredged material at sea: regulations and controls

How to apply for licences for harbour authorities, dredging companies and the marine aggregate industry for capital and maintenance dredging.

Introduction

Organisations like harbour authorities, port and marina operators and developers need to dredge, periodically, in order to deepen harbour and navigation channels or prepare the seabed before laying cables or pipelines. The disposal of this dredged material at sea is, however, strictly controlled under the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 (FEPA) licensing system because it is potentially very damaging to marine life and the marine environment.

The type of licence required and the cost depends on whether the operation is classified as ‘capital’ or ‘maintenance’ dredging. FEPA licences usually only last for one year, but in some cases longer term licences are available for ongoing dredging operations.

Disposing of materials at sea

Disposing of materials at sea can pose a serious threat to marine life and the marine environment so it is strictly controlled and requires a licence granted under FEPA. In most cases, disposing of materials at sea also requires consent under the Coast Protection Act 1949 (CPA).

Most forms of disposal at sea are now prohibited, with the exception of material dredged from harbours and ports. But even this is strictly controlled and is only allowed where the dredged material can’t be used beneficially, for example to replenish beaches.

If the dredged material cannot be used beneficially then it is disposed of at licensed disposal sites. There are over 200 sites in the waters around the UK, of which about 100 are in use at any one time. To find out where these are in England and Wales you can find disposal of dredged materials information on the Marine Management Organisation (MMO) website.

As well as dredged material there are other types of waste that may be considered for disposal at sea, including fish waste from processing plants.

FEPA licence exemptions

There is, however, a range of material-depositing activities that are exempt from the FEPA licensing requirements. These include the deposit of:

  • scientific instruments
  • fishing gear
  • material on the site of drilling for oil and gas

You can read a detailed list of exemptions from FEPA licensing on the MMO website.

The future of marine licensing

From spring 2011, the marine licensing system will be simplified and the existing FEPA and CPA systems will be replaced by a single licensing system. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Waste management licences

As long as you have the correct FEPA licence - or the materials you deposit are exempt from FEPA licensing - you will not need to get a waste management licence from the Environment Agency.

Disposing of dredged materials

Dredging may be carried out periodically by harbour authorities, port and marina operators, dredging companies, developers and the marine aggregate industry for the following reasons:

  • creating navigational channels
  • maintaining a safe depth of water for navigational purposes
  • restoring an adequate flow of water to lessen the risk of flooding
  • removing areas of seabed during construction works
  • preparing the seabed before laying cables and pipelines
  • extracting marine aggregates like sand and gravel

The MMO issues licences for the disposal of dredged material at sea under FEPA. The charge for a FEPA disposal licence varies depending on the type and quantity of the material dredged. This is known as the licence band. Different charges also apply depending on whether the material is classified as ‘capital’ or ‘maintenance’ dredgings - see the next page in this guide on capital or maintenance dredging.

You can check the cost of a FEPA disposal licence on the MMO website.

How the MMO treats licence applications

By defining each dredged material, the MMO then knows how to treat an application for a licence, although it is not always straightforward. There may be cases, for example, where a capital dredging operation includes a small amount of maintenance dredging and the MMO decides that both materials can be disposed of at the same site. They may regard all the material as capital and include it on a single licence but if they can’t be disposed of together, separate licences will be required.

If the dredged material is destined for a beneficial purpose, like enhancing or conserving habitats, the MMO will issue a ‘beneficial use’ licence. These typically cost significantly less than disposal licences.

Capital or maintenance dredging

Dredging operations are classed as either ‘capital’ or ‘maintenance’ for licensing purposes.

Capital dredgings

Capital dredgings refer to materials excavated from a seabed area which has not been dredged for ten years.

Maintenance dredgings

Maintenance dredgings come from:

  • areas where the seabed level is no lower after dredging than it has been for the past ten years
  • areas where there is evidence that dredging has taken place during the last ten years to the proposed level or lower

Costs

Licence costs for capital dredgings disposal are generally more expensive than maintenance dredgings because:

  • capital dredging is often associated with other works, which means the MMO needs to consult with other regulators before issuing a licence
  • capital dredging may generate material that hasn’t been categorised before, which is likely to need more thorough monitoring to validate dispersal predictions and environmental impact
  • the quantity of capital material dredged or the characteristics of the sediment may mean that a new disposal site has to be designated
  • there is often a need to examine whether the capital material can be used beneficially or to lessen the effects of the dredging

You can read a detailed comparison of the costs of capital and maintenance dredging licences on the MMO website.

Contaminated dredged marine sediments

Historic and ongoing contamination of bottom sediments has been caused by:

  • mining and other industries in river catchments
  • industrial shipping and other activities at the coast, in estuaries and particularly within ports

Contaminated sediments can pose environmental and social risks. But it is increasingly necessary to dredge these sediments to create new and improved port facilities that can accommodate large vessels and the expansion of associated maritime trade.

Disposing of contaminated dredged marine sediment

Before applying for a licence to dispose of contaminated marine sediments, you should consider whether there are alternative or beneficial uses for the sediments. If there are, and they will take place in the marine environment, they require a FEPA ‘beneficial use’ licence.

If it is not possible for the sediments to be used in this way, the port authority carrying out the proposed dredging must apply for a FEPA disposal licence to dump the material at sea at a designated disposal site.

Before the MMO will issue a licence, the impact of displacing and disposing the sediments must be thoroughly assessed. This assessment will include identifying whether an area is affected by the presence of contaminants, such as metals or tributyltin.

You can find out about the management of contaminated marine sediments on the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website.

Licences for longer-term dredging

Most dredging campaigns are only carried out periodically and licence extensions are only granted in certain circumstances.

Ongoing dredging

The MMO will consider licensing on-going dredging operations on a longer-term basis. On-going operations include large-scale capital projects, or instances where a broadly similar amount of dredged maintenance material from the same licensed area is removed on an annual basis.

Conditions of longer-term licensing

Before issuing longer-term licences, the MMO needs to be satisfied that:

  • the material being disposed of is consistently uncontaminated
  • there would be no viable alternative method of disposal, which would prevent the material from needing to be disposed of at sea, during the period of the licence
  • ongoing monitoring and inspection of the operation does not raise new concerns about its potential impact on the marine environment and other uses of the sea

Duration of longer-term licences

Longer-term licences are available to cover three years in one-year periods. But consideration must be given every year during the period of the licence to alternative methods of disposing of the material, with a view to reducing the overall quantity of material licensed.

There are also further conditions depending on whether the operation is classed as capital or maintenance dredging.

For a longer-term licence to be issued for disposing of capital dredgings, the operation must be scheduled to continue for more than 12 months.

For a longer-term licence to be issued for disposing of maintenance dredgings:

  • the current and previous two licences must have been issued for the same dredged area, with any variations in the licensed quantity of material for disposal not exceeding plus or minus 25%
  • the results of sample analysis - including samples obtained for the previous three licence applications - must show that the levels of contaminants are acceptable, and that the nature of the dredged material and the sensitivity of the disposal area do not give cause for special consideration
  • there has to have been full compliance with the terms and conditions of the current licence

When the MMO issues a licence, they advise the licensee on whether they meet the conditions for a longer-term licence. If not, they issue a standard licence, except where an application has been refused.

You can check the cost of longer-term licences on the MMO website.

CPA consents and FEPA licences

The current system of CPA consents and FEPA licensing is used to protect the offshore environment.

CPA consents

CPA consent is required for:

  • the construction, alteration or improvement of any works that are on, under or over any part of the seashore lying below the level of mean high water springs
  • the deposit of any object or materials below the level of mean high water springs
  • the removal of any object or materials from the seashore below the level of mean low water springs - for example by dredging

CPA consent normally lasts for three years. But if you need a FEPA licence as well as CPA consent, you can choose to have the duration of the consent changed so that they both expire at the same time. You can find out about CPA jurisdiction and what’s considered before consent is granted on the MMO website.

FEPA licences

FEPA licences are mainly used to permit construction within the marine environment and to control deposits of articles into the sea or tidal waters. You can get details of the activities a FEPA licence is needed for on the MMO website.

FEPA licences for dredging usually last for one year, but longer-term licences may also be issued - see the page in this guide on licences for longer-term dredging.

A FEPA ‘beneficial use’ licence will be issued if dredged material can be put to beneficial use in the marine environment instead of being dumped. You can find out about beneficial use licences on the MMO website.

FEPA licence exemptions

There are a range of activities that involve depositing materials into the sea which are exempt from the FEPA licensing requirement. These include deposits of:

  • scientific instruments
  • fishing gear
  • material at an oil or gas drilling site

You can read a detailed list of exemptions from FEPA licensing on the MMO website.

The future of marine licensing

From spring 2011 the marine licensing system will be simplified and the existing CPA and FEPA systems will be replaced by a single licensing system. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Applying for CPA consents and FEPA licences

Before you apply for a FEPA licence and/or a CPA consent you need to decide if you have to:

  • contact the MMO - for large, novel or contentious projects you’re advised to get in touch with the MMO before you apply
  • provide an environmental statement - you can find out about environmental impact assessments on the MMO website
  • consider any ‘beneficial use’ - you can get information about FEPA licences for disposing of material in a beneficial way on the MMO website

Conservation regulations

Depending on the location of your proposed works, your application may be subject to either of the following:

  • conservation regulations that apply to works within 12 nautical miles offshore - find out about protected conservation areas on the MMO website
  • offshore marine conservation regulations that apply to works beyond 12 nautical miles - read about European protected sites on the MMO website

Fees

The cost of a FEPA disposal licence varies depending on whether the material is ‘capital’ or ‘maintenance’ dredging, and also on the quantity of material dredged. You can find out how much your FEPA licence will be on the MMO website.

The application process

You can apply for CPA consent and/or a FEPA licence by:

  • filling in an online application form
  • downloading or requesting an application form and sending the completed form to the MMO

You can get application forms and find out how to apply for CPA consent and FEPA licences on the MMO website.
