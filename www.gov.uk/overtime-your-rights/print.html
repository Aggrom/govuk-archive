Overtime: employee rights, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

What is overtime?

If you have normal working hours, overtime usually means any time you work beyond these hours.

Normal working hours are the hours fixed by your employment contract.

Overtime pay

Your employer doesn’t have to pay you for overtime. However, your average pay for the total hours you’ve worked mustn’t fall below the National Minimum Wage.

Your employment contract will usually include details of any overtime pay rates and how they’re worked out.

Part 2: Compulsory overtime

Can you be forced to work overtime?

You only have to work overtime if your contract says so.

Even if it does, by law, you can’t usually be forced to work more than an average of 48 hours per week. You can agree to work longer - this agreement must be in writing and signed by you.

Can you be stopped from working overtime?

Unless your contract guarantees you overtime, your employer can stop you working it.

However, your employer can’t discriminate against you, eg by letting others work overtime but stopping you from doing so.

Part 3: Part-time workers

Usually, part-time workers mustn’t be treated less favourably than full-time staff.

Unless it says differently in your employment contract, your employer will usually only pay you overtime if you work:

  • longer hours than set out in your contract
  • more than the normal working hours of full-time staff and full-time staff would get extra pay for working these hours
  • unsocial hours (eg late at night) for which full-time staff would get more pay

Part 4: Time off and paid leave

Time off in lieu (TOIL)

Some employers give you time off instead of paying for overtime. This is known as ‘time off in lieu’.

The terms (eg when you can take it) are agreed between you and your employer.
