Introduction

There are many pests and diseases that can seriously damage crops and plants in the UK. To protect plant health in England and Wales the UK Plant Health Service sets policy and enforces controls and restrictions on the import, movement and keeping of certain plants, plant pests and other materials such as soil.

The Plant Health and Seeds Inspectorate (PHSI) is part of the Food and Environment Research Agency (Fera), an executive agency of the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra), and is the part of the UK Plant Health Service, which implements plant health policy in England and Wales.

If you grow, import, export or move certain plant material you will need to understand and use the PHSI’s services.

Legislation affecting plant health

Plant health legislation controls the import and movement of certain plants, seeds and organic matter - such as soil - and certain plant products, including fruit, potatoes, vegetables, cut flowers, foliage and grain.

Controls differ according to the species - and whether or not they are classified as quarantine organisms - but could include the need for classification, a phytosanitary certificate, a plant passport and/or inspection requirements.

Fera’s Plant Health Division is responsible for plant health policy in England as set out in the Plant Health (England) Order 2005 (as amended).

PHSI is part of Fera and implements and enforces plant health policy in England (and in Wales on behalf of the Welsh Government). If you want to grow, import, export or move certain plants or plant material, you will need to use the PHSI’s services.

There are two main elements of plant health control relevant to growers and crop farmers:

  • protection against quarantine organisms - measures to prevent the introduction and spread of harmful plant pests and diseases which are not established in the EU
  • certification - ensures that high-quality planting material which is free from specific pests and diseases is available to growers

If you find or suspect a quarantine organism, you must contact your local PHSI immediately. Download a list of PHSI offices from the Fera website (PDF, 25K).

Some plants and plant produce are prohibited from entering this country while others must meet certain requirements and be accompanied by a phytosanitary certificate.

If you export plants, plant products, seeds, soil or agricultural machinery to non-EC countries you must satisfy the plant health requirements of the importing country.

Requirements for forestry and wood are covered by separate Plant Health (Forestry) legislation. For more information on this, you can call the Forestry Commission Plant Health Service helpline on 0131 314 6414.

Certification schemes for plant health control

Certification is one of the two key elements of plant health control along with requirements on quarantine organisms. Certification schemes ensure that growers and farmers have access to high-quality planting material to meet prescribed standards on freedom from plant pests and diseases.

Certification schemes

  • The statutory Seed Potato Classification Scheme (SPCS) ensures that seed potatoes marketed meet the specified standards. It also protects purchasers by ensuring that seed potatoes are healthy, good quality, true to variety and free from mixtures. To find out more about the SPCS, see potatoes.
  • The Plant Health Propagation Scheme (PHPS) is a voluntary scheme which promotes the production and use of healthy, proven planting stock. It covers soft fruit (eg strawberries, berry fruit, currants), top fruit (eg apples, pears, cherries, plums), hops, cobnuts and bulbs (eg narcissus and iris). You can find out about the PHPS on the Fera website.

Phytosanitary certificates

Importers must check if a plant product requires phytosanitary certification to be allowed entry into the EU. You must register with Defra before you import any controlled plant products. These certificates confirm that the goods have been inspected in their country of origin and meet EU standards on the absence of pests and diseases.

Quarantine pests and plant passporting

Requirements on quarantine pests and diseases are one of the two key elements of plant health control, along with certification.

Quarantine measures help to keep foreign pests out of areas where they could damage crops, trees, wild plants and ecosystems. Under plant health legislation a number of plant pests and diseases are classified as quarantine organisms and are therefore subject to statutory control. Quarantine pests and diseases include, but are not limited to:

  • Colorado beetle - download an information sheet on the Colorado beetle from the Fera website (PDF, 313K)
  • Phytophthora ramorum (also affects trees) - read about Phytophthora ramorum on the Fera website
  • Phytophthora kernoviae (also affects trees) - read about Phytophthora kernoviae on the Fera website
  • Potato brown rot - read about potato brown rot on the Fera website
  • Potato ring rot - read about potato ring rot on the Fera website
  • Citrus longhorn beetle (Anoplophora chinensis) - including a suspension until May 2012 of imports from China of Japanese maples (Acer palmatum) - download information on the citrus longhorn beetle from the Fera website (PDF, 1.72MB)
  • R. ferrugineus or any other Rhynchophorus species or Red palm weevil - which attacks date palm species and ornamental palms that are regularly imported into Britain - eg Chusan palm (Trachycarpus fortunei) - download a factsheet on red palm weevil from the Fera website (PDF, 314K)

You can find guidance and help with identifying EC quarantine pests and diseases on the European Plant Protection Organisation (EPPO) website.

The lists of quarantine pests undergo revision from time to time as EPPO makes new recommendations to be incorporated into the national phytosanitary regulations of EPPO member governments.

You can also find identification posters, information sheets, booklets and quarantine information cards on quarantine pests and diseases on the Fera website.

What to do if you identify a quarantine pest or disease

Early identification is important to help combat quarantine pests and diseases. If you find a quarantine organism or suspect one may be present, you should contact your local PHSI immediately to alert them. Download a list of PHSI offices from the Fera website (PDF, 25K).

For information on tree-related notifiable pests and diseases, you can call the Forestry Commission helpline on 0117 906 6000 or the Forestry Commission Wales on 0300 068 0300.

Plant passports

If you want to move certain plant material that is susceptible to quarantine plant pests within or between EC member states you will need a plant passport. Many growers are registered and authorised to issue plant passports which allow them to move the prescribed material within the EC. Registration is free and there are no time limits but there is a PHSI charge for annual authorisation.

There are additional requirements for movements into and within EC protected zones. You can access a range of factsheets on notifiable plant pests and diseases on the Fera website.

Cross Compliance for growers, nurseries and crop farmers

If you receive direct payments under Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) support schemes - including the Single Payment Scheme (SPS) - your agricultural land and activities must meet specified standards regardless of how much land is entered into the SPS. This is called ‘Cross Compliance’. Defra administers the Cross Compliance scheme via the Rural Payments Agency (RPA).

Read about the SPS in the guide on the Single Payment Scheme (SPS).

In order to receive full SPS payment - and certain rural development scheme payments - you must comply with a set of Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs) and keep your land in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAEC). These relate to areas of public, animal and plant health, environment and animal welfare.

All SMRs and most GAEC standards reflect legal requirements and best practice that you should already follow.

You can also find information on Cross Compliance requirements.

Further information for farmers and growers

If you grow or trade crops or plant products the following bodies can offer help and support:

Fera’s Plant Health and Seeds Inspectorate is responsible for import and export inspections of plants, plant products and certain other products such as soil, issuing phytosanitary certificates and scientific licences. You can call the Defra Plant Health Division on 01904 455 174 or contact your local PHSI. Download a list of PHSI offices from the Fera website (PDF, 25K).

eDomero is Fera’s online plant help service enabling you to electronically apply for exporting, plant passporting, potato classification, certification and import licensing without need for paper applications. You can find information and register for online plant health services using eDomero. If you have any queries about registering for eDomero, you can call the Fera Plant Health Imports helpdesk on 0844 248 0071.

The Health and Safety Executive’s Chemicals Regulation Directorate (CRD), formerly Defra’s Pesticides Safety Directorate, is responsible for ensuring the safe use of pesticides or plant protection products - and their approval process - for agricultural, horticultural and home garden use.

Only approved pesticides can be sold, supplied, used, stored or advertised in the UK.

CRD is also responsible for biocides, industrial chemicals and detergents to protect the health of people and the environment.

For further information on pesticides you can call the CRD helpline on 01904 455 775.

You can read about CRD’s work and the approval process for pesticides.

The Organic Food Federation promotes organic methods and maintains high standards and best practice within the farming sector. For more information, you can call the Organic Food Federation on 01760 720 444.

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about Cross Compliance. For further information, call the Cross Compliance helpline on 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, for information on cross-compliance requirements, see the guide on Cross Compliance: the basics.

For more general enquiries, you can call the Defra helpline on 08459 33 55 77.

The RPA is responsible for licences and schemes for growers as well as for running the SPS. For more information about SPS and how it can help your farming business call the RPA helpline on 0845 603 7777.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long term viability of rural communities.

You can read about the work of the NFU.
