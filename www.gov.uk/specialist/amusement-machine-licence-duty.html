Amusement Machine Licence Duty

You need to pay Amusement Licence Machine Duty on each of the fruit machines, quiz or gaming machines on your premises.

Introduction

If your business provides gaming machines in the UK that people can use to gamble - eg a fruit machine or quiz machine - you need to pay Amusement Machine Licence Duty. You do this by buying a licence for each machine.

You don’t need the licence for certain amusement-only, skill-based and small-prize machines.

This guide explains what the different kinds of licence are, how to apply for one, and also tells you what machines don’t need a licence.

When you need to apply for an amusement machine licence

Gaming machines provided for play on any premises in the UK need an amusement machine licence from HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).

If you own or lease a gaming machine, or if you manage, own or lease premises where a gaming machine is provided for play, then you need to apply for the licence.

Gaming machines are machines where players can gamble - that is, stake an amount of money and possibly win a prize. Fixed Odds Betting Terminals are also classified as gaming machines.

Exemptions from amusement machine licensing

You don’t need a licence for the following:

  • machines operating on domestic premises that only residents or non-paying individuals play
  • machines that are not gaming machines - such as video games, pinball machines - where the prize, if any, is only a ‘free play’
  • purely skill-based machines - such as crane-crab machines - that do not introduce an element of chance by using a ‘compensator’ - a device that monitors and manages levels of prizes that machines pay out
  • two-penny machines - where there is only a 2 pence stake
  • machines with a maximum stake of 30 pence and maximum prize of £8, where the cash element of the prize does not exceed £5
  • machines with a maximum stake of 10 pence and maximum prize of £15, with a maximum cash element of £8
  • machines with a maximum stake of £1 and a non-cash prize with a maximum value of £50
  • machines provided at an event where the whole proceeds of the event will be given to charity, including the profits from amusement machines, and the entertainment provided at the event does not consist only or mainly of the chance to win prizes from the machines

Find out about exemptions from amusement machine licensing in Notice 454 on the HMRC website

Apply and pay for an amusement machine licence

There are two different types of amusement machine licence:

  • premises-based licences
  • special licences

Which one is best for you will usually depend on whether you expect to move machines frequently between different premises.

You must display the original licence prominently at all times on the premises where the machines are located, unless you have agreed alternative arrangements with HMRC. Details about how to pay are on the HMRC website.

Premises-based licences

A premises-based licence allows you to license one or more machines at a single premises. Different machines require different types of licences. However, on a single application you can specify:

  • how many machines of each type should be covered by a premises licence
  • how long you want the licence to last - from one to 12 months

You will automatically be sent renewal reminders.

To apply for a premises-based licence, you should complete form L 222 and return it to HMRC.

Find form L 222 - Amusement Machine Licence Application on the HMRC website

Bulk applications for premises licences

If you have machines at several different premises and need to apply for more than one premises-based licence at any one time, you should make use of bulk application and issue facilities. To apply, contact the HMRC Excise & Customs Helpline on 0845 010 9000 or write to them at the following address:

HMRC Banking
St Mungo’s Road
Cumbernauld
Glasgow
G70 5WY

You should remember that your bulk application can only cover premises-based licences, and can’t include special licences.

Special licences

Special licences allow you to licence machines, rather than premises and are only available for 12-month periods. You can only apply for a special licence if both of the following apply:

  • you are applying to licence ten or more machines that all end on the same date, or you already hold other special licences when you apply which brings the total to ten or more that all end on the same date
  • the machines are Category C machines with a maximum prize of £10 in money or equivalent - such as vouchers or tokens

Find out which categories your machine comes under in Notice 454 on the HMRC website

To apply for a special licence, you should complete form L 224 and return it to HMRC.

Find form L 224 on the HMRC website

Deadlines when applying for a licence

You should return form L 222 or L 224 to HMRC, ensuring it is received by them at least 14 days before you want the licence to start, or 21 days if you’re making a bulk application.

If HMRC receives your request after the start date you ask for, the start date will be the day they receive your application. You won’t be able to make the machines available for play until that date.

Find out about Amusement Machine Licence Duty and how to apply for licences in Notice 454 on the HMRC website

Pay for an Amusement Machine Licence Duty

Details about how to pay are on the HMRC website. You must either include payment with the application form, or complete form L 223 on the HMRC website.

Rates for Amusement Machine Licence Duty

Gaming machines are divided into different categories, depending on the stakes required and prizes offered. To work out how much Amusement Machine Licence Duty you will need to pay, you need to first check which category or categories your machines fall into.

Find out which categories your machine comes under in Notice 454 on the HMRC website.

Once you know which category your machine falls into, you can work out how much the licence will cost according to how long you need the licence to last.

Duty relief for seasonal businesses

If your business is seasonal, like an amusement arcade in a seaside town, you may be able to claim a special duty relief which will reduce the amount you have to pay.

Find out about seasonal duty relief on the HMRC website

Spare machines

You may keep one spare unlicensed machine for up to three licensed machines on the premises, or two spare unlicensed machines for four or more licensed machines, in case of breakdowns, provided you meet certain conditions.

Read guidance on keeping spare machines and Amusement Machine Licence Duty on the HMRC website

Record keeping and VAT for amusement machines

If you have a special licence for Amusement Machine Licence Duty, you’ll need to keep specific records. For all licences, you’ll need to bear in mind some specific VAT rules related to amusement machines.

Record keeping for Amusement Machine Licence Duty

If you have a special licence, you’ll have to keep records of certain details, including where the machines are.

Find out about record-keeping requirements for special licence holders on the HMRC website

There are no specific record-keeping requirements if you hold a premises-based licence, other than you must always make sure you operate within the terms of the licence.

Amusement machines and VAT

If you are registered for VAT, or should be, you will have to account for VAT on the VAT taxable takings of any amusement machines, whether they need an amusement machine licence or not.

This total is the amount put into the machine - even if any is stolen from the machine - less the amount returned by the machine as winnings to players. You cannot subtract winnings returned to you or to people acting on your behalf.

It also includes any money in the machine’s payout tubes in excess of the float.

Read guidance on VAT and amusement machines on the HMRC website
