Get started in farming

How to choose a farm, find an agent, and get training, employment advice and financial guidance if you want to start up a farming business

Introduction

The UK has approximately 300,000 active farms. The average size of UK farms is larger than their European equivalents - 57 hectares compared to 20 hectares.

This guide outlines the issues you should consider if you are thinking of becoming a farmer, such as deciding what type of farming you want to do, choosing a farm and finding an agent. It also covers your training needs and farming-specific employment issues.

You will also find financial and business guidance relevant to the sector, including support such as the Rural Development Programme for England.

Plan your farming business

Before buying a farm or taking on a farm tenancy, you will want to make sure that the business model for your proposed farming operation is sound. To do this, you will need to draft a business plan, setting out your objectives and goals for your farm.

Your plan will need to be backed up by solid research into the type of farming you want to do, where you want to do it, and include both national and regional sources of business support.

For more information, see the guide on how to prepare a business plan.

Farm benchmarking is an online tool from the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra). You can use it to compare your business - or your business plan - against the performance of other farms of a similar type and size.

See the guide on farm business benchmarking.

Research the farming market

The Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS) Rural Market Survey provides current data on the farm and farmland market.

Find Rural Market Survey information on the RICS website.

Search for UK farming statistics on the UK Agriculture website.

To fine-tune your research, you can use a rural agent. An agent can advise you about farms in the region suitable for your needs, and about local conditions and regulations.

Find a rural agent on the UK Land and Farms website.

Search online for farms

You can search online databases to find farms and farmland for sale and to rent.

Search for a farm or farmland on the Farmers Weekly Interactive website.

When looking at properties, you should assess the usable area - the total acreage (hectareage) will not all be suitable for your type of farming.

If you are purchasing a farm, you will need to commission a commercial survey of the property and the surrounding land.

You can find a surveyor on the RICS website.

Choose the right location for your farm

Locations with good connections to cities and towns can be important, particularly for rural businesses based on farms - eg farm shops - and for businesses targeting urban populations. Location is also important for farms, as it largely determines what types of agriculture are practicable - eg hill farming.

If you live in a city or urban area, think about buying a farm nearby. This will give you access to urban and suburban farmers’ markets, and if you have another job, you can commute to it until the business is established.

There are two main types of UK farming:

  • pastoral - livestock rearing on grassland - mainly north and west
  • arable - land ploughed to grow crops - mainly south and east

Find out about types of UK farming on the UK Agriculture website.

For further advice on best parts of country for different types of farming, consider joining the National Farmers’ Union (NFU).

Types of land

When assessing farming properties, you should check the proportions of different land types, for example:

  • tillable land - land that can be cultivated quite easily to grow crops
  • pasture - land that is normally left uncultivated and down to pasture
  • woodland
  • marginal land

Choose a farm where the land types will be consistent with your business plan. You will need a minimum acreage (hectarage) of tillable land for your main crop, for example, to achieve your projected revenue. Remember too that other types of land, eg woodland, can provide a secondary or diversified income.

For more information, see the guide on ideas and funding for diversification.

Crop yields on marginal land will be limited, so farms with a high percentage of poorly drained, thin topsoil will not be suitable to grow high-value crops such as flowers.

Marginal land can be improved, but this can be expensive, and you should only buy it if you will be using it mainly for pasturage.

For more information, see the guide on grazing and pasture.

Water supply

You should check the potential of land for flooding and ensure that it has enough water for your animals, or to irrigate crops.

Find out about and access the flood map on the Environment Agency website.

For more information, see the guide on air, water and soil quality: the basics.

Biodiversity and historical features

Farms may contain land with areas of high value to wildlife or important cultural sites, such as Sites of Special Scientific Interest (SSSIs) or historical monuments.

You will not be able to develop these areas for farming purposes, but they may provide extra revenue in form of visits from the public, eg birdwatchers or historical tours, which can generate income from farm shops or accommodation.

For more information, see the guides on protecting farm environments: the basics and SSSI and historical monuments.

Planning policies

You should also take into account local or regional planning schemes, implemented by local authorities that may restrict future commercial development on your farm. Your surveyor or rural agent will be able to advise you on these issues.

For more information, see the guide on planning land use.

The Rural Development Programme for England

The Rural Development Programme for England (RDPE) is the main funding support mechanism for rural communities and businesses.

The programme aims to:

  • improve competitiveness of the agricultural and forestry sectors
  • improve environment and countryside
  • support diversification of rural economy
  • improve quality of life in rural areas

ES funding is available for environmentally friendly farming and land management practices. To be eligible for an ES scheme, you must comply with the cross compliance standards of farming management.

See the guides on Environmental Stewardship: the basics, Entry Level Stewardship (ELS), Organic Entry Level Stewardship (OELS) and Higher Level Stewardship (HLS).

You can also find information on the Uplands ELS strand of ES in the guide on hill farming.

For more general information, see the guide on cross compliance: the basics.

Examples of farming activities eligible for RDPE funding include:

  • setting up farm management, farm relief and farm or forestry advisory services
  • vocational training and information for people working in farming, food and forestry
  • farm diversification into non-agricultural activities
  • support for creation and development of agricultural micro-enterprises

Find out about RDPE funding on the RDPE Network website.

For more information, see the guide on the RDPE.

Other grants and support

Other sources of funding and advice for getting started in farming include:

  • government advice on securing funding
  • commercial loans and overdrafts
  • technological funding schemes

For more information, see the page on other funding sources for diversification in the guide on ideas and funding for diversification.

Employment of farm workers

If you are starting up in farming and plan to employ people, you must be aware of the employment regulations specific to this sector.

Wages, conditions and disputes

The Agricultural Wages Board sets minimum pay rates for workers in agriculture, as well as other terms and conditions, such as holidays and rest breaks and these are set out in the annual Agricultural Wages Order. You can obtain a copy of the Order and get further advice on the provisions by calling the Pay and Work Rights Helpline on 0800 917 2368.

For more information, see the guide on agricultural wages.

Where there is a dispute over pay, the Agricultural Wages Enforcement Team of Defra can advise employers.

For more information, see the page on tenant farmers and diversification in the guide on ideas and funding for diversification.

You can also contact Acas to help resolve a dispute. Find out about the work of Acas on the Acas website.

Gangmaster licensing and seasonal labour

If you employ teams of agricultural workers through a sub-contractor, you will need a licence from the Gangmaster Licensing Authority (GLA).

For more information, see the guide on gangmasters licensing.

You may also be eligible to recruit seasonal labour under the Seasonal Agricultural Workers Scheme (SAWS).

Find out about the SAWS on the UK Border Agency website.

Health and safety

Farming sector workers are protected by specific health and safety regulations. These cover areas such as transport, preventing falls, and exposure to chemicals or pathogens.

Find out about health and safety in the agricultural sector on the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) website.

For more information, see the guide on farm health and safety: the basics.
