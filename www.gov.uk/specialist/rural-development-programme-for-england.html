Introduction

If you are a farmer, grower, forester, land owner, rural business or part of a rural community, you can potentially benefit from funding provided through the Rural Development Programme for England (RDPE).

The funding is managed according to the activity being proposed.

The Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) administers RDPE-funded grants for socio-economic and social projects, which aim to increase the competitiveness of farmers, improve the quality of rural life and diversify the rural economy. This delivery has been taken over from Regional Development Agencies. Find out about the transition of responsibility for RDPE funding on the Defra website.

Natural England administers the RDPE-funded Environmental Stewardship Scheme and the Energy Crops Scheme.

The Forestry Commission administers the RDPE-funded English Woodland Grant Scheme.

From April 2012 RDPE delivery will consist of a single offer, available across the whole of England on consistent lines and with a consistent set of business processes.

Local Action Groups have funding priorities for their local areas.

Farmers who receive RDPE funding for farming and land management projects need to meet cross compliance standards on these activities.

Activities that are eligible for funding from the Rural Development Programme for England

RDPE delivery under Defra will consist of a single offer, available across the whole of England on consistent lines, with certain elements of regional and/or sectoral targeting, and with a consistent set of business processes.

Axes 1 and 3 of the RDPE are to be delivered directly by Defra at national level. 

The Leader approach programmes - ie RDPE Axis 4 - will be delivered by existing Local Action Groups (LAGs) in liaison with Defra.

RDPE Axis 2 agri-environment programmes will continue to be delivered by Natural England and the Forestry Commission.

Delivery of these programme elements will continue for the remainder of the programme period to December 2013. 

Defra has committed to continue funding all Axes of the RDPE for the remainder of the Programme. However, budgets are being reduced in some areas and this means that funding will be under increased pressure for the rest of the programme period through to December 2013. Projects in the following key areas are more likely to be eligible for funding:

  • farming competitiveness
  • the Uplands
  • rural broadband
  • tourism
  • skills development

You can read about typical RDPE-funded projects on the RDPE network website.

In general, the RDPE supports eligible activity to achieve objectives such as:

  • good stewardship of land to conserve, enhance and promote the countryside through management practices in Environmental Stewardship
  • woodland projects that provide environmental and recreational benefits and/or improve the economic value of the woodland
  • vocational training and information for people working in farming, food and forestry, including advisory services
  • the setting up of farm management, farm relief and farm or forestry advisory services
  • farm modernisation - particularly investments relating to the conservation of traditional landscapes, rural heritage and relocation of farm buildings, or for the protection and improvement of the environment, hygiene conditions of livestock, animal welfare and occupational safety in the workplace
  • projects to add value to farm and forestry products through processing and marketing activities
  • co-operation for development of new products, processes and technologies in the agricultural and food sector
  • development of infrastructure related to the development and adaptation of agriculture and forestry
  • farm diversification into non-agricultural activities
  • support for the creation and development of agricultural micro-enterprises
  • encouragement of rural tourism activities
  • the provision of basic services for the economy and rural population

Agri-environment schemes - RDPE Axis 2

Activity to support the good stewardship of land and to conserve, enhance and promote the countryside may be eligible for support under the RDPE through agri-environment schemes such as the Environmental Stewardship Scheme, the Energy Crops Scheme and English Woodland Grant Scheme (EWGS).

These schemes provide funding for land managers to farm - or manage woodland - in environmentally sustainable ways.

You can get information about the Environmental Stewardship Scheme and Energy Crops Scheme on the Natural England website.

You can also get information about the EWGS on the Forestry Commission website.

Socio-economic schemes - RDPE Axis 1 and 3

The RDPE can also support eligible activities that seek to:

  • improve the competitiveness of the agricultural and forestry sector
  • improve the quality of life in rural areas
  • support the diversification of the rural economy

If you wish to apply for new funding you will need to apply to Defra.

If you are running an existing scheme, Defra RDPE delivery team (RDT) - or LAG for Leader projects - will continue to be your first point of contact.

The Leader approach - RDPE Axis 4

To find out about funding priorities in your area, find your LAG on the RDPE Network website.

Cross compliance

In order to be eligible for RDPE funding, cross compliance standards must be met. Under cross compliance, the farm must comply with Statutory Management Requirements and the land be maintained in good agricultural and environmental condition.

The Rural Payments Agency (RPA) is responsible for carrying out inspections to make sure farmers are meeting cross compliance standards, and for imposing penalties if farmers are in breach of cross compliance. For more information, see the guide on cross compliance: the basics.

How to get Rural Development Programme for England funding for rural socio-economic schemes

RDPE funding is also used to improve the competitiveness of the agricultural and forestry sector, improve the quality of life in rural areas or support the diversification of the rural economy. These strands fall under the remit of Axis 1 and 3 of the RDPE, and from 1 July 2011 their management transferred from eight Regional Development Agencies to Defra.

By 31 March 2012, RDPE delivery will consist of a single offer, available across the whole of England on consistent lines and with a consistent set of business processes.

RDPE Leader funding

An important element of the RDPE is the Leader approach. Leader is run through LAGs - groups of people drawn from the local community for example local volunteers, farmers, foresters, rural businesses and community representatives.

The LAGs help rural communities improve their quality of life and economic prosperity through small and medium-scale innovative projects. You can find out information about the Leader approach on the RDPE Network website.

Farming projects funded by RDPE through LAGs must also meet cross compliance requirements.

Vocational training

The RDPE may be able to provide support for vocational training.

The Landskills programme is one example of RDPE-funded vocational training. It is managed by Lantra, the Sector Skills Council for environmental and land-based industries. Landskills includes vocational training at local colleges, as well as a variety of short courses for farmers, food processors, and foresters offered by organisations like the English Beef and Lamb Executive, the Farming and Wildlife Advisory Group and ADAS.

You can find out about RDPE-funded short courses on the National Farmers’ Union (NFU) website.

You can also read more information in the related guide on agricultural skills and training.

You can also contact the Natural England Environmental Stewardship Helpline on Telephone: 0300 060 0011 for more information.

Rural Development Programme for England funding for agri-environment schemes

Agri-environment schemes pay farmers and land managers to farm - or manage woodland - in an environmentally friendly way.

All farmers in England are eligible for Environmental Stewardship and the Energy Crops Scheme, which are administered by Natural England and funded by the RDPE.

Farmers involved in these schemes have to deliver environmental benefits that go beyond cross compliance requirements to keep their land in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAEC). For more information, see the guide on cross compliance: the basics.

RDPE funding for environmentally sustainable woodland management is delivered through the Forestry Commission’s EWGS.

The Environmental Stewardship Scheme

The main aims of Environmental Stewardship are to:

  • conserve wildlife (biodiversity)
  • maintain and enhance landscape quality and character
  • protect the historic environment
  • promote public access and understanding of the countryside
  • protect natural resources

The scheme also aims to help with genetic conservation and flood management where possible. It also helps the natural environment to adapt to climate change - eg by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and providing and protecting carbon storage. Find out more in the guide on the Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

There are three elements to Environmental Stewardship:

  • Entry Level Stewardship (ELS) is open to all farmers and land managers for simple - yet effective - environmental management across the whole farm. The scheme is non-competitive and a five-year agreement is guaranteed provided you agree to deliver enough land management options (selected from more than 50) on your eligible land to meet scheme conditions. Management will go beyond the Single Payment Scheme requirement to maintain land in GAEC. For more information, see the guide on ELS.
  • Organic Entry Level Stewardship (OELS) is the organic strand of ELS - see the guide on OELS.
  • Higher Level Stewardship (HLS) builds on ELS to develop a comprehensive agreement involving a wide range of environmental benefits involving detailed management. HLS is a competitive scheme and applications are assessed against specific national, regional and local targets. Ten-year agreements will be offered where applications meet these targets and represent good value for money. Find more information in the guide on HLS.

Uplands Support/Hill Farm Allowance is integrated into Environmental Stewardship as Uplands ELS. Uplands ELS is open to all farmers and land managers in England’s ‘Severely Disadvantaged Areas’ (SDAs), as long as they meet the rules. For more information, see the guide on hill farming.

The Energy Crops Scheme (ECS)

The ECS pays farmers a contribution of the verifiable costs of planting the energy crops miscanthus and short rotation coppice. The ECS is administered by Natural England. For more information, see the guide on industrial fibre crops.

You can also contact the Natural England Environmental Stewardship Helpline on Telephone: 0300 060 0011 for more information.

Rural Development Programme for England funding for woodland management

The Forestry Commission awards RDPE funding for woodland management through the EWGS.

The overall aims of the EWGS are to sustain and increase the public benefits given by existing woodlands and help create new woodlands to deliver additional public benefit.

EWGS will support projects which:

  • increase the area of woodland under certified sustainable forest management and approved management schemes
  • expand the area of woodland with public access
  • improve the condition of woodland Sites of Special Scientific Interest
  • assist delivery of Habitat Action Plan targets for native woodlands
  • improve the environment of disadvantaged urban communities
  • create new woodland

Before applying for an EWGS grant, you must contact the RPA to register both yourself and your land on the EWGS Customer Register and the Rural Land Register respectively.

There are five EWGS grants for stewardship of existing woods:

  • Woodland Planning Grant - for preparing plans that help with management of the woodland and meet the UK Woodland Assurance Standard
  • Woodland Assessment Grant - for gathering information to improve management decisions
  • Woodland Regeneration Grant - for supporting change in woodland composition through natural regeneration and restocking after felling
  • Woodland Improvement Grant - for work in woodlands to create and sustain public benefits
  • Woodland Management Grant - for helping with the additional costs of providing and sustaining higher-quality public benefits from existing woodlands

EWGS also offers a Woodland Creation Grant. This supports the creation of new woodlands where they deliver the greatest public benefits. It includes annual Farm Woodland Payments to compensate for lost agricultural income.

Find out more in the related guide on trees and woodland.

You can also contact the Natural England Environmental Stewardship Helpline on Telephone: 0300 060 0011 for more information.
