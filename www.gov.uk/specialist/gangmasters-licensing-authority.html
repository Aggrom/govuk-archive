Gangmasters Licensing Authority

Licensing and code of practice for gangmasters supplying workers to the agricultural, horticultural and shellfish-gathering industries.

Introduction

The Gangmasters Licensing Authority (GLA) is the UK agency that regulates the supply of workers to the agricultural, horticultural and shellfish-gathering industries. Its aim is to improve health and safety standards and prevent the exploitation of workers in these industries.

This guide will explain the GLA’s code of practice and how to apply for a licence. It will also explain who is exempt from holding a licence and the penalties that apply if you do not have a licence where one is necessary.

Gangmasters licensing and the code of practice

The GLA regulates labour providers or ‘gangmasters’ who provide temporary workers anywhere in the UK, including on the shore or sea bed, estuary or tidal river, in the following sectors:

  • agriculture
  • forestry
  • horticulture
  • shellfish-gathering
  • food processing and packaging

If you are a gangmaster or an employment agency, you will need a licence if you:

  • supply labour for the licensed sectors in the UK - even if you operate from outside the UK
  • use workers to provide services in the licensed sectors
  • use workers to gather shellfish - down to and below the low-water mark

For more information on your general requirements as an agricultural employer, see employing farm workers: the basics.

Register of licence holders

The GLA keeps a register of licence holders, which is available for viewing online by the public. You can search for specific licence holders on the GLA website.

Code of practice for labour providers

The Temporary Labour Working Group, was responsible for improving conditions for temporary workers and for providing guidance for labour providers before the gangmasters legislation was fully implemented.

It produced a code of practice, which has been adopted by organisations including:

  • the National Farmers’ Union
  • the Fresh Produce Consortium
  • the Association of Labour Providers
  • major supermarket groups

You can download the Temporary Labour Working Group full code of practice for labour providers from the Agricultural Document Library (ADLib) website (PDF, 166KB).

Applying for a labour provider licence

You can apply to the GLA for a new labour provider licence or renew an existing one by calling the GLA Helpline on Tel 0845 602 5020.

You can also apply for a licence online through the Government Gateway website.

Find out how to register for Government Gateway online services on the Government Gateway website.

You can also download guidance notes on how to apply for a GLA licence from the GLA website (PDF, 472KB).

If you are applying for a licence to provide workers from outside the UK, you will have to attend an inspection with a GLA officer in the UK.

Download the GLA Licensing Standards document from the ADLib website (PDF, 705KB).

Renewing a licence

A GLA licence lasts for one year, and you should renew it before it expires. If your licence does expire, you will need to make a new application, and you will have to stop trading until your new licence arrives. You would also have to pay another inspection fee. Operating without a licence is considered an offence.

The GLA will need to confirm that the information you originally gave when applying for your licence is still valid when you come to renew it. You can download a list of questions that you will be asked from the GLA website (PDF, 231KB).

The easiest way to renew your licence is over the telephone. You can do this by calling the GLA Helpline on Tel 0845 602 5020. They will send you their copy of your business details, which you should check. If any of your details are incorrect, you will need to make the necessary changes, re-sign the copy and return it to the address on the form.

Licence fees

Fees are calculated according to the annual turnover of your business in the licensed sectors:

Fees based on your annual turnover

£10 million or more A £2,600 £2,900
From £5 million to less than £10 million B £2,000 £2,400
From £1 million to less than £5 million C £1,200 £2,150

Appealing against Gangmasters Licensing Authority decisions

You have a right to appeal against a decision made by the GLA if:

  • your application for a licence is refused
  • you are granted a licence with additional licensing conditions
  • your licence is taken away

If you believe the GLA has made a factual mistake which has led to them refusing your licence, you should contact the GLA Helpline on Tel 0845 602 5020. For enquiries about other GLA decisions, or for information about how to make an appeal, you should call the GLA Appeals Secretariat on Tel 01270 754 231 or email them at gangmasters.appeals@defra.gsi.gov.uk

Download the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs’ (Defra’s) guidance on appealing against a licensing decision from the ADLib website (PDF, 85KB).

Exclusions from gangmasters licensing

There are certain circumstances under which labour providers do not need a licence from the GLA. This includes if they are:

  • supplied for processing and packaging in food retail outlets, catering outlets, retail distribution centres, fresh produce, the meat or fish wholesale market, wholesaling establishments
  • supplied for processing and packaging in non-food industries, such as cosmetic and engine oils, using products from agricultural work - eg oils from fish and shellfish
  • loaned between farmers for agricultural activities, work on farms with share-farming agreements, or are providing a short-term agricultural service
  • supplied by a farmer to an agricultural contractor to operate machinery supplied by the contractor for use on the farmer’s premises
  • part of an agricultural machinery service supplied by an agricultural contractor
  • a skilled worker, eg a qualified slaughterman to work in an abattoir, or an individual skilled worker such as a shepherd or herdsmen
  • providing labour for a sub-contracted service that has been provided
  • supplied to Seasonal Agricultural Workers Scheme (SAWS) operators by another SAWS operator. SAWS allows students from outside the European Economic Area to enter the UK for seasonal agricultural work
  • supplied by an educational establishment - eg for work experience in agricultural setting
  • employed to harvest a crop for someone who has sold land but kept ownership of the crop
  • gathering shellfish from a fishing vessel

If you are not sure whether you need a licence, you can call the GLA Helpline on Tel 0845 602 5020. They will tell you:

  • if you need a licence
  • what conditions you have to meet to get a licence
  • how to apply for a licence

If you do not have a licence when you should have one, it is no defence to claim that you did not know you needed one.

Inspection, penalties and protection for workers

The gangmasters licensing scheme is based on inspections and enforcement by GLA licensing officers.

Penalties

Under gangmasters licensing legislation, it is a criminal offence to operate without a licence or to have false, or improperly obtained, documents. The maximum penalty is ten years in prison, but you could also be fined.

It is also an offence to make agreements with unlicensed labour providers to employ workers. The maximum penalty is six months and a fine.

If a person is working in the UK illegally, they are still considered to be a ‘worker’ under gangmasters licensing legislation. This means that gangmasters licensing requirements also apply to illegal workers.

Inspections

The GLA carries out inspections of licence holders and applicants for licences. Inspections can take place by prior arrangement or without warning.

For further information about how the licensing scheme is enforced, you can [download the GLA enforcement code of practice from the ADLib website (PDF, 339KB).

Labour users

If you are supplied with labour and your business operates in one or more of the licensing sectors, you must only use GLA licensed suppliers. The GLA licensing scheme affects all labour users, and:

  • makes it a criminal offence to knowingly use workers provided by an unlicensed labour provider
  • has the power to shut down labour providers without a licence, which could impact on your business

The GLA public register lists all licence holders, so you can always check to make sure your labour provider is operating legally. You can search for specific licence holders on the GLA website.

Worker protection

If workers feel they are being unfairly treated or exploited by a gangmaster, they can report their concerns anonymously to the GLA by calling the Crimestoppers on Tel 0800 555 111. Alternatively, you can inform the GLA by completing an online reporting form for worker welfare complaints on the GLA website.

The GLA does not need workers to publicly provide evidence to investigate a suspect employer.
