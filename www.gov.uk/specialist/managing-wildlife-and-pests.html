Managing wildlife and pests

How farmers and land managers can control agricultural pests, and prevent damage to livestock and feedstuffs.

Introduction

Wildlife can cause significant damage to farmers’ livestock and feedstuffs if effective prevention and control measures are not in place. Some species can also increase the risk of disease among livestock, pets and humans.

The Natural England Wildlife Management and Licensing Service (WMLS) provides advice on managing wildlife problems and issues wildlife licences in England. Many pest control activities may not need a licence, but if you are unsure, you should check with Natural England.

Mammal pest control

Farmers and land managers can legally control some mammal species for particular problems, such as damage to crops or risks to health and safety.

You will need a licence from Natural England to manage some mammal species or to use certain control measures - see the page in this guide on licences for pest control.

You do not need a licence for certain control measures involving rats and house mice. For more information, see the page in this guide on rat and mice control.

Mammal control

You can get advice from Natural England about minimising damage to land or crops caused by mammal species, eg:

  • foxes
  • rabbits
  • moles
  • badgers
  • edible dormice
  • non-native mammals

You can find information about different mammal species on the Natural England website.

You can find advice on controlling and reducing damage from specific mammal species on the Natural England website.

Foxes

Rural foxes eat a wide range of food items and will sometimes kill large numbers of easy prey animals - such as captive hens. They are less significant as carriers of disease.

Foxes can pose a risk to:

  • poultry
  • lambs
  • outdoor-reared piglets
  • game

Measures to protect livestock may in the long run prove more cost-effective than culling. If you do plan to cull a fox population, you should avoid the fox dispersal period of October to March - as this makes it likely that the culled animals will be quickly replaced.

Other control methods for deterring foxes include:

  • permanent fences
  • electric fences
  • using llamas to guard stock
  • flashing lights
  • shooting
  • snares - see the page in this guide on pest control: snares

Rabbits

Rabbits are estimated to cause damage to rural areas of about £100 million each year. Farmers and landowners have a responsibility to control wild rabbits on their land or take steps to prevent them causing damage. This is a continuing obligation.

As with other species, co-operation with your neighbours is vital. Where rabbits live on railway land, you should call the Network Rail helpline on 08457 11 41 41.

See the page on managing rabbits, hares and grey squirrels in the guide on wild mammals.

You can also find information on rabbits on the Natural England website.

Moles

Mole management is best carried out between October and April, but you should be aware that moles are protected by legislation preventing cruelty to wild mammals.

There are two main approved control methods for moles:

  • poisoning with aluminium phosphide
  • spring traps

See the page on managing mice, rats, moles and edible dormice in the guide on wild mammals.

You can also find information about moles on the Natural England website.

Badgers

The UK has some of the highest densities of badgers in Europe - over 30 per square kilometre in some areas. Problems caused by badgers include:

  • crop damage
  • land damage
  • poultry predation
  • spread of disease
  • undermining drainage works or flood defences

In July 2010, the Badger Vaccine Deployment Project programme (BVDP) began in the Gloucestershire area to develop the techniques to vaccinate wild badgers and reduce the risk of their spreading bovine tuberculosis to cattle. Find more information in the guide on cattle health. You can also find information on BDVP on the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website.

You can apply to Natural England for a licence to close down a problematic badger sett or, in exceptional circumstances, to kill badgers. Preventative measures include:

  • fencing
  • badger-proofing buildings
  • limiting contact with cattle

Find information on controlling and managing badgers on the Natural England website.

Edible (fat) dormouse

Edible dormice, or fat dormice, can cause damage in a number of ways, including stripping tree bark and damaging electrical cables within buildings. No licence is needed for non-prohibited control measures, such as shooting.

Find information on controlling and managing edible dormice on the Natural England website.

You can also find out more on the page on managing mice, rats, moles and edible dormice in the guide on wild mammals.

Cross compliance and Environmental Stewardship

It is possible to get funding for measures to protect and enhance wild mammal habitats through the Environmental Stewardship schemes run by Natural England. For more information, see the guide on Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

You must comply with cross compliance requirements in order to qualify for full payment under the Single Payment Scheme and other direct payments - such as the Environmental Stewardship scheme. These requirements are split into two types:

  • Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs)
  • requirements to keep your land in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAECs)

SMR 5 is the cross compliance requirement to maintain habitats and species by protecting flora and fauna. Download SMR 5 (habitats and species) from the Rural Payments Agency (RPA) website. (PDF, 669KB)

For more information about cross compliance, see the guides on cross compliance: the basics and Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs).

Bird pest control

All wild birds and their nests and eggs are officially protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. The government does permit hunting for some quarry birds during defined seasons, as well as shooting or trapping to control named species under general licence under certain specific circumstances and subject to meeting the general licence’s eligibility criteria.

You can find information on general licence and bird pest control on the Natural England website.

You can find wild bird management advice in the guide on wild birds.

Cross compliance and Environmental Stewardship

You can get information on conservation measures to encourage wild birds through the Environmental Stewardship schemes run by Natural England. For more information, see the guide on Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

You must comply with cross compliance requirements to qualify for full payment under the Single Payment Scheme and other direct payments, such as the Environmental Stewardship scheme. These requirements are split into two types:

  • Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs)
  • requirements to keep your land in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAEC)

SMR 1 is the cross compliance requirement to protect wild birds, their eggs and nests. Find Rural Payments Agency (RPA) information on SMR 1 (wild birds) on the Agricultural Document Library (ADLib) website.

For information about cross compliance, see the guides on cross compliance: the basics and SMR.

Licences for pest control

The Natural England WMLS is responsible for wildlife licensing activities in England.

Some management activities can be carried out under a general licence. You can find information on general licences on the Natural England website.

You may need to apply for a specific licence to manage some species or to use certain control methods. For common pest species, such as rats and house mice, you may not need a licence at all. Some control methods are illegal, irrespective of the species concerned, so you should always seek legal advice if you are unsure.

General licences

A general licence allows you to carry out certain pest control actions to prevent damage or disease that would normally be illegal without a specific licence.

You can get a general licence to control certain species that:

  • compromise air safety
  • cause crop damage
  • threaten the conservation of other species

You should carefully check the terms of the general licences, as each is very specific as to the species and circumstances covered.

You can find information on general licences on the Natural England website.

Specific licences

Many circumstances will not be covered by a general licence and you may need to apply for a specific licence.

You can find specific licence application forms on the Natural England website.

Bird species control

You can apply for specific licences to control bird species to prevent agricultural damage.

You can find specific licence application forms on the Natural England website.

For more information about controlling birds, see the page in this guide on bird pest control.

Rat and mice control

You are responsible for controlling any rats or house mice that are on your agricultural land. You do not have to notify your local authority.

Rats consume, on average, 25-30 grams of food per day and contaminate their surroundings with droppings, urine and hairs. They also cause structural damage to buildings by gnawing and burrowing.

Diseases and parasites carried by rats that are potentially harmful to humans and animals include:

  • leptospirosis (Weil’s disease)
  • toxoplasmosis
  • salmonella

Contamination of food premises by rats and mice could lead to prosecution under food safety laws. The presence of rats can also threaten assurance scheme accreditation.

Control methods for rats and mice

You can use several methods to control rats and mice. You should always consider preventative measures first, such as proofing, removal of potential food sources and removal of harborage. If necessary control methods can then be carried out alongside preventative measures.

For best results, it is usually best to use a combination. Always ensure that the products you use are legal for the species of rodent and situation involved. You must always follow the statutory instructions on the pesticide label to ensure a safe, effective and lawful treatment. Remember that these products are toxic to humans and pets as well as rats and mice and proper safety precautions must be taken to minimise risks.

If you don’t control rats and mice on your land, your local authority can step in to do the work and recover the costs from you.

Control methods could include:

  • trapping
  • shooting
  • chemical control using fumigants or rodenticide baits

When planning a control programme, you should take into account:

  • the scale of infestation
  • available resources
  • your experience
  • risks to non-target wildlife, pets, livestock and humans

Break-back spring traps can be useful for controlling house mice and rats - especially when only small numbers are present.

Mice control: rodenticides

Rodenticides can be used to control rats and house mice. As with any pesticide it is important to read the label and follow the instructions so that treatments are undertaken safely and effectively. Users must be competent and adequately instructed in all aspects of rodenticide use.

To poison mice, you should use second-generation anti-coagulant compounds - such as bromadiolone or difenacoum. First-generation anti-coagulants, eg warfarin and chlorophacinone, are not suitable as many populations are now resistant.

If consistent with the product instructions, you should place small amounts of bait in many different locations that show signs of use, as mice prefer to feed in this way. Bait points must be protected from non-target species.

Find guidance on controlling house mice on the Natural England website.

Find guidance on house mouse management controls on the Natural England website.

Rat control: rodenticides

As with any pesticide it is important to read the label and follow the instructions so that treatments are undertaken safely and effectively. Users must be competent and adequately instructed in all aspects of rodenticide use. Poisoning options for rats start with first-generation anti-coagulant rodenticides such as:

  • warfarin
  • coumatetralyl
  • chlorophacinone

Where resistance is present among rat populations, you can also use more toxic, second-generation rodenticides, eg:

  • difenacoum
  • bromadiolone
  • flocoumafen (must not be used outdoors)

You should place the bait at suitable points throughout the entire area where rat activity has been noticed. Bait points must be protected from non-target species.

Find information on controlling rats with rodenticides on the Natural England website.

Read advice about controlling rat populations on the Natural England website.

Pest control: snares

Snares are a common capture and control method, often used for foxes and rabbits.

Capture and control methods raise welfare concerns and pose a potential risk to non-target species. You should research whether you need to use snares. Preventative measures can sometimes be a better option.

If you do set snares for animal control, your methods must:

  • be legal
  • be humane
  • avoid non-target species
  • be carried out with sensitivity and respect for other countryside users

Target species for snaring

The most common target species for snaring are:

  • foxes
  • rabbits
  • grey squirrels
  • mink

Snare design

You should use a free-running snare for trapping animals. This is a wire loop that relaxes when the animal stops pulling.

Snares: how to operate

Snares are designed to be used to capture, not kill, animals. You should have considerable experience of placing snares and animal tracks/signs to ensure only target species are likely to be caught. You should always place them where surrounding objects, buildings or vegetation will not cause the animal unnecessary injury after it has been caught.

To improve the effectiveness of snares, you can remove the manufacturers’ lubricant by boiling them before use. You can also coat the wire with paraffin wax to reduce the human scent.

Snares: code of practice

Defra has introduced a code of good practice for snaring.

Download Defra’s code of practice on snaring to control fox and rabbit populations from the ADLib website. (PDF, 47KB)

Spring traps

It is an offence to use spring traps unless they are of a design approved by the Secretary of State.

You can find information on approved traps on the Defra website.

If you need further advice, the Natural England WMLS can also advise on which types of trap can be used. You can call the Natural England WMLS helpline on 0845 601 4523.

If you suspect that an illegal spring trap is being used, you should contact the police. See the next page in this guide on reporting illegal pest control and wildlife crime.

How to report illegal pest control and wildlife crime

If you suspect that illegal activities are taking place, you should contact your local police force, many of which have specialist wildlife crime officers.

Examples of wildlife crimes include:

  • setting illegal spring traps (eg leghold traps)
  • poisoning protected species
  • cruelty to wildlife
  • illegally trading in protected species

Suspected pesticide poisoning of wildlife

Pesticides should always be used responsibly. The Campaign against Accidental or Illegal Poisoning (CAIP) aims to raise awareness of the issue of poisoning of wildlife and companion animals.

The Chemicals Regulation Directorate is part of the Health & Safety Executive (HSE). They are responsible for the regulation of pesticides and administering the Wildlife Incident Investigation Scheme (WIIS).

You can find information on WIIS on the HSE website.

Read advice for farmers about the illegal poisoning of wildlife on the CAIP website.

Download CAIP’s best practice guide on controlling pests in the countryside from the ADLib website. (PDF, 2.60MB)

To report a suspected poisoning incident, you can call the WIIS helpline on 0800 321 600.

You can also get advice about wildlife crime and reporting from the Partnership for Action Against Wildlife Crime (PAW). Find out about reporting wildlife crime on the PAW website.

To report a suspected poisoning incident of wildlife or companion animals, you should call the WIIS helpline on 0800 321 600.

Organisations that can help

Below are details of organisations that can offer further advice on pest control.

Natural England is responsible for conserving and enhancing the natural environment across England. Its WMLS provides advice and issues licences for activities affecting wildlife species.

You can find information on the WMLS on the Natural England website.

You can also call the Natural England WMLS helpline on 0845 601 4523.

One of the major roles of the Defra is to help the farming industry operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies that provide around £3 billion to UK agriculture. They also oversee a number of agencies that work with arable farmers, imports and exports of crops, and implement pest and disease controls. You can call the Defra helpline on 08459 33 55 77.

The RPA is responsible for licences and schemes for growers as well as for running the Single Payment Scheme (SPS). For more information about the SPS and how it can help your farming business, you can call the RPA helpline on 0845 603 7777.

You can also read the guide on the SPS.

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about cross compliance. For further information call the Cross Compliance helpline on 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, find information on cross compliance requirements on the Cross Compliance website.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities.

You can read about the work of the NFU on their website.

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities over a number of farming, land use, food standards and environmental regulations. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources. Find contact details for your local authority through the Contacts Directory.
