Get a passport for your child, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: When you can apply

Children under 16 years old

You can usually apply for a passport for your child if:

  • they’re under 16
  • you legally have ‘parental responsibility’ for them

You automatically have parental responsibility if you:

  • are the child’s mother
  • are the child’s father and are married to the child’s mother
  • were married to the child’s mother when the child was born (or, in Scotland, when the mother became pregnant)
  • have adopted the child (alone, or jointly)

If your situation is more complex, you can get more information about parental responsibility.

Cost

It costs £46.00 to get a child passport by post, or £54.75 using the Post Office Check & Send service.

Children nearly or over 16 years old

If your child will be 16 within the next few weeks or is over 16, they must apply for a 10-year adult passport. They can apply without getting parental consent.

Situation Action
Your child had a British child passport Follow the process for renewing an adult passport
Your child has never had a British child passport or has been included on your passport Apply for their first adult passport

Fostering, surrogacy and assisted reproduction

There are special rules if:

  • you’ve adopted a child
  • you’re fostering a child
  • the child was born through assisted reproduction or surrogacy

Part 2: Your child's first passport

To get a child passport, your child must be under 16 and one of the following:

  • a British citizen
  • a British overseas territories citizen
  • a British overseas citizen

You can get more information on eligibility for a British passport.

If your child is eligible, get an application form.

It takes at least 6 weeks to get your child’s first passport - you’ll need to use a different service if you need the passport more quickly.

Documents you must send

You must send supporting documents with your application form that prove:

  • your child’s identity
  • they’re eligible for a British passport

You must send original documents - photocopies won’t be accepted.

The documents you must send depend on your child’s circumstances - check the guidance notes for the passport application form.

Download ‘Guidance notes - Applying for a passport’ (PDF, 495KB)

How your documents will be sent back

Your documents will be sent back through the normal post.

You can pay an extra £3 to get your supporting documents sent back to you via secure delivery when you application has been processed.

Choose this service on your application form if you want to use it.

Part 3: Renewing or replacing your child's passport

If your child is under 16, you can renew or replace a damaged passport up to 9 months before the current one runs out.

Up to 9 months of the time left on the passport will be added to the new one.

You must use the standard application form to renew or replace your child’s passport.

It will take at least 3 weeks to get the passport - you’ll need to use a different service if you need the passport more quickly.

Part 4: The application form

You can:

  • fill in the form online - the Identity and Passport Service (IPS) will post it back to you to check and sign
  • get a form from a Post Office that offers the passport Check & Send service
  • call the Passport Adviceline and get them to post you a form

What you’ll need

Signatures

The application form must be signed by:

  • an adult with parental responsibility for the child
  • someone who can confirm the adult has parental responsibility for the child (called a ‘countersignature’)
  • your child (If they’re aged between 12 and 15)

If your child can’t sign the form, you must include a covering letter with the application that explains why.

Check who can sign to prove you have parental responsibility for the child and what they have to do.

Photos

Follow the rules about passport photos or your application may be rejected.

The person who signs the application (‘countersigns’) must also sign the back of 1 photo, confirming it’s a good likeness of the child.

Check who can countersign photos and what they have to do.

Send the application form

You can:

  • post your application (include your child’s existing passport, if they have one) to the IPS using the pre-addressed envelope that comes with the form
  • use the Post Office Check & Send service

What happens next

The IPS will check your application.

Contact the Passport Adviceline to track your application.

The new passport will be sent to your home address. You must sign for it.

Part 5: Adopted children

You send specific documents if you’re applying for a passport for a child you’ve adopted.

You must send original documents - photocopies or certified documents will be rejected.

If your child was adopted in the UK

You child can get a British passport if either adoptive parent is British and was usually living in the UK when the child was adopted.

You must send your child’s adoption certificate showing the British parent’s details.

You must also send 1 of the following:

  • section 4 of the application form with the British passport details for either parent filled in
  • UK birth certificate for either parent
  • Home Office certificate of registration or naturalisation for either parent
  • the passport that was valid at the time of the child’s birth for either parent

If your child was adopted overseas

Adopted before 1 June 2003

Your child doesn’t automatically qualify for a British passport - even if one of the parents is a British citizen.

You may have parental responsibility for the child, but they may not have British nationality. Call the Passport Adviceline for help before you apply.

Adopted on or after 1 June 2003

Your child can get a British passport if either parent is British and the parent was usually living in the UK when the child was adopted.

You must send the child’s full Hague Convention adoption certificate showing parent’s details.

You must also send 1 of the following:

  • section 4 of the application form with the British passport details for either parent filled in
  • UK birth certificate for either parent
  • Home Office certificate of registration or naturalisation for either parent
  • the passport that was valid at the time of the child’s birth for either parent

Part 7: Children born through assisted reproduction or surrogacy

There are special rules about your child’s nationality if they were born through assisted reproduction or surrogacy.

Your child can take the nationality of either parent if both the following apply:

  • your child was born using assisted reproduction treatment
  • you’ve got a parental order granted in the UK after 6 April 2010

Proof you’ll need to give

You must include with your application both:

  • the child’s full birth certificate showing both parents’ details
  • the parental order

You must also send 1 of the following:

  • British passport details for either parent (in section 4 of the application form)
  • either parents’ UK birth certificate
  • Home Office certificate of registration or naturalisation for either parent
  • a passport for one of the parents on the parental order that was valid when the child was born

If you need help or your circumstances are more complicated, call the Passport Adviceline.
