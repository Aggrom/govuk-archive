Record and report workplace accidents and injuries, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Your business' responsibilities

You must keep a record of:

  • injuries that mean employees can’t do their normal work for more than 3 days in a row
  • injuries to people who don’t work for you if they need hospital treatment and the accident is related to your business
  • specific dangerous occurrences (sometimes called ‘near misses’), whether someone is hurt or not
  • specific types of illness or disease (‘occupational diseases’) that someone gets because of their work for you

How to keep records

You must record:

  • the date, time and place that the incident happened or the illness or disease was confirmed by a doctor
  • the date it was reported and how (eg by phone, employee told their manager)
  • personal details of the people involved (eg name, job title, contact details)
  • a description of what happened and what its effects were

If you’ve got more than 10 employees or run a factory, mine or quarry, you must record accidents and incidents in a formal accident book.

If you’ve got 10 or fewer employees, you can keep your records however best suits you, eg on a computer, in a notebook or in an accident book.

Reporting accidents, incidents and illness

You must report:

  • if someone can’t do their normal work for more than 7 days in a row (3 days in Northern Ireland) because of an accident or incident in your workplace
  • major injuries related to your business (including injuries to people who don’t work for you but need hospital treatment)
  • dangerous occurrences/near misses
  • occupational illnesses and diseases

Reporting deadline

You must send the report within:

  • 15 days of the incident happening (England, Wales and Scotland)
  • 10 days of the incident happening (Northern Ireland)

In England, Wales and Scotland, you report to the Health and Safety Executive (HSE). In Northern Ireland, you report to the Health and Safety Executive Northern Ireland.

You can report accidents or incidents online.

Part 2: Injuries

Keeping a record of injuries

You must keep a record of injuries if they’re related to your business.

For example, if a customer or employee falls and bruises their knee on your premises, you must record it.

But if an employee has a car accident driving home from work, you don’t need to record this.

Reporting injuries

You must always report:

  • injuries that mean an employee can’t do their normal work for 7 calendar days in a row (3 days in Northern Ireland)
  • injuries to people who don’t work for you if they need hospital treatment for the injury
  • fractured bones (apart from fingers, hands and toes)
  • amputations
  • dislocated shoulders, hips, knees or spines
  • loss of sight (temporary or permanent)
  • chemical or hot metal burns to eyes
  • something going into someone’s eye
  • electric shock or electrical burn
  • hypothermia or illness brought on by heat if the person becomes unconscious, needs resuscitation or hospital treatment
  • unconsciousness caused by suffocation or exposure to a chemical

Part 3: Dangerous occurrences

A ‘dangerous occurrence’ is a specific type of incident that could cause an injury. They’re sometimes called a ‘near-miss’.

You must always:

  • keep a record of them
  • report them, whether someone’s been hurt or not

Road and transport incidents

You must record and report:

  • a road tanker carrying a dangerous substance overturning, being seriously damaged, catching fire or the substance being released
  • a dangerous substance being conveyed by road is released or involved in a fire
  • any collision with a train

Electrical incidents

You must record and report:

  • equipment touching overhead power lines
  • electrical short circuits or overloads that cause a fire or explosion

Equipment failures

You must record and report:

  • collapse, overturning or failure of load-bearing parts of lifts, lifting machinery or freight containers
  • failure of any passenger restraint or load-bearing part of fairground equipment, or derailment or unintended collision of fairground cars or trains
  • breakdown in breathing apparatus, either in use or during testing before use

Building, construction and demolition problems

You must record and report:

  • collapse of a wall or floor in a workplace
  • unintended collapse of anything being built, altered or demolished if more than 5 tonnes of material fall
  • collapse or partial collapse of a scaffold over 5 metres high
  • scaffolding put up near water where there could be a risk of drowning after a fall
  • collapse of a temporary supporting structure where something is under construction (‘false-work’)
  • unintended explosions, misfires or failed demolition attempts
  • debris landing outside a demolition site

Explosions and fire hazards

You must record and report:

  • explosions or fire causing suspension of normal work for more than 24 hours
  • sudden uncontrolled release in a building of 100 kg or more of a flammable liquid, 10 kg or more of a flammable liquid above its boiling point or 10 kg or more of a flammable gas
  • sudden uncontrolled release of more than 500kg of flammable liquid in the open air
  • sealed containers or their pipework exploding, collapsing or bursting

Chemicals, radiation and biological agents

You must record and report:

  • the accidental release of any substance which may damage health, including biological agents or chemicals
  • if industrial radiography or irradiation equipment doesn’t de-energise or return to its safe position after the exposure period

Part 4: Occupational diseases

An ‘occupational disease’ is an illness that someone gets as a result of working for you, including:

  • repetitive strain injury caused by using a computer keyboard for work
  • dermatitis if it’s caused by contact with something as part of work (eg chemicals, acids, solvents, cement, plaster, fish or meat)
  • hand-arm vibration syndrome caused by using tools like saws, drills or hammers
  • hepatitis if someone works with blood or blood products
  • Lyme disease if someone’s working outdoors and exposed to ticks
  • lung cancer or Mesothelioma if someone has been exposed to asbestos because of their job

Check if you must record and report an illness

If a member of staff tells you that they’ve got an illness or disease because of their work, you must find out both:

  • the condition they’ve got
  • the reason they’ve got it

Check the listing of recordable and reportable occupational diseases to see what you need to report.
