Arms embargo on Zimbabwe

How to apply for an export licence for arms, ammunition, weapons and related goods going to Zimbabwe.

Introduction

There is an arms embargo in force in Zimbabwe, imposed by the United Nations (UN) and implemented in UK law.

You must obtain an export licence to export any controlled goods from the UK to Zimbabwe. These are issued by the Export Control Organisation (ECO).

Controlled strategic goods include military goods, products used for torture and repression, radioactive sources and Dual-Use Goods.

This guide will explain the extent of the arms embargo, how it is currently in operation, and the UK and international laws that are enforcing it. You can also find out how to apply for an export licence to Zimbabwe.

This guide relates specifically to the arms embargo. If you are considering exporting to Zimbabwe, you should be aware that financial asset restrictions and a travel ban are also in place.

Extent of the arms embargo on Zimbabwe

An arms embargo is a ban on the export of ‘arms and related material’ (ie military ammunition, weapons and goods). This can be put in place by either the UN, the EU, the Organisation on Security and Co-operation in Europe or at a UK national level.

The UK interprets an arms embargo as covering all goods and items on the UK Military List (which forms part of the UK Strategic Control Lists), unless stated otherwise. For more information see the guide on the UK Strategic Export Control Lists - the consolidated list of strategic military and dual-use items.

Additionally, some goods which are not on the UK Military List might also need an export licence. For more information, see the guide on Military End-Use control guidance notes.

Zimbabwe is also subject to Trade Controls under Schedule 4 Part 2 of the Export Control Order 2008. This means that the destination is both embargoed and subject to transit control for military goods. For more information, see the guides on Transport Controls and Trafficking and Brokering (Trade Controls).

Export control updates

Updates to the current sanctions and embargoes situation are also issued by the Export Control Organisation (ECO).

Find out about current Notices to Exporters on the Department of Business, Innovation & Skills (BIS) website.

To receive regular updates on the latest changes you can join the ECO mailing list, find contact details for the ECO on the BIS website.

Applying for an export licence to Zimbabwe

You can apply for an export licence for your goods. All applications will be considered by the Government on a case-by-case basis in line with the provisions of the Consolidated EU and National Arms Export Licensing Criteria. Read the guide on assessment of export licence applications - criteria and policy.

For details on different export licences available see the guide on licences - export, trade control and transhipment.

All licence applications should be made via the SPIRE website.

You can apply for an export licence on SPIRE with BIS.

When applying for a licence, you should be aware of the current licence processing times by destination. You can view details of licensing statistics on the BIS searchable database website.

In applying and using any licence, exporters should be aware of their responsibilities. For more guidance, see the guide on compliance and enforcement of export controls.

If you are unsure if your goods are controlled, you should read the guide about how to determine if your goods need an export licence.

Zimbabwe arms embargo key legislation

There is a wide range of EU and UK legislation regarding the arms embargo on Zimbabwe.

UN Security Council Resolutions

There are currently no UN Security Council Resolutions on Zimbabwe.

EU legislation

The EU first imposed an arms embargo on Zimbabwe in March 1994. This original legislation has subsequently been amended and strengthened by the imposition of Council Common Position 2004/31/CFSP and Council Regulation (EC) No 131/2004. These regulations have subsequently both been amended further by Council Common Position 2005/411/CFSP and Council Regulation (EC) No 838/2005 in line with the UN Resolutions.

The main EU legislation regarding Zimbabwe is Council Regulation (EC) No 310/2002. It has been in force since 18 February 2002.

The Regulation provides for an arms embargo and ban on export of equipment for internal repression, as well as a number of other sanctions. The EU has since renewed, modified and extended the scope of the Regulations to apply the following until 20 February 2010:

  • embargo on arms and related material
  • ban on exports of equipment for internal repression
  • ban on provision of certain services related to the arms embargo, and embargo on equipment which might be used for internal repression
  • travel ban, and freezing of funds and economic resources in respect of listed targeted individuals

You can find the latest updates to EU legislation on the European Commission website.

UK legislation

The relevant UK laws that apply to the arms embargo on Zimbabwe are the:

  • Export Control Order 2008 (SI 2008/3231), under Schedule 4, Part 2 - ‘Embargoed and Subject to Transit Control for Military Goods’.
  • Overseas Territories (Zimbabwe) (Restrictive Measures) (Amendment) Order 2004
  • Zimbabwe (Sale, Supply, Export, Technical Assistance, Financing and Financial Assistance and Shipment of Equipment) (Penalties and Licences) Regulations 2004

For more information, see the guide on the Export Control Order 2008.

Electronic updates on sanctions and embargoes

You can keep up to date with the latest changes to sanctions and embargoes by joining the ECO mailing list. Find contact details for the ECO on the BIS website.

Other restrictions on Zimbabwe

Zimbabwe is also subject to financial, travel and other sanctions.

Financial sanctions

There are a number of notifications issued in respect of the financial measures taken against Zimbabwe. They are frequently updated and supplement the main legislation provided by the EU and UK government.

You can find information on the financial sanctions on Zimbabwe on the HM Treasury website.

Other sanctions

The government will not grant export licences for dual-use equipment where there is a clear risk that the equipment would be for the suppression of:

  • human rights
  • freedom of expression
  • good governance

These sanctions are not lifted until progress has been made on democracy, human rights and the rule of law.

Find information on all the sanctions covering Zimbabwe on the Foreign & Commonwealth Office (FCO) website.
