Notes

Part 1: Rent Assessment Committee: disputes about rent

You can appeal to a Rent Assessment Committee if you’re a private tenant and think your rent is too high.

The committee is independent, and can change the rent if it’s unfair.

You can usually appeal when you get a letter from your landlord about a rent increase.

The committee can raise, lower or agree with the amount of rent you’re challenging.

Time limits for appealing to the committee

There are deadlines for appealing to the committee, depending on the type of tenancy:

  • assured or assured shorthold tenancy: before the new rent is due to start
  • regulated tenancy: within 28 days of the rent being decided by the rent officer

If you’re not sure what type of tenancy you have, check your tenancy agreement, or use Shelter’s tenancy checker.

How the committee makes its decision

The committee may ask for:

  • more information - eg what you think the rent should be and why
  • permission to inspect the property

You may have to go to a hearing, with your landlord, to tell the committee why you think the rent is too high.

If you and your landlord agree, the committee can decide on the case without a hearing, using written evidence.

The committee’s decision

The committee will send its decision in writing, saying:

  • what the rent should be
  • the date the rent is payable from
  • the reasons for their decision

If you disagree with the committee’s decision

The only way to challenge the committee’s decision is through the High Court.

If you wish to do this, you may want to get legal advice.

You have to appeal to the High Court within 28 days of getting the full reasons for the committee’s decision.

Part 2: Leasehold Valuation Tribunal: changes to your lease

If you’re a leaseholder, you can appeal to the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal if you disagree with changes to your lease, including:

  • service charges
  • extending a lease
  • buying the freehold
  • insuring the building

The tribunal is independent, and can decide if a charge is too high or unreasonable, or if changes to the lease are fair.

Appeal to the tribunal

To apply to the tribunal, fill in an application form.

The forms are listed under the heading ‘leasehold disputes’.

Send the form to your local rent assessment panel.

Cost of appealing to the tribunal

You may have to pay a fee if:

  • you’re appealing against a service or administration charge
  • the case goes to a hearing

£500 to £1,000 £70
£1,000 to £5,000 £100
£5,000 to £15,000 £200
£15,000 or more £350

You can pay by postal order or cheque (payable to ‘HM Courts & Tribunals Service’).

What happens after you send your appeal

The tribunal may ask for:

  • more information - eg a copy of the lease
  • permission to inspect the property

You may have to go to a hearing with the freeholder.

If there isn’t a hearing, the tribunal will ask you to send written evidence.

The tribunal will send you a letter giving its decision (usually within 6 weeks of any hearing).

If you disagree with the tribunal’s decision

You may be able to appeal against the decision to a higher tribunal, called the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber).

To do this, you have to ask the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal for permission to appeal, using the form below:

Part 3: Residential Property Tribunal: council homes and park homes

The Residential Property Tribunal can help solve disagreements about:

  • council homes
  • park homes (static caravans or mobile homes)

Council homes

You can appeal to the tribunal if you disagree with a decision about your council home, eg to:

  • reuse an empty building
  • improve parts of a building
  • demolish or close a building
  • make a property safe
  • restrict how many people live in a property

You can also appeal if you’re refused the ‘Right to Buy’ your council or housing association home because it’s suitable for elderly people.

Park homes

You can appeal to the tribunal if you disagree with a decision about your park home - eg to:

  • change or end a residence agreement
  • change the pitch fee
  • move a park home
  • repair a park home
  • transfer a park home to someone else

Appealing to the tribunal

To make an appeal to the tribunal, you have to fill in an application form.

The forms are listed under the headings:

  • ‘Housing Act appeals and applications’
  • ‘Right to Buy appeals’
  • ‘Park homes’

Send the form to your local rent assessment panel.

Cost of appealing to the tribunal

You may have to pay a £150 fee for some cases. The application form will tell you if you have to pay a fee.

You can pay by postal order or cheque (made payable to ‘HM Courts & Tribunals Service’).

Help paying costs

If you’re on benefits, like Income Support, you can ask for help with your fees, using the form below:

What happens after you send your appeal

The tribunal may ask you for permission to inspect the property or park home.

You may have to go to a hearing with the council or site owner.

If there isn’t a hearing, the tribunal will ask you to send written evidence.

The tribunal will send you a letter giving its decision, usually shortly after any hearing.

If you disagree with the tribunal’s decision

You may be able to appeal against the decision to a higher tribunal, called the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber).

To do this, you have to ask the Residential Property Tribunal for permission to appeal, using the form below:
