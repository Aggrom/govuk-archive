Run a local bus service, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

A local bus service uses public service vehicles (PSVs) to carry passengers who pay separate fares over short distances.

The route can be any length, as long as passengers can get off within 15 miles (measured in a straight line) of where they got on.

Example An express service runs from Bristol to Chepstow. You may think it doesn’t need to be registered as your passenger will travel more than 15 miles between where they get on and off. However, it does need to be registered because it’s less than 15 miles in a straight line.

With a few exceptions local services operating outside London must be registered with the local traffic commissioner.

To run a service in London you need a London Service Permit.

Who can register

You can register a local bus service if you:

  • hold a valid PSV operator’s licence
  • hold a community bus permit
  • are a local education authority and want to provide a local service using a school bus belonging to you

Taxi or private hire vehicle owners can also register by getting a special PSV operator’s licence.

Before you register

Before registering a local bus service you should consider if:

  • your route is suitable
  • you have the right sort of vehicles
  • you can keep to the timetable given the traffic conditions on route
  • you have enough drivers to cover absences though sicknesses, holidays, etc
  • you have replacement vehicles if other vehicles are off-road
  • there are any traffic regulation conditions – contact your local traffic area office
  • a Quality Partnership or Quality Contract Scheme exists - contact your local transport authority

You’ll have to run the service just as you have registered it, even if staff members are ill or a vehicle is off the road. There are penalties if you don’t.

Part 2: How to register

You must give the traffic commissioner at least 56 days notice of your intention to start a bus service. This notice period begins on the day when the traffic commissioner accepts your application.

Holders of a ‘Section 22’ community bus permit must give at least 28 days notice.

If you want to start the service sooner you must complete a supplementary form. The traffic commissioner decides whether or not to agree to this.

There are additional requirements for planned services in Scotland.

Part 3: Changing or cancelling a bus service

Apply to the traffic commissioner if you want to:

  • change a timetable, route or other detail of your service
  • cancel the service

You should give the traffic commissioner at least 56 days notice. Holders of a section 22 community bus permit must give at least 28 days notice.

If you want to make the changes or cancel the service sooner, you must apply in writing to the traffic commissioner by completing a supplementary form. The traffic commissioner decides whether or not to agree to this.

Fees

There’s no fee for a cancellation.

Part 4: Exemptions

You don’t have to register a bus service if all of the following apply:

  • someone other than you or your agent is responsible for arranging the journey and bringing the passengers together
  • the journey is not advertised in advance to the general public
  • all passengers travel together to or from the same place - eg a school or factory
  • passengers pay the same fare no matter how far they travel

School or college bus services

If you operate a bus service provided by a local education authority in England or Wales, you may not have to register it.

You don’t need to register a service if the only passengers who pay fares are either:

  • studying at a school or college
  • supervising pupils or students
  • teachers or assistants working at the school or college

If other people can also use the service, it must be registered.

Other exemptions

You don’t need to register:

  • a replacement bus service (eg when a train service is temporarily cancelled and a bus is used instead) provided under an agreement with the Secretary of State, the Scottish Ministers or the National Assembly for Wales
  • excursions or tours - unless they operate once a week or more for at least 6 weeks in a row

An excursion or tour is where passengers travel together on a journey, with or without breaks, from one or more places to one or more places and back.

Part 5: Traffic regulation conditions

The local council can ask the traffic commissioner to impose conditions on your licence. The traffic commissioner will decide if conditions are needed to:

  • prevent dangers to other road users
  • reduce traffic congestion
  • limit environmental pollution

The conditions could affect:

  • your bus routes
  • where you can stop
  • when you can stop and for how long
  • where you can turn or reverse
  • the number of vehicles, their type or their frequency

In some cases the conditions will start straight away.

You’ll be told if conditions have been imposed and they’ll be added to your licence.

You can’t meet the conditions

You must change the registration within 28 days if you can’t run your service under the conditions. You won’t have to pay a fee if you change the registration because of these conditions. You must meet the conditions until the change of registration takes effect.

You disagree with the conditions

If you disagree with the traffic commissioner’s decision you can ask for a public inquiry within 28 days.

It’s against the law to disobey traffic regulation conditions.

Part 6: Penalties for poor service

Once you have registered your service you must run it:

  • at the times you’ve said it would run
  • along the route you’ve registered

Penalties

If you don’t run a reliable or punctual service the traffic commissioner can stop you from running the service - or even running any services at all.

The traffic commissioner can also fine you. The size of the fine is based on the number of vehicles you’re licensed to use.

If you provide the local bus service in England and Wales, you may also have to:

  • spend money on providing or improving local services or facilities
  • compensate passengers

If you disagree with the traffic commissioner’s decision you can appeal to the Upper Tribunal.

Part 7: Register a bus service in London

To run a bus service in London you must have a London Service Permit. Permits last for 5 years and you must normally apply at least 3 months before starting the service.

You can apply for a shorter period of notice if Transport for London (TfL) agrees.

Download ‘London service permit guidance’ (PDF 33KB)

Part 8: Concessionary fares

You may be eligible to take part in a concessionary fare scheme. This reimburses you for carrying passengers who get discounted travel, such as:

  • older people
  • disabled people
  • children

Contact your local council for more information on taking part.

Some councils offer a voluntary membership scheme for operators. Others have a compulsory scheme.

Part 9: Bus Service Operator's Grant

Your local bus service might qualify for a grant if it provides a service to the general public where:

  • at least half the seats are available to and regularly used by them
  • bus stops are in places where people are likely to use them reasonably often
  • if there aren’t fixed stopping places, the arrangements for letting passengers on and off mean people are likely to use them reasonably often
  • single journey fares are reasonably priced
  • they can pay fares conveniently
  • the bus doesn’t have any sign or other indication that it’s not available to them
  • information about the service, its route and timetable is accessible to them
  • if it’s a flexible service, where passengers book in advance, these arrangements mustn’t deter people who want to make a single journey

If your bus service is intended for transporting pupils or the disabled or elderly there are slightly different conditions.
