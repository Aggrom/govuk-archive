Mental health tribunal, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can apply to the mental health tribunal if you disagree with a decision to ‘section’ or detain someone in a psychiatric hospital.

The tribunal can release (‘discharge’) the patient or recommend they are:

  • transferred to a local hospital
  • considered for treatment in the community

The patient is sometimes allowed some time out of the hospital, to see if they’re ready for life in the community.

The tribunal deals with cases in England and Wales. Scotland has its own tribunal.

Other applications

You can also apply to the tribunal if you disagree with:

  • a community treatment order
  • the conditions placed on a discharge from the hospital

When you can apply

You can apply at different times, depending on how the patient was detained.

The rules are complicated, but generally the time-limits are:

Type of detention Period of detention When you can appeal
Admitted for assessment Up to 28 days Within first 14 days
Admitted for treatment Up to 6 months, but can be renewed Once every 6 months
Ordered by court Up to 6 months, but can be renewed After 6 months, then once every 6 months

The tribunal will automatically look at the cases of patients that have not been reviewed for some time. These are called ‘referrals’.

Help and advice

Patients are entitled to legal aid and can get free legal representation.

If you’re a relative of the patient, you can represent yourself or get legal help.

You can find a mental health solicitor on the Law Society website.

You can also get advice from:

  • Carers Direct
  • Mind
  • Rethink

Part 3: Hearings and decisions

The tribunal will set a date for a hearing when it receives the appeal form:

Type of detention When tribunal will organise hearing
Admitted for assessment Within 7 days of getting application form
Admitted for treatment Within 8 weeks of getting application form
Ordered by court/transferred from prison Within 16 weeks of getting application form

Before the hearing

The tribunal’s doctor will examine the patient before the hearing, and the tribunal will send you copies of any reports about the patient.

Your legal representative may ask for an independent psychiatric report.

Who will be at the hearing

Hearings are usually held in the hospital and attended by:

  • 3 tribunal members (including the tribunal doctor)
  • the patient
  • their hospital doctor, ward nurse and social worker

You can bring a legal representative and a relative.

Your representative may also arrange for witnesses or experts to attend, to speak for the patient.

What happens at the hearing

The doctor, nurse and social worker usually give evidence first.

The patient can also give evidence, if they wish.

You, or your legal representative, will have the chance to ask questions.

The tribunal normally makes a decision at the end of the hearing on whether the patient should be discharged.

The tribunal sometimes has to move the hearing to another date if it needs more information or reports.

Claiming expenses

You may be able to claim expenses for going to the hearing. This includes money for:

  • travel costs
  • loss of earnings
  • food and drink, if you’re away for more than 5 hours

Read the guidance on claiming expenses.

Part 4: If you lose the case

If you lose your case, you can ask the tribunal:

  • to look at the decision again
  • for permission to appeal to a higher tribunal

You must appeal within 28 days of getting the written decision.

Ask the tribunal to look at the decision again

You can ask the tribunal to look at the decision again if there was a problem with the way the case was handled, eg:

  • you didn’t get important documents about the case
  • you or your representative weren’t at the hearing

If there was a problem, the tribunal may be able to restart the case.

Fill in the application to set aside a decision, and return it to the mental health tribunal.

Appeal to the upper tribunal

You can ask for permission to appeal to the upper tribunal if you think there was a legal mistake.

To do this, fill in the application for permission to appeal, and return it to the mental health tribunal.

Another judge will look to see if there’s a legal problem with your case. If there is, the case may have to be heard again.

Find out more about appealing to the Upper Tribunal (Administrative Appeals Chamber).
