News Increasing protection for homeowners

Organisations:
  • Department
    for Communities and
    Local Government
Published:
15 November 2012
Policies:
Increasing the number of available homes and Providing housing support for older and vulnerable people
Minister:
The Rt Hon Eric Pickles MP

Eric Pickles announces new safeguards to protect homeowners from having their homes taken by the council if they're left empty for while.

Communities Secretary Eric Pickles today announced that homeowners will be protected from having their homes taken against their wishes by councils.

In order to protect civil liberties, Mr Pickles has introduced powerful safeguards to restrict the use of Empty Dwelling Management Orders. Legislation came into force today that means people who leave their house empty for more than 6 months no longer risk having it seized by their local authority.

In 2006, the previous government introduced Empty Dwelling Management Orders. They allowed councils to take over properties that had been empty for as few as 6 months even if they weren’t boarded up or where the occupant had died. Furniture and possessions were often taken and councils could even seize the property when it was for sale if they thought the asking price was unrealistic.

These powers will now be limited to empty properties that have become magnets for vandalism, squatters and other forms of anti-social behaviour - blighting the local neighbourhood. A property will have to stand empty for at least 2 years before an Empty Dwelling Management Order can be obtained, and property owners will have to be given at least 3 months’ notice before the order can be issued.

Councils have previously used the orders to instigate action against homeowners that is inappropriate, including against people in vulnerable situations. Councils have, for example:

  • attempted to use the powers against a homeowner while she was caring for her injured daughter in France
  • taken action against the house of a 96-year old howeowner as soon as he passed away in a nursing home
  • taken action against a divorcee, who faced action because he only lived in the property at weekends to visit his estranged children

These new safeguards will ensure that responsible homeowners do not face having their properties seized. Mr Pickles said it is wrong that a bereaved family could face having their loved one’s home seized for a period if there is a delay in them deciding what to do with it.

Eric Pickles said:

“An Englishman’s home is his castle and I’m proud to be able to help homeowners protect theirs from councils who are too draconian and heavy-handed in using their powers.

“The government is standing up for the civil liberties and property rights of hard-working people. Those suffering the loss of a loved one should not have to endure the added indignity of having their home seized because of a delay in them deciding what to do with it.

“That’s why I’ve introduced these safeguards that mean the rights of responsible homeowners will be protected, while allowing action to be taken against genuine derelict buildings which blight neighbourhoods.”

The move comes on top of government plans to bring back into use many of the 300,000 properties in England that have been empty for a long period of time.

Councils now have a new incentive to bring empty properties back into use through the New Homes Bonus, under which the government will match the Council Tax raised from any Council Tax collected from a property that previously stood empty.
