Providing security for and training the Afghan National Security Force

The mission in Afghanistan is, and remains, the main effort of the Ministry of Defence (MOD).

The MOD’s purpose in Afghanistan is to combat insurgents, train and build the Afghan National Security Forces (ANSF) to a level where they will be able to effectively manage their own security after the completion of security transition at the end of 2014.

However, the close partnership that has evolved between the participating multinational forces and the Afghans will continue well beyond 2014.

UK forces operate in Afghanistan as part of the 50 nation military task force, the International Security Assistance Force (ISAF). NATO commissioned ISAF in 2001 following a rolling UN Security Council mandate (which must be renewed each year) to create a secure environment for the reconstruction of Afghanistan. The UK contributes 9,000 conventional troops to the ISAF mission, carrying out operations mostly in Task Force Helmand.

As transition progresses, the campaign is shifting from an ISAF-led counter insurgency mission to an Afghan one.

This means that for ISAF the mission is gradually evolving from one concentrated primarily on combat to one based on the concepts of train, advise and assist.

The Security Force Assistance model is the mechanism that oversees this process.

Implemented in 2012, it will be fully operational by mid-2013, when we expect the final Afghan districts to enter the transition process.

This will mark a point of huge significance where the Afghans will be in the security lead across the country.

The Security Force Assistance model will take place over 4 stages. Each level of the Security Force Assistance model has a progressively lighter relationship with the ANSF, will be generally smaller, and will enable greater flexibility, allowing troop-contributing nations to gradually draw down their force levels.

The ANSF is now running most of its own training, planning its own operations and increasingly taking responsibility for specialist tasks like counter-IED and route clearance. Around three-quarters of the Afghan population now live in areas of transition where the ANSF are now taking the lead in providing security.

The security situation in Afghanistan is steadily improving but there are strong regional variations and we must continue to expect challenges ahead. While violence levels in Afghanistan in 2012 are broadly commensurate with 2011 figures, we continue to see evidence that insurgent activity is increasingly displaced from the major population centres.

This shift of violence away from the towns, markets and extensively farmed areas is significant. It provides space for Afghan local confidence to grow and for security and economic gains to have tangible effect on an increasing number of Afghan lives.

Over the course of this year the insurgency has continued to evolve in preparation for the end of the ISAF mission in 2014. As part of this there has been continued insurgent emphasis on improving their internal governance structures to complement their fighting. Although the insurgency has probably not made the gains they would have hoped for, and their 2012 ‘al Farooq’ summer campaign does not appear to have achieved its strategic aims, they remain a resilient force and we should not underestimate the threat they represent
