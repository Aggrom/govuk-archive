Smart ticketing

Smart ticketing is the name given to the system where the entitlement to travel is stored electronically on a microchip rather than being printed on a paper ticket. In most smart ticketing schemes, the microchip on which the ticket is stored is embedded in a smartcard, for example the Oyster card used across London.

Integrated ticketing describes a system where the entitlement to travel is accepted on different transport modes or on services run by different operators.

ITSO and smart ticketing

The government supports ITSO as the technical specification for smart ticketing in England. ITSO is an open technical specification designed to provide a platform for interoperable smart ticketing. Its advantages include not being locked to specific suppliers, the capacity to include transport related ticketing and non-transport applications, and it provides a highly secure environment for ticketing transactions.

The specification is developed and maintained by ITSO Ltd, an independent not-for-profit company.

There are already a number of ITSO smart ticketing schemes in England, and the Scottish and Welsh national smart ticketing schemes also use the ITSO specification. The use of the common ITSO specification across England, Wales and Scotland is a key step towards ensuring that schemes developed in different areas can work together.

The South East Flexible Ticketing programme

The South East Flexible Ticketing (SEFT) programme is a government-funded scheme to introduce smartcards for rail commuters in the south-east of England. This group of passengers makes a third of all rail journeys in the UK.

The Department for Transport is working with train operators Transport for London and passenger organisations such as Passenger Focus on this programme. It will require the purchase and installation of back-office systems as well as new equipment at stations. The first products to be made smart will be season tickets, followed by other existing ticket types in 2013 and new products in 2014.

Passengers will benefit immediately from a switch to smart tickets because:

  • they trigger automatic ticket gates more quickly and reliably
  • it will be easier to buy tickets online and collect them at stations
  • they will help to shorten queues at ticket offices
  • they are much more durable than paper tickets and do not wear out as quickly
  • they will allow delay/repay procedures to be simplified

In the longer term, smart tickets will enable train operators to offer a more flexible range of products, better suited to the way we work and travel now. For example, a ‘part-time’ season ticket would benefit passengers who only travel 2 to 3 days per week.

The Managed Service

Bus operators in England receive an increase in the grant they receive from the government where ITSO compliant smart ticketing equipment has been installed. While this incentive has been very attractive to operators of larger bus fleets, it has been less attractive to small and medium sized operators because of the high capital outlay needed for equipment.

The Managed Service is a government-funded scheme that will offer small and medium sized bus operators the opportunity to equip their buses with smart ticketing equipment and receive maintenance and back office services.  With this offer, the government is aiming to establish an equal status for all bus operators and encourage the development of smart ticketing which can be used across different operators, using a consistent technical platform.

Multi-operator ticketing: guidance for councils

The Competition Commission reported on the operation of the local bus market in December 2011.

The report proposes a number of solutions to promote competitiveness in the bus market and improve the offer to passengers. These include guidance on developing multi-operator tickets. Multi-operator tickets allow passengers to use different bus operators’ services with a single ticket.

The guidance to support the development of a multi-operator ticket offer is due to be published in 2013. It’s aimed at local transport authorities and will give them options and ideas, rather than tell them what they should do.
