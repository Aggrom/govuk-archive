Tax and National Insurance after State Pension age, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Calculating tax and National Insurance

Paying National Insurance

When you reach State Pension age you stop paying National Insurance.

Paying tax

You only pay Income Tax if your taxable income - including your private pension and State Pension - is more than your tax-free allowances (the amount of income you’re allowed before you pay tax).

If you think you should be paying tax you must contact HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).

However, HMRC may have already contacted you to help you work out if you should be paying tax at State Pension age.

When you’re 65 HMRC may also send you an Age-related Personal Allowance form to calculate your age-related personal tax allowance. You must fill this in to get any age-related allowances.

If you’re within a month of reaching 65 and you haven’t been sent a form, contact HMRC.

Part 2: Making sure you've stopped paying National Insurance

National Insurance contributions build up your entitlement to your State Pension.

If you’re employed you pay Class 1 National Insurance contributions as a percentage of your earnings.

If you’re self-employed you pay Class 2 contributions at a flat weekly rate and Class 4 contributions annually, as a percentage of your taxable profits.

What happens at State Pension age

At State Pension age:

  • you stop paying Class 1 and Class 2 contributions, even if you’re still working
  • you still have to pay Class 4 contributions if you have taxable profits from the year you reach State Pension age - the next year, you’ll be exempt

You can claim back National Insurance if you’ve overpaid.

If you continue working, you need to get an Age Exemption Certificate to make sure you don’t pay National Insurance - contact HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) to get one.

Part 3: Tax on your State Pension and other income

If your total income from work, private pensions and the State Pension is more than your tax-free allowances you’ll have to pay tax.

You’re just getting the State Pension

Your State Pension is paid to you without any tax taken off. If you only get the State Pension you’re under the tax-free allowance and don’t have to pay tax.

If you have any other income apart from your State Pension and are over the tax free allowance, you’ll have to pay tax.

If you’re employed, HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) will give your employer a separate tax code and ask them to take any tax due from your earnings - this is called PAYE (Pay As You Earn).

If your tax affairs are complicated you might have to fill in a Self Assessment tax return (eg if you’ve foreign income or a lot of investment income).

If you’re self employed you must fill in a Self Assessment tax return at the end of the tax year, declaring your overall income, including your State Pension. HMRC will then work out your tax-free allowances.

If you get more than one pension

You’ll have several tax codes and pay tax in different ways.

Income from your State Pension, job and company or personal pensions is taxed through PAYE if you’re above your tax-free allowance. Any tax due will be taken out by your employer and the pension provider.

Income from self employment has to be declared by filing a Self Assessment tax return at the end of the tax year.

You can get your tax code corrected if you think it’s wrong.

You can claim a tax refund if you think you’ve paid too much.

If you’re working and getting a pension but you’re still on a low income you can get pension credit.

Part 4: Age-related tax allowances

As you get older there are allowances that can reduce your tax.

Increased Personal Allowance from age 65

If you get a Personal Allowance the amount increases in the tax year of your 65th birthday, depending on your taxable income.

Married Couple’s Allowance

You can claim the Married Couple’s Allowance if you’re married or in a civil partnership and at least one partner was born before 6 April 1935. It gets taken off your tax bill - the amount that’s deducted depends on your income.

Maintenance Payments Relief

You can get an allowance to reduce your tax bill for maintenance payments you make to an ex-spouse or civil partner if:

  • you or they were born before 6 April 1935
  • you’re separated or divorced and you’re making payments under a court order
  • the payments are for the maintenance of your ex-partner (as long as they haven’t re-married or formed a new civil partnership) or your children under 21

How much you can get

For the 2012 to 2013 tax year Maintenance Payments Relief can reduce your tax bill by the lower of the following:

  • £296 - where you make maintenance payments of £2,960 or more a year
  • 10% of the money you’ve actually paid - where you make payments of less than £2,960 a year

How to claim

You can claim Maintenance Payments Relief by contacting HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).

You can’t claim a tax reduction for any voluntary payments you make.

There’s more information about Income Tax rates and allowances online.

Part 5: Claiming back tax or National Insurance

National Insurance refunds

If you’ve overpaid tax or National Insurance you can claim it back.

Tax refunds

If you’re on a low income and have savings, you can claim back tax deducted from the savings interest.

You can claim a tax refund if you’ve:

  • had too much deducted from your pension
  • overpaid through your job
  • overpaid National Insurance

If you complete a tax return, you can also correct mistakes and claim a refund via Self Assessment.

Claiming back tax on savings interest

If you’re on a low income (less than £155 a week) you can register to have savings interest paid tax-free. You can also claim a refund of any tax you’ve overpaid on interest.

If your taxable income is only slightly higher than your tax-free allowance you can pay tax at a rate of 10% instead of the usual 20%.

You can check if you can get your interest tax-free or pay tax at the 10% rate.
