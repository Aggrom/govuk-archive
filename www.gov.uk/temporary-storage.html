Starting to import

Introduction

When you put imported goods into temporary storage, you only need to complete the presentation and summary declarations at that time. This means you can defer the full customs declaration and payment of duties and taxes on non-Community goods until the end of the permitted temporary storage period.

This guide explains what ‘temporary storage’ is and the benefits of using temporary storage facilities, the criteria such facilities must meet and the way in which they must be operated.

Temporary storage for imported goods

The term ‘temporary storage’ applies to the status of goods imported from outside the EU from the time that they are presented to Customs until the time that they are assigned to a customs-approved treatment or use. The term also applies to the special customs-approved premises or facilities that are used to store such goods - whether situated inside or outside the approved area of a sea or airport - during the time that the goods have the ‘status of goods in temporary storage’.

The term ‘customs approved treatment or use’ covers the following:

  • the placing of goods under a customs procedure
  • the entry of goods into a free zone or free warehouse
  • the re-exportation of goods from the customs territory of the community
  • the destruction of goods
  • the abandonment of goods to the Exchequer

The term ‘customs procedure’ used in the first point above covers the following procedures:

  • release for free circulation
  • transit
  • customs warehousing
  • inward processing
  • processing under Customs control
  • temporary admission
  • outward processing
  • exportation

Deferment options may be available for import, VAT and customs and excise duties, subject to certain criteria. For more information, see Notice 101 - deferring duty, VAT and other charges on the HMRC website.

Download form C1600A for the presentation of third country goods from the HMRC website (PDF, 4K).

How does temporary storage work and what are the benefits?

Goods imported from outside the EU must be presented to the Customs authorities by using an approved computerised trade inventory system linked to Customs or lodging form C1600A (presentation of Third Country Goods) at the designated customs office, the address of which will be advised by the port or airport concerned.

The goods must be presented within three hours of their arrival at the place of unloading - and be followed by a summary declaration within 24 hours of presentation of the goods. The summary declaration may be on an approved computerised trade inventory system linked to customs or by lodging form C1600 (goods arrived from non-EC countries) at the designated customs office at the port or airport concerned. A full customs declaration must be made immediately or shortly after the summary declaration. The full customs declaration assigns the goods to a customs-approved treatment or use. Payment of the relevant duty and taxes is made at the time of the full declaration or soon after.

However, where imported goods are put into temporary storage, only the presentation and summary declaration will need to be completed within the relevant timescales. The full customs declaration and payment of the relevant duties and taxes do not need to take place until the permissible time limit for temporary storage has expired.

Benefits of temporary storage to the trade

Some potential advantages of using temporary storage facilities to store goods imported from outside the EU are:

  • payment of duty and taxes on the imported goods will be deferred until the goods are assigned to a customs-approved treatment or use. However, security will be required in the form of a deed of undertaking signed by the temporary storage operator for goods while they are in temporary storage.
  • saving on storage costs at the port where traders have their own temporary storage premises.
  • time to obtain relevant import licences or further documentation if required, prior to completing a full customs declaration.

You may also want to look into speeding up the importation of your goods through customs while delaying submission of full documentation and customs and duty payments. You can find the detail on how to use this voluntary scheme which requires authorisation in the guide on Customs Freight Simplified Procedures.

Temporary storage authorisations and time limits for storage of goods

All importers can use temporary storage facilities, but only certain types of traders can apply to have their premises approved as a temporary storage facility. They are:

  • freight forwarders
  • customs agents
  • warehousekeepers
  • transport companies

All of these must be involved in the movement of non-Community goods.

Time limits

Goods in temporary storage must be cleared to a customs approved treatment or use within:

  • 45 days from the date of the summary declaration - in the case of goods brought in by sea
  • 20 days in the case of goods brought in by any other means such as air or road

These time limits may be extended by making a written application to HMRC at the National Clearance Hub in Salford:

Entry Processing Team
National Clearance Hub
Customs House
Furness Quay
Salford
M50 3ZZ
Fax Number: 0800 496 0699

Such extensions will only be granted where it is justified by the circumstances. Failure to obtain approval can result in the goods being seized, destroyed or sold.

Temporary storage facilities

Temporary storage facilities must be approved by HMRC. The premises in question must meet the criteria and conditions laid down by Customs, for example:

  • such facilities must meet the national health and safety at work legislation
  • they must be suitable for storing, loading, examining and sampling of goods
  • the facility must be physically secure and maintained in good repair
  • proper stock records must be kept

Full details of the conditions and criteria can be obtained from the HMRC National Frontier Approvals Unit on 0121 697 4045.

Types of temporary storage facilities

Temporary storage facilities may be situated inside or outside the approved area of a sea or airport.

There are five main types of temporary storage facilities:

  • transit shed - an approved place situated within the appointed area of an approved port or airport, but outside the Customs approved area
  • remote transit shed - an approved place within the boundaries of the appointed area of an approved port or airport
  • enhanced remote transit shed - an approved place situated outside the appointed area of the approved port or airport
  • inland clearance depot - an inland site usually made up of individual transit sheds, where goods arrive in containers or vehicles
  • inland rail depot - as above but in a rail environment

In some cases, temporary storage facilities may also include tanks and unshedded areas.

Forms of handling allowed in temporary storage

There is a restriction on the forms of processing or handling that is allowed on goods whilst they are in temporary storage. EU legislation restricts forms of handling in temporary storage to that which is necessary to ensure preservation of the goods in an unaltered state without modifying their appearance or technical characteristics.

The breaking up of individual packages in temporary storage is not permitted except for essential examination or preservation purposes. This means that all goods covered by a summary declaration, whether unloaded or not, must be re-presented intact and can only be unpacked after goods have been cleared to a customs approved treatment or use such as Free Circulation or Customs warehouse.

Read JCCC Customs Information Paper (CIP) (09) 66 on changes to part removal of consignments from temporary storage on the HMRC website.

Movement of goods to, and between, temporary storage facilities

EU legislation requires that goods that are not in free circulation and moving from one point in the Community to another may only do so under Customs control.

Therefore goods being removed to, from and between Temporary Storage facilities must be entered to the Community Transit procedure using the New Computerised Transit System or the Community Systems Providers National Transit simplification.

Where goods are to moved between the 27 EU Member States and the EFTA countries - Iceland, Norway, Liechtenstein and Switzerland - the common transit procedure must be used.

Read the Transit Manual on the Europa website.

Temporary storage and clearance

Sometimes storage is made at Enhanced Remote Transit Sheds (ERTS) or under Customs Freight Simplified Procedure (CFSP) arrangements for plant, animal and protected species imports.

All goods are eligible to be kept in temporary storage although some goods such as foodstuffs and firearms are subject to special requirements. You can download Public Notice 199A about temporary storage from the HMRC website (PDF, 175K). You can find out which products and species are restricted in our section on controlled goods.

Goods subject to checks prior to being moved to temporary storage facilities

Certain goods cannot be moved to a temporary storage facility from their place of importation until they have undergone physical and documentary checks. For example:

  • Veterinary products are subject to checks by the Department for the Environment Food and Rural Affairs (Defra). For more information, see the guides on using TRACES to trade in animals and animal products and overseas veterinary certificates and Border Inspection Posts.
  • Goods subject to control under the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES) must have a licence. For more information, see the guide on [https://whitehall-admin.production.alphagov.co.uk/government/admin/specialist-guides/3882).
  • Plants and fresh produce. For further information, see the guide on using the PEACH system to import plants and fresh produce. You can also download the plant health guide for importers from the Fera website (PDF, 108K).

Inland plant health examinations and clearance at ERTS or under CFSP arrangements

The Plant Health Seed Inspectorate, the Horticultural Marketing Inspection and the Forestry Commission provide a facility whereby all plant health controlled material imported from outside the EU can be brought inland to an ERTS or to a designated premises under the CFSP for examination and clearance purposes. You can read information on inland inspections at ERTS on the HMRC website or download CFSP Notice 760 from the HMRC website (PDF, 394K).

Temporary storage application and authorisation process

A summary of the application process:

Step 1: Application forms can be obtained from the HMRC National Frontier Approvals Unit on 0121 697 4045.

Step 2: The completed application form must be submitted to:

HMRC National Frontier Approvals Unit
2 Broadway
Broad St
Five Ways
Birmingham B15 1BG

Step 3: The request for approval must specify the type of goods to be stored. This will enable the National Frontier Approvals Unit to advise the applicant of other government departments that need to be contacted as part of the authorisation process. For example, the Home Office must be contacted regarding storage of firearms whilst Defra must be contacted for plant health related goods.

Step 4: HMRC will visit the premises in question to ensure that they are suitable and meet the appropriate criteria and regulations.

Download Notice 199A on temporary storage from the HMRC website (PDF, 175K).

Support and advice with temporary storage

As a business interested in applying for authorisation to operate temporary storage premises, you can turn to HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) for help and information.

Government sources of help and information

The government organisation with primary responsibility for providing trade support is HMRC. You can contact the HMRC VAT Helpline on 0845 010 9000. You can also obtain detailed information if you download Notice 199A on temporary storage from the HMRC website (PDF, 175K).

Temporary storage rules are based on the European Community Customs Code. You can find out about temporary storage status, the Customs Code and the transit procedure for moving goods in temporary storage on the European Commission website.
