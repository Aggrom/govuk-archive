Owning a river or canal boat, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You need to have the following in place before you can take your boat on canals or rivers in Britain:

  • insurance
  • proof that it’s safe
  • a place to store it when it isn’t being used (called a mooring)
  • the correct licence or registration

Part 2: Insurance

You must have ‘third party’ insurance for at least £1 million. This covers others if there’s a problem; it doesn’t cover your boat or crew.

Example: If you crashed into another boat, third party insurance would cover damage to that boat and its crew.

You may choose to take out a comprehensive policy to cover your own boat and crew as well.

The Waterscape website has a list of insurance companies that provide boat insurance.

Part 3: Boat safety

Your boat must meet the standards set out in the Boat Safety Scheme before you can get a licence or registration.

If your boat is new, it should already meet the standards. From then on, it will need to be tested every 4 years.

Part 4: Storing your boat

You’ll need a place to keep your boat when you aren’t using it. This is called a ‘mooring’.

There are hundreds of marinas and mooring sites across Britain, including:

  • moorings provided by private companies
  • bankside moorings offered by navigation authorities
  • boat club mooring - find a club near you on the Association of Waterways Cruising Clubs website

You won’t usually be given a licence or registration until you have a permanent mooring - unless you’re planning to live in the boat and move around.

When you’re on the move and looking for overnight stopping places, you can moor up on canal towpaths for free. There’s a fee even for temporary mooring on some rivers - check with the organisation that manages the waterway if you’re unsure.

Part 5: Licences and registrations

Once you have insurance and a boat safety certificate, you need to buy the right licence or registration for the waterway you want to use.

Almost all of Britain’s waterways are run by 3 main organisations:

  • British Waterways runs almost all the canals, and rivers such as the Severn, Trent and Yorkshire Ouse
  • the Environment Agency runs the River Thames, the River Medway, and the rivers of East Anglia
  • the Broads Authority runs the Norfolk and Suffolk Broads
