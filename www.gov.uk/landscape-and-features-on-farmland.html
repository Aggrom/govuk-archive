Landscape and features on farmland

Managing specific landscape features such as trees, hedgerows, dry stone walls and scheduled monuments, as well as funding.

Introduction

Our landscapes are an important part of our cultural heritage and must be planned, designed and managed sympathetically. Landscapes and features in the UK are protected by a range of different measures - including Cross Compliance requirements, planning policies and laws.

Natural England is the statutory adviser for landscapes with specific responsibilities for protecting national parks, areas of outstanding natural beauty and heritage coasts. It encourages protection of some of our best loved areas of outstanding landscape through participation in agri-environment schemes. The Forestry Commission and English Heritage also have responsibilities for certain aspects of landscapes and their features.

Landscape features and Cross Compliance

To qualify for full payment under the Single Payment Scheme (SPS) and other direct payments - eg the Environmental Stewardship schemes - you must meet all relevant cross-compliance requirements. These requirements are split into two types:

  • Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs)
  • requirements to keep your land in Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAECs)

For more information on SMRs and GAECs, see the guides on Cross Compliance: the basics, Statutory Management Requirements (SMR) and standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAEC).

GAEC 7 - Scheduled monuments

GAEC 7 aims to protect scheduled monuments. You can download GAEC 7 Scheduled monuments from the Rural Payments Agency (RPA) website (PDF, 730K).

If you have stone walls on your land you must comply with GAEC 13. If you have hedgerows on or adjoining your land you must comply with GAECs 14 and 15.

GAEC 13 - Stone walls

You must not remove a stone wall or remove stone from a stone wall, although there are some exceptions to this rule.

You can download GAEC 13 Stone walls from the RPA website (PDF, 738K).

GAEC 14 - Protection of hedgerows and watercourses

You must do everything you reasonably can to keep green cover on land within:

  • two metres of the centre of a hedgerow, watercourse or field ditch
  • one metre of the top of the bank of a watercourse of field ditch

You must not cultivate or apply fertilisers or pesticides on these strips of green cover, although there are certain exceptions to this rule.

Also, see the page on water pollution on farms.

You can download GAEC 14 Protection of hedgerows and watercourses from the RPA website (PDF, 1.08MB).

GAEC 15 - Protecting hedgerows

You must not remove hedgerows without written consent from your local authority nor may you cut or trim hedgerows between 1 March and 31 July.

You can download GAEC 15 Protection of hedgerows from the RPA website (PDF, 1.08MB).

GAECs 16 and 17 aim to protect trees which are important habitats and landscape features.

GAEC 16 - Felling of trees

You must not fell a tree without a felling licence or fail to comply with licence conditions, a restocking notice, enforcement notice or directions served on you.

You can download GAEC 16 Felling of trees from the RPA website (PDF, 748K).

GAEC 17 - Tree Preservation Orders

You must not cut down, damage or destroy, uproot, top or lop any tree protected by a Tree Preservation Order (TPO) without the written consent of your local planning authority. You must also give 42 days written notice to your local planning authority if you wish to cut down any tree located in a conservation area.

You can download GAEC 17 Tree Preservation Orders from the RPA website (PDF, 744K).

Hedgerows

Hedgerows are a priority habitat for conservation action through the UK Biodiversity Action Plan for rare and declining species. They are recognised as a priority habitat and refuge for many farmland wildlife species. It is against the law to remove or destroy certain hedgerows without prior permission from your local planning authority.

You can find the details on hedgerow legislation, management and preservation on the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website.

Healthy hedgerows can provide significant benefits for your farm management. They can:

  • help prevent soil erosion, pollution
  • help reduce water run-off and flooding
  • provide shelter, control livestock and protect crops from the wind
  • provide an important habitat for wildlife

Poor management, including excessive cutting/trimming can damage a hedgerow, so cutting is recommended on a two- to three-year cycle. Careful management can keep a hedge healthy and in good condition for many years. It can extend the period between management events such as laying or coppicing.

Hedge trimming

You should follow these basic principles for habitat-friendly hedge trimming:

  • Trim in January or February so as to protect birds’ nests. You must avoid trimming hedgerows between 1 March and 31 July - the main nesting season for birds. This will also allow development of berries for winter bird food. This is also a requirement under Cross Compliance.
  • Trim on a two or three-year rotation and avoid trimming all hedges in the same year so that thick nesting cover is available somewhere on the farm every year. In addition to saving time and money you will also be boosting the berry crop and populations of overwintering insects.
  • If soil or crop conditions mean that you need to carry out an autumn trimming regime then it is even more important that you trim on a two- or three-year rotation.

Hedgerows legislation

The Hedgerows Regulations 1997 provides protection for most countryside hedgerows by prohibiting their destruction or removal without permission from the local planning authority. You will need to obtain permission before removing hedges that are deemed to be ‘important’ and which meet criteria set out in the regulations. These criteria include hedges that are:

  • over 30 years old
  • 20 metres or more in length
  • contain certain plant species

Over 70 per cent of countryside hedgerows in the UK meet these criteria and are deemed to be ‘important’.

You can find a summary of the law regarding hedgerows in the leaflets ‘The Hedgerow Regulations: Your Questions Answered’ and ‘The Hedgerows Regulations 1997: A Guide to the Law and Good Practice’. You can obtain both leaflets free of charge by emailing Defra at farmland.conservation@defra.gsi.gov.uk

Hedgerows in areas covered by a Historic Landscape Character statement are often protected on the basis of historical importance and their value for wildlife.

If you claim payments under the Single Payment Scheme or certain Rural Development schemes and have hedgerows on or adjoining your land as part of Cross Compliance requirements - you must meet the standards of good agricultural and environmental condition (GAECs) 14 and 15 for protecting hedgerows.

You can find a full list of GAECs on the RPA website.

Funding

Funding for a number of hedgerow management options is available to farmers and land managers through Environmental Stewardship including:

  • Entry Level Stewardship - includes simple management options for hedgerow maintenance or enhanced hedgerow management. It also includes payments to move away from annual trimming and provide a winter food source for birds and mammals.
  • Uplands Entry Level Stewardship - this recently introduced strand of Environmental Stewardship includes options for stone-faced hedgebank management/restoration and hedgerow restoration.
  • Higher Level Stewardship - options include payments for Maintenance of Hedgerows of Very High Environmental value (HB 12/13) and capital item payments for new planting, laying and coppicing. It also allows hedgerow management to be tailored to meet specific requirements for target farmland birds and other species or to strengthen local historic landscape character.

For more advice on how to qualify for Environmental Stewardship see the guides on Environmental Stewardship: the basics, Entry Level Stewardship (ELS) and Higher Level Stewardship (HLS).

You can find information and advice on hedgerows and their management on the Hedgelink website. The DVD ‘Cut Above the Rest: managing hedges for the future’ and supporting information sheets produced by Hedgelink are available from the Natural England Enquiry Service on 0845 600 3078.

Managing and preserving trees and woodland

Trees are protected through a combination of best practice woodland management and legislation. A number of good practice guides on managing semi-natural woodlands and guidance sheets on minimising damage to woodlands are available from the Forestry Commission.

Download a guide on the prevention of mammal damage from the Forestry Commission website (PDF, 374K).

Download the Forestry Commission’s guide on the prevention of rabbit damage from the Agricultural Document Library (ADLib) website (PDF, 232K).

Download the Forestry Commission’s guide on the impact of deer on woodland biodiversity from the ADLib website (PDF, 127K).

The Forestry Commission has conducted detailed research into the restoration of native woodland on ancient woodland sites. You can download the Forestry Commission’s ancient woodland preservation guide from the ADLib website (PDF, 1.93MB).

The UK Woodland Assurance Standard

The UKWAS is an independent certification standard for verifying sustainable woodland management in the UK. It provides a way to assure buyers and users that wood and wood products come from sustainably managed woodlands.
 
You can read about the UKWAS on the UKWAS website.

Tree felling and Tree Preservation Orders

It is an offence to fell trees without a felling licence from the Forestry Commission unless an exemption applies. You can read more on the page on tree felling legislation in the guide on trees and woodland.

TPOs are issued by Local Planning Authorities (LPAs) which have discretionary powers under section 198 of the Town & Country Planning Act 1990 and the Town & Country Planning (Trees) Regulations 1999 to protect selected trees or woodland for public amenity and enjoyment. A TPO prohibits cutting down, uprooting, topping, lopping, wilful destruction or damage of trees subject to the order without LPA consent.

TPOs can apply to individual or groups of trees, woodland, or cover all trees in a defined area and can apply to hedgerow trees but not to hedgerows themselves. They tend to be most used in urban or semi-urban settings but can apply on farmland or parkland.

GAEC standards 16 and 17 aim to protect trees as they are important habitats and features of our landscape. GAEC 16 relates to felling of trees and GAEC 17 relates to TPOs. You must meet these standards if you claim payments under the Common Agricultural Policy such as the Single Payment Scheme or are signed up to agreements under Rural Development such as Environmental Stewardship.

You can download GAEC 16 Felling of trees from the RPA website (PDF, 676K).

You can download GAEC 17 Tree Preservation Orders from the RPA website (PDF, 667K).

Read more about GAEC in the guide to standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAEC).

Environmental Impact Assessments of forestry projects

Afforestation, deforestation, forest road works and forest-related quarrying are subject to EIAs and must not be carried out without permission from the Forestry Commission. For more information, see the guide on trees and woodland.

Native tree health and diseases

The Forestry Commission’s Plant Health Service (PHS) aims to protect forests and the timber industry in the UK and throughout the EU. The PHS publishes a range of advisory leaflets and posters on common pests and diseases. You can find PHS publications on the Forestry Commission website.

You can also read information about woodland pests and diseases on the Forestry Commission website.

Dry stone walls

Stone walls of all types are important as landscape and historic features and for stock management. There are four main issues to consider if you have dry stone walls on your land:

  • maintenance
  • legal issues
  • good practice
  • funding

Maintenance

Dry stone walls are made without the use of mortar or cement so any damage to walls should be repaired as soon as it happens. You should check the condition of walls at least once a year and any vegetation found should be removed. This ‘airs’ the wall and helps to prevent frost damage.

You should also take care that ivy does not destabilise the wall and if found should be removed as soon as possible to avoid weakening the wall.

Uncultivated strips of land running alongside walls are important habitats for wildlife and act as ‘wildlife corridors’.

Legal issues

Unlike hedgerows there is no general legal protection for dry stone walls. However, some walls may fall under the jurisdiction of other legislation due to their location. For example, the Hedgerows Regulations 1997 may apply to hedgerows on hedge banks - hedges growing on a stone-faced or earth bank, a particular feature in some areas such as Dartmoor and Exmoor.

Good practice

If you receive payments under the Single Payment Scheme and certain rural development schemes you are required to keep stone walls in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAEC) as part of Cross Compliance requirements. Particular attention should be paid to GAEC 13, which prohibits the removal of stone walls from farmland. In addition, stones must not be removed from walls, except under strict circumstances.

You can find guidelines on GAEC 13 concerning stone walls on the RPA website.

Read more about GAEC conditions in the guide on standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAEC).

Funding

Funding is available to farmers and land managers whose land is in Environmental Stewardship for dry stone wall management:

  • Entry Level Stewardship option EB11 encourages dry stone wall protection and maintenance for complete walls or complete sections of walls
  • Uplands Entry Level Stewardship has options for stone wall protection and maintenance above the Moorland Line (UB11) and  stone wall restoration (UB17)
  • in Higher Level Stewardship capital payments are available for stone wall repair and restoration options

For more information, see the guides on this site on Entry Level Stewardship (ELS) and Higher Level Stewardship (HLS).

Scheduled monuments

Scheduling refers to the legal system for protecting nationally important archaeological sites in England. Its aim is to preserve significant examples of the archaeological resource for the educational and cultural benefit of future generations.

Not all scheduled monuments are old or above ground - they might be archaeological sites from the prehistoric era, such as burial mounds, or 20th-century remains from the coal industry, World War II or the Cold War.

If you own or occupy a scheduled monument you will need to be aware of the issues and implications. For example, if you wish to carry out works to the scheduled monument, both above and below ground level, you will need to apply to your regional English Heritage office for their written consent. This is known as ‘scheduled monument consent’ (SMC). You can find contact details for English Heritage regional offices on the English Heritage website.

There may also be grants available to help maintain or repair scheduled monuments. Find information on grants for historic buildings, monuments and designed landscapes on the English Heritage website.

‘Works’ are defined by the Ancient Monuments and Archaeological Areas Act 1979 (as amended) as demolishing, destroying, damaging, removing, repairing, altering, adding to, flooding or tipping material onto the monument. To avoid damaging a monument you are strongly advised to consult English Heritage while in the planning stages of any intended works.

Certain development works to your property may also require planning permission from your local authority. Obtaining such permission does not remove the need for scheduled monument consent.

GAEC 7 relates to scheduled monuments. You must meet the requirements of this standard if you claim payments under the Common Agricultural Policy such as the Single Payment Scheme or are signed up to agreements under Rural Development such as Environmental Stewardship. You can find guidelines on GAEC 7 concerning scheduled monuments on the Rural Payments Agency (RPA) website.

Class consents

Certain existing or seasonal operations don’t require an application because permission is granted automatically. These are known as ‘class consents’. Most common class consent permits are for agriculture or domestic gardening. You must carefully check that your planned work fully matches the conditions and restrictions of the class consent.

Farm woodland funding schemes

Woodland that was eligible for the Single Payment Scheme (SPS) in 2008 keeps its eligibility for SPS payments even after trees have been planted on it. Since that change, in support of woodland creation, all new Forestry Commission English Woodland Grant Scheme (EWGS) contracts with Farm Woodland Payments are SPS compatible and landowners can continue claiming SPS as well as Farm Woodland Payments on land in their EWGS after planting woodland.

SPS eligible woodland must be kept in accordance with Cross Compliance rules.

In order to meet Cross Compliance standards you must keep your land in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAEC), as well as meeting certain SMRs. For more information on SMRs and GAEC, see information on Cross Compliance: the basics, Statutory Management Requirements (SMR) and standards of Good Agricultural and Environmental Condition (GAEC).

Environmental Stewardship

Payments for woodland options are available in Environmental Stewardship:

  • In Entry Level Stewardship (ELS) options include maintenance of stock-proof woodland boundaries to prevent environmental damage, and woodland edge management
  • Higher Level Stewardship (HLS) can fund woodland creation, restoration and maintenance. HLS options also include the creation, restoration and maintenance of wood pasture, scrub and orchards

Woodland grants

There are several woodland grants available under the Forestry Commission’s EWGS, including the:

  • Woodland Planning Grant - for the preparation of plans that assist woodland management
  • Woodland Assessment Grant - for the gathering of information to improve management decisions
  • Woodland Regeneration Grant - for improving woodland through natural regeneration and restocking after felling
  • Woodland Improvement Grant - for woodland work to create, enhance and sustain public benefits
  • Woodland Management Grant - for the cost of providing and sustaining better public benefits from existing woodlands
  • Woodland Creation Grant - for the creation of new woodlands to deliver benefits to the public - note that the deadline for woodland creation grant applications is 30 September 2010

EWGS applicants and the land must be registered on the Customer Register and the Rural Land Register respectively. EWGS is one of the Rural Development Programme for England (RDPE) schemes.

For more information, see the guide on trees and woodland.

Further information on landscape and features

Further information on maintaining landscape and features is available in other guides on this website and from the following organisations.

The Forestry Commission aims to protect and expand Britain’s forests and woodlands and increase their value to society and the environment. It manages 7,720 square kilometres of land in Britain and is responsible for forestry regulation. It also administers the English Woodland Grant Scheme (EWGS).

You can contact the Forestry Commission helpline on 0117 906 6000, or the dedicated EWGS hotline on 01223 346 004.

English Heritage is the body responsible for protecting and preserving England’s natural and cultural heritage. It takes an active role in identifying monuments to be scheduled, and can provide advice on land planning for heritage protection. You can call the English Heritage helpline on 0870 333 1181.

One of the major roles of Defra is to help the farming industry operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies through the Rural Development Programme for England 2007-2013. These provide approximately £3 billion of funding to UK agriculture. It also oversees a number of agencies that work with arable farmers, imports and exports of crops and implements pest and disease controls. You can call the Defra helpline on 08459 33 55 77.

The RPA is responsible for licences and schemes for growers as well as running the SPS and making Environmental Stewardship payments. For more information about SPS and how it can help your farming business, you can call the RPA helpline on 0845 603 7777.

You can also read a guide to the Single Payment Scheme (SPS).

In England, the Cross Compliance Advice Programme, as part of the Farm Advisory System provides advice to farmers about Cross Compliance requirements. They also organise events, including seminars and farm walks designed to help farmers meet their obligations. For further information you can call the Cross Compliance helpline on 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, find information on Cross Compliance requirements on the Cross Compliance website.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities.

You can read about the work of the NFU on the NFU website.

Natural England runs several schemes for farmers and landowners on behalf of Defra, including Environmental Stewardship. This is an agri-environment scheme that supplies funding to farmers and other land managers in England who deliver effective environmental management on their land. It consists of several strands:

  • Entry Level Stewardship (ELS) - simple and effective land management that goes beyond the Single Payment Scheme requirement to keep land in good agricultural and environmental condition.
  • Organic Entry Level Stewardship (OELS) - for land registered with a recognised organic body. Similar to ELS but offering higher payments to reflect the extra commitment and work required.
  • Uplands ELS  (UELS) - for land in Severely Disadvantaged Areas (SDAs). Similar to ELS but offering higher payments to reflect the difficulties associated with upland farming.
  • Higher Level Stewardship (HLS) - involves complex land management in targeted areas and which receives additional support and advice.

You can access the ELS Handbook (which includes  Uplands ELS) on the Natural England website.

You can access the HLS Handbook on the Natural England website.

Natural England also runs the Organic Entry Level Stewardship (OELS) funding scheme, which helps to support organic farmers in England. This is part of Natural England’s programme of financial incentives for farmers and land managers, aimed at protecting and enhancing the natural environment.

You can access the OELS Handbook on the Natural England website.

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities over a number of farming, land use and environmental regulations. Planning permission for work on hedgerows or scheduled monuments on your land will need to be obtained through the planning department at your local authority. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources. Find details of your local authority’s services on the Directgov website.
