Incorporating a UK business: guidance for agents

How to choose the right legal structure for your client's business, how to set up and register a business or change its structure.

Introduction

If a client asks you to handle the incorporation of their business or to change its legal status, there are various options available.

This guide gives outline information on the structures available to UK businesses, and shows you where to find information on setting up and registering different types of business. It also includes advice on how to help your client choose the most appropriate structure for their particular needs.

Choosing the right legal structure

There is a range of structures available to businesses in the UK, and choosing the right one depends largely on the type of business and on the personal preferences of the owners. As an agent, you will need to explain these different structures to your clients and where necessary advise them on choosing the most appropriate one for their needs. They will also need information on how to register the business.

The main types of business structure available to businesses in the UK are:

  • sole trader
  • partnership
  • registered company

There are different types of partnerships and registered companies and there are different kinds of European business structure that have been developed by the EU.

If your client has a business that trades for a social purpose, they may wish to register as a social enterprise. For more information, see the guide on how to set up a social enterprise.

Registering a legal structure

The way in which your client must register their business depends on the type of legal structure they want to set up.

Sole trader

A sole trader is a business that is owned and run by one person who takes all of the profits, but also accepts all of the risks and is responsible for all of the debts. To register as a sole trader you need to tell HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) within six months of setting up.

For more information, see the guide on how to set up and register as self-employed.

Partnership

There are two main types of partnership.

An unlimited (or standard) partnership is made between two or more people, who combine their skills and experience for the benefit of the business. Partners have unlimited liability for the debts of the business and share the profits between them in an agreed proportion. Unlimited partnerships must register with HMRC.

The second form of partnership is a limited liability partnership (LLP). An LLP is a flexible form of business where the personal liability of the partners is limited. The main difference between a limited company and an LLP is that an LLP has the organisational flexibility of a partnership and is taxed as a partnership. In other respects it is very similar to a private company. See the guide on how to set up and register an LLP.

All companies and LLPs must be registered with Companies House.

Registered company

A registered company has a separate identity from those who own or operate it. The main types of registered company are:

  • private company limited by shares - the liability of members is limited to any amount unpaid on shares they hold
  • private company limited by guarantee - the liability of members is limited by the amount they agree to contribute to the assets of the company if it is wound up
  • private unlimited company - this may or may not have share capital, but members have unlimited liability - for this reason, it has to disclose less information than other types of company
  • public limited company - the liability of members is limited to any amount unpaid on shares they hold, which may be traded on the stock exchange and sold to the general public

See the guide on how to set up and register a limited company (private or public).

Changing business structure

It is sometimes possible to change the structure of a business, depending on its circumstances. To change business structure, you must comply with the appropriate registration procedures and notify Companies House and HMRC.
