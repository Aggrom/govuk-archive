Criminal Records Bureau (CRB) checks, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

A Criminal Records Bureau (CRB) check may be needed for:

  • certain jobs - like working with children, or in healthcare
  • volunteering positions - eg to work with children
  • applying to foster or adopt a child

A full list of positions that require a CRB is available for download.

Download ‘CRB checks: eligibility guidance’ (PDF, 147KB)

If you’re the person being checked - ‘the applicant’ - your employer will give you a form to fill in and return to them along with documents proving your identity.

You can’t do a criminal records check on yourself.

Types of criminal records check

There are 2 types of check - the employer decides the level of check needed.

Type of check and cost What it will check for How long it normally takes
Standard - £26 Spent and unspent convictions, cautions, reprimands, final warnings About 2 weeks
Enhanced - £44 As above - plus information from local police records About 4 weeks

Checks for volunteers are usually free of charge.

An employer must not apply for a check unless the job needs one, and must tell the applicant why they’re being checked.

Part 2: Documents the applicant must provide

The person going through a Criminal Records Bureau (CRB) check - ‘the applicant’ - must give their employer original documents proving their identity. The documents needed will depend on the route the application takes. The applicant must try to provide documents from route 1 first.

Route 1

The applicant must be able to show:

  • 1 document from Table 1, below
  • 2 other documents from either Table 1 or Table 2a or 2b, below

One of the documents must show the applicant’s current address.

Route 2

If the applicant doesn’t have any of the documents in Table 1, then they must be able to show:

  • 1 document from Table 2a
  • 2 other documents from either Table 2a or 2b

One of the documents must show the applicant’s current address. The organisation conducting their ID check must then also use an appropriate external ID validation service to check the application.

Route 3

Route 3 can only be used if it hasn’t been possible to process the application through Routes 1 or 2.

For Route 3, the applicant must be able to show:

  • an original copy of their birth certificate (UK & Channel Islands)
  • 1 document from Table 2a
  • 3 further documents from Table 2a or 2b

One of the documents must show the applicant’s current address. If the applicant can’t provide these documents they may need to be fingerprinted.

Part 3: Tracking the application and getting a certificate

The person being given a Criminal Records Bureau (CRB) check - ‘the applicant’ - can check on the progress of their application using the CRB tracking service.

The applicant, employer and organisation that applied for the search will see the results of the check.

The CRB certificate

Once the check is completed, the CRB will send a certificate listing the results to both the applicant and the authorised person requesting the check.

Security features

Certificates have security features to prove they’re genuine:

  • a ‘crown seal’ watermark repeated down the right hand side, visible both on the surface and when holding it up to the light
  • a background design featuring the word ‘Disclosure’, which appears in a wave-like pattern across both sides of the certificate; the pattern’s colour alternates between blue and green on the reverse of the certificate
  • ink and paper that change colour when wet

Certificates printed by the CRB have these security features, but ones sent by email don’t.

Expiration

Although a CRB certificate doesn’t expire, it only contains information from a CRB check on a certain date and for a particular purpose. It can be accepted at a later date, but the employer will have to decide if it’s recent enough or suitable for the current purpose.

If the applicant loses the certificate

If the applicant loses the certificate, or it’s destroyed, the CRB can’t provide a replacement.

If there’s a mistake on the certificate

If the certificate contains incorrect personal details or criminal record information, the applicant should let the CRB know as soon as possible using the ‘Certificate disputes form’.

Download ‘Certificate disputes form’ (PDF, 148KB)

The applicant can also contact the CRB.

Part 4: Arranging CRB checks as an employer

Employers should only arrange a Criminal Records Bureau (CRB) check on a successful job applicant. Employers who need to do fewer than 100 checks a year can use an umbrella body.

Employers who need to do more than 100 checks a year can register to deal with their own applications if they arrange to do so with the CRB.

Employers who need to do more than 1,500 checks a year can use the CRB’s e-Bulk service, which can help reduce costs and waiting times for CRB checks.

Download ‘e-Bulk service guidance’ (PDF, 106KB)

Checks on carers

Employers providing care services for adults - eg in a care home - can use a service called Adult First.

This will confirm, usually within 2 days, if the applicant:

  • can start work, as long as they’re supervised
  • should wait for the results of an enhanced check

The service costs an extra £6.

Employers in Scotland or Northern Ireland

Employers in Scotland can get a criminal records check from Disclosure Scotland, while ones in Northern Ireland should go through Access NI.

Overseas applicants

If the applicant is from overseas, the employer can ask their foreign embassy if it can get a similar check done in their country.

Employing ex-offenders

If you carry out criminal records checks, you must have a policy on employing ex-offenders. You must show it to any applicant who asks for it.

Download ‘Sample policy on the recruitment of ex-offenders’ (PDF, 53KB)
