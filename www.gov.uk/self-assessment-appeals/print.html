Appeal against an HMRC tax decision, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Appeal against a penalty

You may be charged a penalty if you’ve missed the tax return or payment deadline.

You can appeal against the penalty if you’ve got a ‘reasonable excuse’ for being late.

What may be a reasonable excuse

A reasonable excuse is something that is unexpected and outside your control that prevented you from sending your tax return or payment on time, like:

  • a close family member died
  • you had a serious or life-threatening illness
  • you lost important documents in a fire and couldn’t complete your tax return
  • your cheque wasn’t paid or your payment failed because of an error by your bank
  • you registered for the HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) online service on time but didn’t get your activation code or user ID in time

What’s unlikely to be a reasonable excuse

The following aren’t usually accepted as a reasonable excuse:

  • pressure of work
  • you found the tax return too difficult to complete
  • your cheque bounced or payment failed because you didn’t have enough money
  • you didn’t get a reminder from HMRC
  • you registered for the HMRC online service too late and didn’t get your activation code or user ID in time

How to appeal

You can:

  • fill in an appeal form
  • use the appeal form that came with your penalty letter

Only the nominated partner can appeal against penalties for sending partnership tax returns late.

Appeal deadlines

You must appeal within 30 days of the date of the penalty letter you get from HMRC.

If you appeal after 30 days, HMRC will decide if they will consider your appeal.

Part 2: Appeal against a tax bill

If you think your tax bill is wrong

If you think you’ve made a mistake on your tax return, you may be able to correct it.

If HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) has estimated the bill because you didn’t send a tax return, you should send your tax return.

You’ll be sent a new tax bill if you correct or amend your tax return.

If you disagree with your bill because HMRC have changed what you owe after a check on your return, you can appeal against it.

Appeal deadlines

You have 30 days from the date of HMRC’s letter to appeal.

If you appeal after 30 days, you’ll have to explain why. HMRC will decide if they will accept your appeal.

How to appeal

You can:

  • use the appeal form if one came with the letter HMRC sent you
  • appeal in writing to the office that sent the letter

Appeal by letter

If you appeal by letter, you must include:

  • your name
  • your 10-digit unique taxpayer reference number
  • the amount of the tax or penalty you’re appealing against
  • the reasons why you’re appealing

You must sign the letter.

Part 3: Delay payment of a penalty or tax bill

You may be able to delay paying a penalty or tax bill if you’re appealing the amount you have to pay.

Write to the HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) office that sent you the letter about your tax bill or penalty, telling them:

  • why you think the amount you’ve been asked to pay is too much
  • what you think the correct amount is and when you’ll pay it

HMRC will tell you in writing if they agree.

You may have to pay interest on the amount you don’t pay straight away.

If you win your appeal, the interest will be cancelled and repaid. If you lose your appeal, you may have to pay interest.

Part 4: After your appeal

HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) will tell you their decision in writing - along with how much you must pay or be repaid.

If you disagree with the appeal decision, you can either:

  • ask HMRC to review their decision
  • appeal to the independent tax tribunal
