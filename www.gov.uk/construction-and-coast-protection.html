Marine construction and coastal protection

Which licences you need for marine construction works, and how to get them. Also how you can get emergency works approved.

Introduction

Marine construction is a highly regulated area which requires consent granted under the Coast Protection Act 1949 (CPA) and a licence granted under the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 (FEPA). Other consents may be needed, and a formal environmental impact assessment may also be required. All marine construction projects need scientific evaluation to prevent or minimise damage to the environment.

When planning coastal flood defences, coastal and flood authorities will need approval from other bodies, such as the Environment Agency, as well as CPA consent and a FEPA licence. Genuine emergency work is permitted with just prior written notice, but the authority must apply for a FEPA licence as soon as possible after the work has begun.

Marine construction

Consents or licences are required for a broad range of marine construction projects. For regulatory purposes, marine construction includes the use of materials for works such as:

  • new harbours and marinas
  • marine structures and piers
  • outfalls
  • pontoons and jetties
  • offshore wind farms and energy facilities
  • land reclamation
  • flood defences and sea walls

It also includes the use of materials in ‘soft-engineered’ coast protection works like beach nourishment and associated groynes and revetments.

Licences and consents needed

Marine construction projects where materials or articles are placed in the sea or in tidal waters below the mean high water spring tide mark usually require CPA consent and a FEPA construction licence.

Other requirements

When you apply for CPA consent and a FEPA licence, the nature of your application may trigger the need for you to carry out a formal environmental impact assessment. You can find out about environmental impact assessments on the Marine Management Organisation (MMO) website.

Depending on the location of your proposed project, you may also have to take into account conservation regulations and carry out an appropriate assessment. You can read about protected conservation areas on the MMO website.

The future of marine licensing

In spring 2011, the marine licensing system was simplified and the existing CPA and FEPA systems replaced by a single licensing system. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Environmental protection

When the MMO considers an application, the estimated benefits of the project are weighed against the potential impact on the environment, and the need to conserve natural resources and prevent undue interference to existing uses of the sea.

All construction projects need detailed scientific evaluation to ensure that adverse environmental impact can be prevented or reduced. Although concern for the environment is key to this evaluation, it also takes into account legitimate development aspirations and the need to promote economic growth.

Further consents may be required, particularly if your works extend into inter-tidal areas or are land-based. You may also need separate consents or licences for disposing of material arising from your project - see the guide on licensing and disposing of dredged material at sea.

Coast protection works

Coast protection works require CPA consent and a FEPA licence. They are also likely to need other consents, including approval from the Environment Agency.

Streamlining the consents process

The Environment Agency will generally only approve a coast protection project if a FEPA licence has been, or will be, granted.

Coast protection authorities are legally obliged to advertise any proposed coast protection, sea or tidal flood defence works in relevant newspapers to inform interested parties in the area of their plans and give them time to comment.

The process of obtaining Environment Agency approval can be streamlined if the authority contacts the MMO regarding their FEPA licence at around the time they place the advertisement. Because the proposals for the works are likely to be sufficiently advanced at that point, the MMO will be able to advise the authority on whether a FEPA licence will be granted. The authority can then satisfy the Environment Agency that a FEPA licence will be granted by the MMO, allowing the Environment Agency to grant approval.

For detailed information on the regulatory aspects of carrying out coastal protection works, download a guide for organisations working on the coast from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 1.92MB).

Emergency coastal protection works

The MMO recognises that flood and coastal defence authorities are occasionally required to carry out genuine emergency works to protect lives or property that are at imminent risk.

In cases like these there may not be enough time for the authority to make a formal application to the MMO for the necessary FEPA licence. So it’s normally acceptable for the authority to give the MMO prior written notice of their intentions, as long as they submit a FEPA licence application and a supporting explanation as soon as possible after the work begins.

CPA 1949 consents and FEPA 1985 licences

The system of CPA consents and FEPA licensing is used to protect the offshore environment.

CPA consents

CPA consent is required for:

  • the construction, alteration or improvement of any works that are on, under or over any part of the seashore lying below the level of mean high water springs
  • the deposit of any object or materials below the level of mean high water springs
  • the removal of any object or materials from the seashore below the level of mean low water springs - for example by dredging

CPA consent normally lasts for three years. However, if a FEPA licence is required as well as CPA consent, the duration of the consent changes so that they both expire at the same time. You can find out about CPA jurisdiction and what’s considered before consent is granted on the MMO website.

FEPA licences

FEPA licences are mainly used to permit construction within the marine environment and to control deposits of articles into the sea or tidal waters. FEPA licences for construction and coastal protection works usually last for one year. You can get details of the types of construction work a FEPA licence is needed for on the MMO website.

FEPA licence exemptions

There are a range of activities that involve depositing materials into the sea which are exempt from the FEPA licensing requirements. These include deposits of:

  • scientific instruments
  • fishing gear
  • material at an oil or gas drilling site

You can read a detailed list of exemptions from FEPA licensing on the MMO website.

The future of marine licensing

In spring 2011, the marine licensing system was simplified and the existing CPA and FEPA systems replaced by a single licensing system. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Applying for CPA 1949 consents and FEPA 1985 licences

Before you apply for a FEPA licence and/or a CPA 1949 consent you need to decide if you have to:

  • contact the MMO - for large, novel or contentious projects you’re advised to get in touch with the MMO before you apply
  • provide an environmental statement - you can find out about environmental impact assessments on the MMO website
  • consider any ‘beneficial use’ - you can get information about FEPA licences for disposing of material in a beneficial way on the MMO website

Conservation regulations

Depending on the location of your proposed works, your application may be subject to either of the following:

  • conservation regulations that apply to works within 12 nautical miles offshore - read about protected conservation areas on the MMO website
  • offshore marine conservation regulations that apply to works beyond 12 nautical miles - read about European protected sites on the MMO website

Fees

The cost of a FEPA licence varies depending on its type. The fee charged can also depend on the licence band, which is decided by the overall cost of the construction project. You can find out about FEPA licence costs on the MMO website.

The application process

You can apply for CPA consent and/or a FEPA licence by:

  • filling in an online application form
  • downloading - or requesting - an application form and sending the completed form to the MMO

You can get application forms and find out how to apply for CPA consent and FEPA licences on the MMO website.
