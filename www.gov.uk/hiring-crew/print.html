Hiring crew for ships and yachts, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

When you’re hiring crew for a ship or yacht, you must:

  • check your crew members’ discharge books to make sure that they have the right qualifications and certificates of competency for the jobs they’ll be doing
  • check that all crew members have the necessary medical certificate for the work they’ll be doing
  • make sure that everyone has the necessary travel documents
  • check that all crew can speak the ship’s common working language
  • draw up a crew agreement
  • send a full crew list to the owner of the ship or yacht

You must make sure that all crew members are familiar with the ship’s safety and emergency procedures before the start of your journey.

Part 2: Checking crew qualifications

You must check crew members’ discharge books to make sure that everyone has the necessary qualifications and experience.

Crew members doing specialist jobs or working on particular types of craft may need special training.

Officers

Check that the officers in your crew have the necessary certificates of competency.

Merchant navy ratings

Make sure that all crew have the correct rating for the work they’ll be doing. Watch ratings need a certificate of competency if they’re performing navigation or engine room duties.

Crew on large yachts

If you’re hiring crew for a yacht that’s 24 metres long or over, you must make sure that they have the correct certification.

Part 3: Certificates of competency

Some members of your crew will need a certificate of competency (CoC) before they can carry out certain duties.

Deck officers

Masters and other deck department officers need a CoC if they’re performing:

  • bridge watch-keeping duties
  • navigational duties

CoCs for deck officers are restricted depending on:

  • the size of the ship or yacht
  • the area of sea where it will be operating

Engineer officers

Engineer officers on a ship or yacht with a power output of 750 kilowatts or more need a CoC. There are different CoCs for engineer officers, depending on:

  • the power output of the ship or yacht
  • the area of sea where it will be operating

Watch ratings

Watch ratings on merchant ships need a CoC if they’re performing navigation or engine room duties.

Radio operators

Radio operators need CoCs if they handle distress and safety radio-communications. All radio personnel serving on UK-registered ships must have either:

  • a Restricted Operator’s Certificate (ROC)
  • a General Operator’s Certificate (GOC)

Other crew

Ship’s cooks and security officers may also need a CoC.

Certificates of equivalent competency

If you hold a CoC or similar certification from another country, you may be able to apply for a certificate of equivalent competency.

Part 4: Crew agreements

A crew agreement is an employment contract between a ship or yacht’s owners and its crew.

All crew agreements must have:

  • an outer cover with details of the ship and its owners
  • an up-to-date crew list with names, dates of birth and addresses
  • a list of anyone on board who is under 18 or exempt from a crew agreement
  • ‘contractual clauses’ for each crew member

A crew agreement can be for a maximum of 12 months. After this period, a new agreement must be drawn up.

What goes in a contractual clause

These must include:

  • the name of the crew member
  • a description of the journey(s) that the agreement relates to
  • the crew member’s job description
  • details of their pay, hours and leave
  • details of notice and how the crew agreement is to be terminated

The Maritime and Coastguard Agency (MCA) provides guidance on drawing up crew agreements for merchant ships and yachts.

What to do once you’ve drawn up a crew agreement

  1. Get every crew member to sign it when they join the ship and at the end of the journey.

  2. File it with the shipping registry of the ship’s flag.

  3. Display it onboard the vessel.

  4. Send it (with the official log book, if a merchant ship) to a superintendent or proper officer within 3 days of its expiry.

#Who has to sign a crew agreement

Most of the people on board a ship or yacht must sign a crew agreement. However, certain personnel will have separate employment contracts and won’t have to sign, like:

  • captains
  • bodyguards
  • nannies
  • entertainment staff

Part 5: Travel documents

All crew members must have either:

  • a valid passport
  • a Seafarers Identity Document (SID) containing a photograph, signature (or fingerprints) and a description of the holder, including their nationality

The standard seafarer’s identity document for British citizens is the British Seaman’s card.

Part 6: Crew lists

The master or skipper of a UK-registered ship must provide the ship’s owner with a copy of an up-to-date crew list at the start of each journey. They must also tell the owner if there are any changes of crew during the journey.

If a ship is lost at sea, the crew list will be used to find out who is missing. The ship’s owner should hand the crew list in to a local Maritime and Coastguard Agency (MCA) Marine Office, or post it to:
