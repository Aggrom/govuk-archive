Special educational needs and disability tribunal, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can appeal to the special educational needs and disability tribunal if you disagree with the council’s decisions about your child’s special educational needs.

For example, the council may not agree that your child needs extra support in the classroom to help them with learning difficulties.

You can also appeal to the tribunal if the school or council has discriminated against your disabled child.

Special educational needs appeals

You can appeal for a number of reasons, eg if the council refuses to:

  • assess your child’s special educational needs
  • make a statement of their needs
  • reassess their needs

You usually have to appeal to the tribunal within 2 months of getting the council’s decision letter.

Help and advice

You may want to get legal advice before you appeal.

You could be entitled to legal aid to pay for a meeting with a solicitor.

You can also get help and advice from your local Parent Partnership Service.

Contact the National Parent Partnership Network for details of your nearest service.

National Parent Partnership Network
Email: nppn@ncb.org.uk

You live in Wales

If you live in Wales, you can appeal to the Special Educational Needs Tribunal for Wales.

Part 2: Before you appeal

Before you appeal, see if you can discuss the problem with your school or the council.

A free mediation service in your area can help you do this.

The service will bring you, the school and the council together to see if you can work out a solution.

You don’t have to try this, but if you do it won’t affect your right to appeal.

Part 4: Going to a tribunal hearing

The tribunal will hold the hearing at a regional hearing centre.

You don’t have to go to the hearing, but you may want to ask the council questions and put your case in person.

Fill in the attendance form if you want to bring:

  • someone to represent you
  • someone to support you at the hearing

Download ‘attendance form’ (PDF, 61KB)

Who will be at the hearing

The hearing will usually be attended by:

  • up to 3 tribunal members
  • a tribunal clerk
  • someone representing the council
  • any witnesses

Both parents can attend.

You are usually allowed to bring up to 3 witnesses to a hearing.

You may not have to call professional witnesses if they have already written a report on your child.

You can bring your child, but you’ll have to arrange for someone to look after them when they’re not in the hearing room.

Travel costs

You and your witnesses will be able to claim for travel expenses for going to the hearing.

If you bring a friend or relative to the hearing, you will also be able to claim for their travel costs.

The tribunal clerk will give you the forms you need.

You will usually get the tribunal’s decision in the post within 10 days of the hearing.

Part 5: If you win your appeal

If you win your appeal, the council must act on the tribunal’s decision within a fixed amount of time:

Decision When council must act on decision
Start assessment or reassessment within 4 weeks
Make statement of child’s needs within 5 weeks
Change statement within 5 weeks
Change the school within 2 weeks
Continue with statement of child’s needs at once
Cancel a statement at once

If the council doesn’t keep to the decision you can complain to the Local Government Ombudsman:

Part 6: If you lose your appeal

If you lose your case, you may be able to:

  • ask the tribunal to look at the case again, or overturn it
  • appeal against the decision

The tribunal will send you details of how you can ask for the decision to be reviewed or ‘set aside’.

To appeal against the decision, fill in the application for permission to appeal form.

Send this to the special educational needs tribunal:

First-tier Tribunal (Special Educational Needs and Disability) Mowden Hall
Staindrop Road
Darlington
DL3 9BG

Email: sendistqueries@hmcts.gsi.gov.uk
Telephone: 01325 392 760
Fax: 01325 391 080
or 01325 391 310

You must appeal within 28 days of the date of the tribunal’s decision letter.

If you’re given permission to appeal, the case will be heard by a higher tribunal called the Upper Tribunal (Administrative Appeals Chamber).
