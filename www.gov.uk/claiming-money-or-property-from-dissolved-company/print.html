Claiming money or property from a dissolved company, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

When a company is dissolved, all of its assets pass to the Crown, and are legally known as ‘bona vacantia’ (ownerless property). Assets include:

  • property and land
  • mortgages
  • shares
  • intellectual property, eg trademarks, registered designs and patents

If you were a director or shareholder of a dissolved company, you may be able to restore the company without a court order.

Claiming or buying assets

You may be able to claim or buy assets from the dissolved company if:

  • they owe you money - by getting a court order to restore the company
  • you’re someone affected the company closing - by ‘referring’ their assets
  • you were a shareholder - by applying for a discretionary grant

Part 2: Claiming money you're owed

If you’re owed money by a dissolved company, you can apply for a court order to have the company restored. This is the first step in trying to get your money back.

Court order restoration

You may be able to apply for a court order to restore a company if:

  • you did business with them
  • you worked for them
  • they owed you money when they were dissolved
  • you’re responsible for their employee pension fund
  • you have a shared or competing interest in land
  • you were a shareholder or director when it was dissolved

Except in cases of personal injury, you need to apply within 6 years of the company being dissolved.

You can get information about a dissolved company from Companies House.

How to apply

Use form N208 to apply for court order restoration in England and Wales.

Download ‘Form 208, claim to restore by court order’ (PDF, 117KB)

Download ‘Form 208a, guidance notes’ (PDF, 437KB)

Send your completed form to the nearest county court to the company’s registered office that deals with bankruptcy (select ‘County Court’ under ‘Search by court type’ in the court finder), or the Royal Courts of Justice.

You’ll also need to include:

  • the court fee (cash, postal order or cheque made payable to Her Majesty’s Courts & Tribunal Service)
  • a witness statement, containing the supporting information outlined in section 4 of the Treasury Solicitor’s guide to company restoration

See the ‘guide to company restoration’ for information about restoring a company.

In Scotland

Apply to the Court of Session if initial value of the company’s shares that have been paid for (‘paid-up capital’) is more than £120,000.

For other companies, apply to the local sheriff court.

You’ll then have to serve a ‘petition to restore’ on the Registrar of Companies in Scotland, and any other bodies the court asks you to.

What happens next

If your claim is accepted, the court will issue an Order to restore the company - you’ll be sent this, and you must send it on to the Registrar of Companies. Once they have it they will restore the company.

You’ll then need to take further action to try and get your money:

  • get a ‘judgment’ from court for the amount of the money you’re owed together with interest and costs
  • issue a statutory demand
  • take out a winding up petition

Part 3: Buying a dissolved company's assets

If you want to claim or buy an asset belonging to a dissolved company, you may be able to do this by ‘referring’ the asset to the body representing the Crown. This depends on where the company was registered.

Anybody can refer an asset, including if:

  • you’re the leaseholder of a property where the freehold was owned by the company
  • you want to buy or are affected by land owned by the company
  • you want to buy other assets held by the company like shares, trade marks or copyrights
  • you’re a shareholder trying to get cash held by the company

How to refer an asset

In England and Wales (but not Cornwall or Lancashire), the Treasury Solicitor looks after dissolved company assets. Use the dissolved company notification form to refer an asset to the Treasury Solicitor.

Part 4: Discretionary grants for shareholders

You may be able to get back a dissolved company’s assets if you were one of its shareholders - apply for a discretionary grant.

How to apply

In England and Wales (but not Cornwall or Lancashire) you must apply to the Treasury Solicitor.

Send an application form and a statutory declaration to the Treasury Solicitor’s Office.
