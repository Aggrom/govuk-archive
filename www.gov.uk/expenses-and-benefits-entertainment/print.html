Expenses and benefits: entertainment, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

As an employer covering the costs of your employees providing entertainment for clients, you have certain tax, National Insurance and reporting obligations.

What’s included

Entertainment can involve eating, drinking and other hospitality.

Types of entertainment include:

  • ‘business entertainment’ of clients - eg discussing a particular business project or forming or maintaining a business connection
  • ‘non-business entertainment’ of clients - eg entertaining a business acquaintance for social reasons

Part 2: What's exempt

You don’t have to report or pay anything to HMRC if the employee you provide expenses for earns at a rate of less than £8,500 and it’s:

  • ‘business entertainment’
  • ‘non-business entertainment’ but you pay and arrange it

Part 3: What to report and pay

If the entertainment isn’t exempt, you must report it to HMRC, and you may have to deduct and pay tax and National Insurance on it.

Business entertainment of clients

The same rules apply regardless of whether you:

  • arrange and pay for the entertainment directly
  • pay the supplier for entertainment arranged by your employee
  • reimburse your employee’s entertainment costs
Who gets the benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more a year Report on form P11D, section K, L, M or N Nothing
Directors Report on form P11D, section K, L, M or N Nothing

You can apply for a dispensation for expenses relating to business entertainment of clients, which means you won’t have to report them.

Non-business entertainment – you arrange and pay

Benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more per year Report on form P11D, section K, L or M Pay Class 1A National Insurance on the value of the benefit
Directors Report on form P11D, section K, L or M Pay Class 1A National Insurance on the value of the benefit

Non-business entertainment – employee arranges, you pay

Benefit What to do What to deduct or pay
Employees earning at a rate of less than £8,500 a year Report on form P9D, section A(2) Deduct and pay Class 1 National Insurance (but not PAYE tax) on the value of the benefit through payroll
Employees earning at a rate of £8,500 or more per year Report on form P11D, section K, L, M or N Deduct and pay Class 1 National Insurance (but not PAYE tax) on the value of the benefit through payroll
Directors Report on form P11D, section K, L, M or N Deduct and pay Class 1 National Insurance (but not PAYE tax) on the value of the benefit through payroll

Non-business entertainment – employee arranges and pays, you reimburse

The amount you reimburse counts as earnings, so:

  • add it to your employee’s other earnings
  • deduct and pay PAYE tax and Class 1 National Insurance through payroll

Note about form P11D

Please note that there’s an entertainment-related tick-box on form P11D, section N. This is used by HMRC to determine whether or not your employee will be able to claim a tax deduction for the entertainment expenses you’ve provided.

There’s no need to enter anything in the box if you’re completing this form for a charity or a tonnage tax company, but otherwise:

  • put a tick in the box if the cost of the entertainment will be disallowed in your business’s tax calculations
  • put a cross in the box if it won’t be disallowed
