Parking tickets, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can get a parking ticket from:

  • local councils
  • the police
  • Transport for London
  • private companies

Ticket types

The most common type of parking ticket is a Penalty Charge Notice (PCN). These are issued by local councils and Transport for London.

In most cases, you have 28 days to pay or challenge the ticket. Find out how to pay.

Other types of parking ticket are:

  • Fixed Penalty Notices (FPNs): issued by the police
  • Excess or Standard Charge Notices (ECNs and SCNs): issued by some local councils

Private companies can issue parking tickets and may call them PCNs. These are not the same as PCNs issued by a council.

Driving points

If you get a PCN, ECN or SCN you won’t get any points on your driving licence.

You also won’t get points for most parking FPNs issued by the police.

Part 2: Paying a ticket

Penalty Charge Notices issued by local councils

The cost of a Penalty Charge Notice (PCN) parking ticket goes up the longer you don’t pay it:

  1. If you pay within 14 days, the penalty charge is reduced by 50% (or 21 days if your car was caught on camera and the PCN is posted to you).

  2. If you pay after 14 days but within 28 days you pay the full amount.

  3. If you don’t pay within 28 days, the council will send you a reminder (called a ‘Notice to Owner’).

  4. If you don’t pay within 28 days of getting the reminder, the council may increase the penalty charge by 50%.

If you think the PCN is wrong, you can challenge it.

Example: A £60 PCN will cost you £30 if paid within 14 days, £60 if paid within 56 days, and up to £90 if paid after 56 days.

Fixed Penalty Notice (FPN)

You have 28 days to pay an FPN or ask for a hearing in court if you want to appeal it. After this, the fine increases by 50%.

If you pay the fine, you won’t be prosecuted and no record of your offence will be kept.

If you ignore it, you’ll receive a court summons.

Excess or Standard Charge Notice (ECN or SCN)

You usually have 28 days to pay an ECN or SCN. In most cases, the fine is reduced by 50% if you pay it within 14 days.

Ways to pay

These are usually listed on the ticket. The main ways are:

  • online
  • by credit or debit card over the phone
  • in person at your council’s customer service offices
  • by cheque or postal order

Part 3: Challenging a ticket

Penalty Charge Notice (PCN)

If you think the ticket is wrong, you can challenge it:

  1. If you challenge it within 28 days, this is called an ‘informal challenge’. You need to write to your council or challenge it online. If you challenge it within 14 days, and the council turns you down, the council may give you more time to pay only 50% of the penalty charge.

  2. If the council sends you a ‘Notice to Owner’, you can challenge it. This is called a ‘formal challenge’.

  3. The council has 56 days to consider your formal challenge.

  4. If the council agrees with you, it will cancel the fine. If it turns your challenge down, it will send you a ‘Notice of Rejection’.

  5. If you disagree with the ‘Notice of Rejection’, you have 28 days to appeal.

You can appeal to the:

  • Traffic Penalty Tribunal for PCNs issued in England (outside London)
  • Parking and Traffic Appeals service (PATAS) for PCNs issued in London

Fixed Penalty Notice (FPN)

To challenge an FPN, you need to go to a magistrate’s court. The back of the ticket will tell you how to do this.

You’ll be sent a summons with a date to attend a court hearing.

Excess or Standard Charge Notice (ECN and SCN)

You appeal to the local council who issued the ECN or SCN. Some councils let you appeal online.
