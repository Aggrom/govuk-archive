Driving disqualifications, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can be disqualified from driving if you’re convicted of a driving offence. You might have to take your driving test again, answer health questions or have medical tests before you get your licence back.

You can also be disqualified if you get 12 or more penalty points (endorsements) within 3 years. You can be disqualified for:

  • 6 months if you get 12 penalty points or more within 3 years

  • 12 months if you get a 2nd disqualification within 3 years

  • 2 years if you get a 3rd disqualification

Short period disqualification

If you’re disqualified for less than 56 days, your paper driving licence or counterpart document will be stamped by the court and given back to you. This is called ‘short period disqualification’.

You won’t need to renew your licence. The stamp shows the disqualification period and you can use the same licence again once this is over.

Disqualification for 56 days or more

If you’re disqualified for 56 days or more you have to renew your licence before driving again.

You might also have to retake your driving test or take an extended driving test before getting your licence back.

If you’ve been disqualified for some alcohol-related offences, DVLA will make medical enquiries before you can get your licence back.

Part 2: Disqualification until 'test pass' or 'extended test pass'

If you’re disqualified until ‘test pass’ or ‘extended test pass’, you’ll need to apply for a provisional driving licence and take your theory and practical tests again before you can get your full licence back.

Motorcycle riders will have to complete compulsory basic training (CBT).

Disqualified until extended test pass

You’ll have to sit an extended practical driving test if you’ve been disqualified until extended test pass.

You’ll need to pass a test in category A (motorcycle) or B (car) if you want to drive all categories of vehicles you were previously entitled to.

The local traffic commissioner will need to agree before you can get category C (large vehicle) and or D (bus) entitlements back.

Part 3: Disqualification for alcohol-related offences

If you’re disqualified for some alcohol-related offences, DVLA will make medical enquiries before you can renew your driving licence.

These offences are:

  • disqualified with an alcohol level of over 200mg in 100ml of blood, or 87.5mg in 100ml of breath, or 267.5mg in 100ml of urine

  • 2 disqualifications within 10 years for drinking and driving, or being in charge of a vehicle while unfit through drink

  • disqualified for refusing or failing to supply a specimen for analysis

You may also have to attend and pay for a medical examination and pay a higher fee to renew your driving licence.

Part 4: Checking when your disqualification ends

To check when your disqualification ends you can:

  • check the D27 reminder sent by DVLA

  • view your driving licence details online - if you got your licence after applying on the internet

  • phone DVLA customer enquiries

Part 5: Disqualification outside Great Britain

If you’ve been disqualified on your Great Britain driving licence you’re also disqualified from driving in Northern Ireland, Ireland and the Isle of Man.

This is called ‘mutual recognition of disqualification’. Disqualified drivers from those areas are also disqualified from driving in Great Britain.

Part 6: Applying to reduce your disqualification period

You can ask the court to reduce your disqualification period if you’ve served:

  • 2 years of the disqualification period if the disqualification was for more than 2 years but less than 4 years

  • half the disqualification period if the disqualification was for more than 4 years but less than 10 years

  • 5 years of the disqualification period if the disqualification was for 10 years or more

Write to the court that disqualified you with the date of offence, date of conviction and any other supporting information. If the court decides to reduce your disqualification period you can apply to renew your licence.

If the court refuses your request you have to wait 3 months before you can ask again.

Part 7: Changes to your name and address while disqualified

Tell DVLA if you change your name or address while disqualified.

Write with details of your old and new address, name if changed, your driving licence number (if known) and date of birth.

Part 8: Getting your driving licence back after disqualification

If you’ve been disqualified for 56 days or more you need a new licence before driving again.

You can apply to renew your licence up to 56 days before your disqualification ends.

Renewal reminder

You’ll be sent a D27P ‘Driving licence application for renewal after disqualification’ form 56 days before your disqualification ends.

If you have a non-Great Britain licence you’ll get your old licence back on the date your disqualification ends.

Renewing your licence if you’ve got a photocard driving licence

You’ll need to:

  • complete your D27P form

  • enclose the correct payment (this will be shown on the reminder)

  • send your application to DVLA, Swansea, SA99 1AB

If your name has changed you’ll also need to include:

  • a completed D1 form, available from the DVLA form ordering service or selected Post Offices

  • original documents showing your new name

Renewing your licence if you’ve got a paper driving licence

You’ll need to:

  • complete your D27P form

  • send original documents confirming your identity

  • include a passport-type photo

  • enclose the correct payment (this will be shown on the reminder)

  • send all your documents to DVLA, Swansea, SA99 1AB or use the premium checking service at selected Post Offices or DVLA local offices

Renewing without a D27P form

If you don’t get the D27P reminder form, order a D1 ‘application for a driving licence’ form from the DVLA form ordering service or get one from selected Post Offices.

Send the completed form, documentation and fee to DVLA, Swansea, SA99 1AB.

If you need to renew your lorry or bus entitlement order a D2 ‘application for Lorry, Bus or Minibus driving licence’ form from the DVLA form ordering service or get the form from a DVLA local office. You don’t need to complete a D1 form.

When to expect your new driving licence

DVLA aims to deliver your driving licence within 3 weeks of getting your application. Wait at least 3 weeks before contacting DVLA.

It’ll take longer if your medical or personal details have to be checked, eg if you were disqualified for certain alcohol-related offences.
