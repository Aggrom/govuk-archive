Liquidate your limited company, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can choose to liquidate your limited company.

Liquidating a company means its assets are used to pay off its debts - any money left goes to shareholders. After liquidation, your company won’t exist.

There are 2 kinds of voluntary liquidation:

  • creditors’ voluntary liquidation - you choose to liquidate your company because it can’t pay its debts
  • members’ voluntary liquidation - your company can pay its debts but you want to close it

Your company may be forced into compulsory liquidation if it can’t pay its debts.

Part 2: Members' voluntary liquidation

You may choose members’ voluntary liquidation if:

  • you want to retire
  • you want to step down from the family business and nobody else wants to run it
  • the business has run its course

Before you start, you (the director) must publicly declare your company is ‘solvent’ (can pay its debts) by making a ‘members’ declaration of solvency’.

Members’ declaration of solvency

  1. Get form 4.70 ‘Members voluntary winding up declaration of solvency’ from a legal stationer - or in Scotland, get form 4.25 (Scot) from the Accountant in Bankruptcy.

  2. Fill in the declaration - it must be signed by the majority of directors.

  3. Send the declaration to Companies House.

Start voluntary liquidation

You must call a general meeting with shareholders and:

  • pass a resolution for voluntary winding up (which should be sent to Companies House)
  • appoint an authorised insolvency practitioner as a liquidator who will take charge of winding up the company

You must make the declaration of solvency at least 5 weeks before the resolution to wind up the company is passed.

When the liquidator is appointed

They take control of the company. Your responsibilities as a director will change.

Part 3: Creditors' voluntary liquidation

You (the director) can choose a creditors’ voluntary liquidation if your company can’t pay its bills (‘insolvent’).

Shareholders can voluntarily liquidate the company by voting and passing a resolution to stop trading.

Once the resolution has been passed, you must:

  • appoint an authorised insolvency practitioner as liquidator
  • advertise the resolution in the London Gazette within 14 days, or the Edinburgh Gazette in Scotland
  • send a copy of the resolution to Companies House within 15 days

When the liquidator is appointed

They take control of the company. They will let Companies House know they’ve been appointed.

Your responsibilities as a director will change.

Meeting with creditors

You must hold a meeting with the company’s creditors within 14 days of the resolution. They can:

  • question company directors about the business’ failure
  • suggest an alternative liquidator

You must make a statement of affairs using form 4.19 (or form 600 in Scotland) and present it at the meeting. After the meeting you must give the statement to the liquidator who will pass it on to Companies House.

Part 4: The role of the liquidator

The liquidator runs the liquidation process. As soon as the liquidator is appointed, they will take over control of the business from the directors.

They will:

  • settle any legal disputes or outstanding contracts
  • sell off the company’s assets and use any money to pay creditors
  • meet deadlines for paperwork and keeps authorities informed
  • pay liquidation costs and the final VAT bill
  • bring together people owed money (creditors) and hold meetings where necessary
  • decide which creditors should be paid first
  • interview the directors and report on what went wrong in the business
  • get the company removed from the Company Register

In a creditors’ voluntary liquidation, the liquidator acts in the interest of the creditors not the directors.

Find a liquidator

You can find an insolvency practitioner who is licensed to act as a liquidator.

Part 5: What happens to directors

When a liquidator is appointed, if you’re a company director:

  • you no longer have control of the company or anything it owns
  • you can’t act for or on behalf of the company

You must:

  • give the liquidator information about the company they ask for
  • hand over the company’s assets, records and paperwork
  • be interviewed as part of the liquidator’s report for the Insolvency Service on why the business failed

You may be banned from being a director for 2 to 15 years or prosecuted if the liquidator decides your conduct was ‘unfit’.

Reusing company names

If you were a director of a company in liquidation, you’re banned for 5 years from forming, managing, promoting or managing any business with the same or similar name to the company in liquidation.
