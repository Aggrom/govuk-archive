Plan your retirement income, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Increase your retirement income

There are a number of things you can do to make sure you have enough money in retirement.

Join a workplace pension

If your employer offers a pension scheme, it may be worth joining it. Your contributions will increase in value by the time you retire and you don’t pay tax on the money that goes into it.

Automatic enrolment

If you’re not a member of a scheme and you haven’t specifically chosen not to join one, you may get automatically enrolled into a scheme.

Make extra workplace pension contributions

You can pay extra into your workplace pension scheme, if you’re a member of one. Contact the pension scheme to find out how, or ask your employer.

Make extra National Insurance contributions

If you want to increase the amount of State Pension you’ll get, you can make extra voluntary National Insurance contributions.

Find a lost pension

If you have any old pensions and you think the company might not be able to contact you, you can find a lost pension.

Work longer

You don’t have to retire at a particular age, unless there’s a good reason that the employer can justify.

Defer your State Pension

You can choose not to claim your State Pension when you reach State Pension age but take it later (‘deferring your State Pension’). This means you’ll either get a lump sum or an increased weekly amount when you do claim it.

Pay into a personal pension

This is a voluntary scheme you pay into to save for retirement, but it also allows you to pay less tax. They’re often used by self-employed people instead of a workplace pension.

For help and advice with personal pensions, contact the Pensions Advisory Service.

Other ways to save

There are also other ways to save including savings accounts and Individual Savings Accounts (‘ISAs’).

You can read about ways to save and invest on the Money Advice Service website.

Part 2: State Pension and Pension Credit

Basic State Pension

If you pay National Insurance you’ll build up entitlement to the basic State Pension. You also build up entitlement for years when you were:

  • looking after a child under 12
  • a foster carer
  • caring for at least 20 hours per week for a disabled person (this is called ‘Carer’s Credit’)
  • getting Employment and Support Allowance, Jobseeker’s Allowance, Incapacity Benefit or Carer’s Allowance

The current full rate of basic State Pension is £107.45. Work out how much State Pension you’re going to get.

Additional State Pension

You can get Additional State Pension on top of your basic State Pension if you’re:

  • looking after children under 12 years old and claiming Child Benefit
  • caring for a sick or disabled person for more than 20 hours a week and claiming Carer’s Credit
  • a registered foster carer and claiming Carer’s Credit
  • getting illness or disability benefits

Pension Credit

If you’re on a low income, Pension Credit is extra money for older people to bring your weekly income up to a minimum amount.

Part 3: Get financial advice

You can search for a financial adviser on the Personal Finance Society website.

All financial advisers must be registered with the Financial Services Authority (FSA). You can search the FSA register to check if a financial advice company is registered.
