Benefits and prison, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Benefit payments and entitlement may change or stop if you, your partner or one of your children is:

  • sent to prison or a young offenders’ institution
  • in custody awaiting trial (on remand)

You can’t claim State Pension while you’re in prison.

If you’re in prison or on remand

You can get help from a benefits advisor to suspend or close down benefits you’re no longer able to claim if you go to prison or on remand.

If your partner or child is in prison or on remand

If your partner or child has gone to prison or is on remand, you must tell whoever pays your benefits and find out:

  • if your benefit claims will be affected
  • if you can claim other benefits

If your partner has gone to prison you may still be entitled to benefits, as long as you satisfy the benefit entitlement conditions in your own right.

Part 2: Benefits that stop or are suspended

If you go to prison, your entitlement to benefit stops apart from:

  • Housing Benefit for shorter sentences and help with council tax if you’re eligible
  • Industrial Injuries Disabled Benefit, which is suspended

You may be able to continue claiming other benefits if your partner or child goes to prison.

Benefits while on remand

If you’re on remand, you can’t claim:

  • Job Seeker’s Allowance
  • Income Support - apart from help with housing costs
  • Pension Credit - apart from help with housing costs
  • Working Tax Credit - although your partner may be able to claim for an absent partner
  • Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit
  • Incapacity Benefit
  • Employment and Support Allowance
  • Attendance Allowance
  • Disability Living Allowance

You won’t be entitled to Statutory Sick Pay or Statutory Maternity Pay from your employer.

Benefit arrears

If you are owed any benefit arrears at the time you’re sent to prison or on remand, you can make a request in writing for these to be paid to someone else.

Benefit arrears if you’re not convicted

If you’re not convicted, you can get benefit arrears for:

  • Contributory Employment and Support Allowance
  • Incapacity Benefit

Benefit arrears if you’re convicted

If you’re sent to prison and claim Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit, you may be entitled to up to a year’s arrears whenever you’re released.

Claiming benefits on release

If you’re entitled to benefits, you can put in new claims as soon as you leave prison. You may also be able to get other financial and practical support.

You can’t claim Job Seeker’s Allowance if you’re released on temporary licence (ROTL).

Part 3: Housing Benefit

You may be able continue getting Housing Benefit or make a claim for the first time if you go to prison or on remand.

When you won’t be able to claim

You won’t be entitled to claim Housing Benefit if:

  • you’re likely to be on remand for more than 52 weeks
  • you’re likely to be in prison for more than 13 weeks (including any time on remand)
  • you’re not intending to return home on release
  • you’re claiming as a couple and you’ve split up
  • the property is going to be rented out

On remand

If you’re single

You can claim Housing Benefit payments for up to 52 weeks while you’re on remand, if you’re likely to return home in a year or less.

If you’re in a couple

You can claim joint Housing Benefit for up to 52 weeks while one of you is on remand, if it’s likely to be for a year or less.

If your child is on remand

Your Housing Benefit payments can continue for up to 52 weeks if your child’s on remand, if they’re likely to be away for a year or less.

In prison

If you’re single

If you’re single and go to prison and are likely to return home in 13 weeks or less - including any time on remand - you can claim Housing Benefit for up to 13 weeks.

If you’re in a couple

If one of you has gone to prison and is likely to return home in 13 weeks or less - including any time on remand - you can claim joint Housing Benefit for up to 13 weeks.

If your partner’s been the one claiming Housing Benefit and goes to prison, you may be able to claim it instead. You may need to have your name added to the tenancy agreement, if it’s not there already.

If your child is in prison

If your child goes to prison, you’ll need to contact your local council to see if your Housing Benefit entitlement will change. For example, if you rent from a private landlord, the amount of benefit paid is limited, depending on who’s living with you.

Part 4: Council Tax Benefit

If you’re in prison or on remand you won’t count as an adult living in a property for Council Tax.

If you’re single

If you’re single and in prison or on remand and there’s no one living in your home, you can apply for it to be exempt from Council Tax.

Your home won’t be exempt if you’re in prison for not paying Council Tax or a fine for not paying it.

If you’re in a couple

On remand

If your partner’s on remand and is expected home in a year or less, you can claim or continue to claim joint Council Tax Benefit for up to 52 weeks.

In prison

If your partner’s expected to be in prison for 13 weeks or less – including any time on remand, you can claim or continue to claim joint Council Tax Benefit for up to 13 weeks.

Making a new claim

If your partner’s going to be absent for more than 13 weeks and you’re the only adult in the property, you’ll get a 25% discount off your Council Tax bill. You may also be able to make a single claim for Council Tax Benefit.

Part 5: Tax Credits

Working Tax Credit

If you’re single

Your Working Tax Credit will stop if you’re single and go on remand or are sent to prison.

If you’re in a couple

If you’re in a couple and your partner goes to prison for a year or less, you can continue claiming Working Tax Credit if you’re working at least 16 hours a week.

You continue to get extra tax credits because there’s a second adult in your household.

If your partner is going to prison for a year or more and you’re working, you may want put in a single claim for Working Tax Credit.

Work done inside or outside a prison while you’re serving a sentence or on remand won’t count as work for Working Tax Credit.

Any pay from this work will be counted as income when working out tax credit payments.

Child Tax Credit

If you’re single

Your Child Tax Credit will stop if you’re single with children and go to prison unless your child is with you in prison.

The person looking after your child or children may be able to claim Child Tax Credit instead.

If your child goes to prison or on remand for more than 4 months, you won’t get Child Tax Credit for them until they return home.

If you’re in a couple

Your Child Tax Credit may continue if you’re in a couple and one of you goes on remand or to prison.

Part 6: Child Benefit

You can continue to claim Child Benefit when you’re in prison if:

  • your child is with you in prison
  • the child you’re claiming for for is living with someone else and you pay an equivalent sum to them

You can also ask to transfer the Child Benefit payment to someone else.

If your child is being cared for by the local authority, your Child Benefit payments will stop after 8 weeks.

If your child goes to prison or is on remand

If your child goes to prison or is on remand, your Child Benefit payments will stop after 8 weeks. If they’re cleared of the offence, you will get arrears.

Looking after someone else’s child

If you look after your partner’s child or someone else’s child while they’re in prison, you may be able to claim Child Benefit and Guardian’s Allowance.

You may also be able to to claim Child Tax Credit.

Part 7: Support for Mortgage Interest

You can’t get Support for Mortgage Interest (SMI) if you’re serving a prison sentence but your partner might be able to claim instead.

Your partner’s name may not have to be on the mortgage to be able to claim.

Claiming SMI while on remand

If you’re single and on remand, you may be able to continue getting SMI if you meet the eligibility conditions.

If you’re part of a couple and on remand, you can’t claim SMI or Income Support, but your partner can claim benefit and housing costs.

If you’re on remand, you can’t make a new claim.

How SMI is paid

If you’re on remand, SMI can only be paid through Pension Credit or Income Support.

You’ll need to apply for one of these benefits if you’ve been getting it paid through Income-based Job Seeker’s Allowance or Employment and Support allowance.

You’ll only be able to get the housing costs part of Pension Credit or Income Support.

You won’t get help for the first 13 weeks if you apply for help with interest payments through Income Support.
