Vehicle approval, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Type approval is a process of making sure that vehicles meet certain safety and environmental standards.

There are different ways to approve a vehicle:

  • Individual Vehicle Approval - if you’re making or importing a single vehicle or a very small number of vehicles
  • Single Vehicle Approval - if you’re making or importing a single special purpose passenger vehicle
  • Motorcycle Single Vehicle Approval - if you’re making or importing a single 2, 3 or smaller 4-wheeled vehicle
  • Mutual Recognition scheme - if you’re importing a left-hand-drive vehicle that’s been approved and registered in the European Union

If you manufacture or officially import vehicles in modest numbers, the National Small Series Type Approval (NSSTA) scheme may be a simpler way of getting approval.

Help with type approval enquiries

Contact the Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA) if you need help or information about:

  • Individual Vehicle Approval (IVA)
  • Single Vehicle Approval (SVA)
  • Motorcycle Single Vehicle Approval (MSVA)

Part 2: When you need type approval

Most motor vehicles and trailers used on the road need type approval. Normally, the manufacturer or importer will take care of this.

However, your vehicle might not have type approval, and you might need to apply for it. Examples of when this might happen include if you’ve:

  • built or rebuilt a vehicle
  • radically altered a vehicle
  • reconstructed a classic vehicle
  • imported a vehicle

You don’t need type approval for:

  • cars and light goods vehicles over 10 years old
  • heavy goods vehicles over 25 years old

If your vehicle needs type approval, you must send in proof of this when you apply to register it. If you don’t, the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA) won’t register the vehicle.

If your vehicle fails its approval tests, it can’t be registered.

Part 3: Individual Vehicle Approval

Who can use the scheme?

You can use the Individual Vehicle Approval (IVA) scheme if you’re making or importing a single vehicle or a very small number of vehicles in the following categories:

  • passenger cars
  • light goods vehicles (under 3,500kg)
  • buses and coaches
  • medium to heavy sized goods vehicles (more than 3,500kg)
  • trailers
  • special purpose vehicles, like vehicles specially designed to hold a wheelchair

You can’t use the normal ‘compulsory’ IVA if your vehicle has been registered before in the UK - you’ll need to use the voluntary version of the scheme instead.

Vehicle identification number

Your vehicle needs a vehicle identification number (VIN) before having an IVA inspection. If it doesn’t have one, apply to your Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA) local office.

Basic and normal IVA

There are 2 levels of IVA inspection: basic and normal.

Basic IVA

A basic IVA involves a visual inspection and other tests to make sure the vehicle meets the necessary standards. You won’t normally need to provide any documentary evidence.

You can apply if you have a passenger car or a light goods vehicle in one of these categories:

  • left-hand drive vehicles
  • personal imports
  • amateur built vehicles (kit cars)
  • rebuilt vehicles
  • very low volume production vehicles
  • ambulances
  • motor caravans
  • hearses
  • armoured vehicles
  • a vehicle manufactured using parts of a registered vehicle

For help understanding these categories, download VOSA’s guide to the IVA (PDF, 1.84MB) - annex 1 and 2.

Normal IVA

If you don’t meet the criteria for a basic IVA, you’ll have to apply for a normal IVA.

This involves a more detailed inspection. Vehicles have to meet extra standards and you’ll have to provide documentary evidence of this.

How to show your vehicle meets IVA standards

There are several ways to prove your vehicle meets the IVA standards (is ‘compliant’). See part 5 of VOSA’s guide to the IVA, below, for full details.

You can also download VOSA’s IVA manuals from Business Link. These tell you what technical specifications your vehicle must meet.

Download VOSA’s guide to the IVA (PDF, 1.84MB)

Model reports

One way of proving your vehicle is compliant is by showing it’s the same specification as another vehicle (a ‘master vehicle’) that’s been proved compliant. You do this using a model report.

If a model report has already been produced for your exact model of vehicle, you may be able to use it for a fee. VOSA has a list of model reports and their owners.

Otherwise, you’ll need to pay for your own laboratory tests to be carried out.

Download ‘UK authorised testing laboratories’ (PDF, 21KB)

For more information on model reports, see part 11 of VOSA’s guide to the IVA (PDF, 1.84MB).

How to apply

You need to choose the correct application form depending on your type of vehicle. Send it to the address on the form.

Choosing a test station

Your vehicle will be inspected by VOSA at one of their approved test stations or Privately Owned Test Facilities (POTFs). You should say which one you want to use on your application form.

Download list of IVA test stations and POTFs (PDF, 274KB)

Cost of the scheme

You have to pay a fee for the Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA) to inspect your vehicle.

What happens next

VOSA will inspect your vehicle at the test location you’ve chosen and will issue an Individual Approval Certificate (IAC) if it passes. You’ll need to use this certificate when you register your vehicle.

Appeal against a refusal

If your vehicle doesn’t pass its inspection and you’re not satisfied with the decision, you can appeal and have a re-examination carried out by an independent inspector.

You must make your appeal within 14 days of the decision and you’ll have to pay a fee. This will be refunded, either partially or fully, if your appeal is successful.

You mustn’t modify the vehicle before the appeal inspection.

You can appeal by downloading and filling in the IVA notice of appeal.

Download IVA notice of appeal (DOC, 139KB)

For more information on appeals, see part 8 of VOSA’s guide to the IVA, below.

Part 6: Single Vehicle Approval

Who can use the scheme?

The Individual Vehicle Approval (IVA) scheme is replacing the Single Vehicle Approval (SVA) scheme. From now on, you can use the IVA scheme for any type of vehicle needing type approval except for special purpose light goods vehicles.

Until 29 October 2014, you can still use the SVA scheme for special purpose light goods vehicles not more than 3,500kg gross vehicle weight that need first licensing and registration in the UK and are less than 10 years old.

Part 7: Motorcycle Single Vehicle Approval

When you must use the scheme

To register your motorcycle, 3-wheeled or light 4-wheeled vehicle, it must:

  • comply with European Community Whole Vehicle Type Approval (ECWVTA)
  • have a Certificate of Conformity (CoC) to prove this

If it doesn’t have a Certificate of Conformity, it must be inspected under the Motorcycle Single Vehicle Approval (MSVA) scheme.

You must also use the MSVA scheme if your vehicle has been radically altered or built using a mixture of parts from previously registered vehicles. For example:

  • amateur built vehicles
  • rebuilt vehicles
  • vehicles converted to a different wheelplan

Eligibility for MSVA

You can use the MSVA scheme for vehicles that:

  • are under 10 years old
  • haven’t been registered before in the UK
  • don’t have ECWVTA

The following types of vehicle are eligible:

  • 2-wheeled mopeds
  • 3-wheeled mopeds
  • light quadricycles
  • solo motorcycles
  • motorcycle combinations
  • motor tricycles
  • heavy quadricycles

These vehicle types are defined in appendix 1 of the MSVA guide, produced by the Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA).

Download VOSA’s guide to the MSVA (DOC, 343KB)

Partial MSVA

If you have a Certificate of Conformity (CoC), it will say which side of the road and which speedometer units your vehicle is equipped for.

If your vehicle isn’t equipped for use in Great Britain, you must modify it. It will then need a Partial MSVA to check the following items:

  • headlamp dipped beam pattern - this checks the headlamp is suitable for driving on the left
  • speedometer - the speed must be shown in miles per hour (mph) or dual mph and kilometres per hour
  • mirror location - on mopeds fitted with a single mirror, the mirror must be fitted to offside (right) of the vehicle

Part 8: Voluntary approval

If your vehicle hasn’t been registered before in the UK, use the normal ‘compulsory’ Individual Vehicle Approval (IVA) or Single Vehicle Approval (SVA) schemes.

If your vehicle is already registered and you need to prove its compliance, use the voluntary version of these schemes.

Example: A taxi licensing authority may need evidence of compliance if your vehicle has been modified since it was originally registered.

Choosing which voluntary scheme to use

Up until 29 April 2012, you can use the voluntary SVA for special purpose vehicles (eg wheelchair-accessible vehicles). After that date, and for any other vehicle, you need to use the voluntary IVA.

You’ll need to choose whether to use the standard/enhanced or basic/normal level. The criteria is exactly the same as the compulsory version of the schemes.

How the voluntary scheme works

In general, the voluntary and compulsory versions of the IVA scheme and the SVA scheme are exactly the same.

The only differences are:

  • you can use the voluntary scheme if your vehicle has already been registered in the UK
  • you’ll have to pay VAT on the fee
  • for the voluntary SVA scheme, you’ll have to fill out a different application form

Cost of the schemes

You have to pay a fee for the Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA) to inspect your vehicle. The voluntary versions of the schemes cost the same, except that VAT is payable.

Part 9: Inspection fees

You have to pay a fee for the Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA) to inspect your vehicle.

The cost depends on:

  • whether you’re using the compulsory (statutory) or voluntary version of the scheme
  • the type of inspection (basic/normal or standard/enhanced)
  • the category of vehicle
  • the class of vehicle
