Non-native fish: regulations and controls

Why the UK protects native fish, and the type of controls needed if you keep or release non-native species of fish to inland water.

Introduction

Regulations and controls exist to protect native wildlife species from threats caused by non-native species being introduced. Non-native fish can carry and spread diseases to which native species have no defence. They can also prey on native fish and other animals, and upset the natural ecological balance.

There are special legal controls in the UK to protect native fish populations from damage caused when non-native varieties are introduced into their habitats. These controls aim to prevent both deliberate and accidental introductions of non-native species by ensuring that only people covered by an appropriate licence can keep or introduce them.

This guide explains the regulations and licensing requirements that apply to non-native fish. It covers the type of licence you need depending on the nature of your business and the species of fish, and also explains the application process.

Controls on non-native fish species

Non-native fish species can have serious effects on native species and ecologies if introduced - either deliberately or accidentally. In some cases they prey on native fish and other animals and can upset the natural ecological balance. They may also introduce harmful diseases and parasites that native species have little or no defence against. You can find out why non-native species are a threat to fish stocks on the Environment Agency website.

Therefore, legislation exists to protect native fish species from the harm caused by unauthorised releases of non-native species.

Wildlife and Countryside Act

The Wildlife and Countryside Act (WCA) 1981 makes it an offence for anyone without a licence to release a non-native animal into the wild in Great Britain, or to allow one to escape. For example, it would be an offence under the act to introduce non-native fish into a pond from which they could potentially escape into the wild.

Specific controls on fish

Additional legislation exists to provide further protection for native fish species. It is an offence for anyone without a licence to keep or introduce specific named species of non-native fish into any water in England and Wales. ‘Any water’ includes tanks, pools and ponds.

Download a list of controlled non-native fish species from the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website (PDF, 181KB).

Both Scotland and Northern Ireland have separate legislation designed to protect wildlife from damage caused by non-native species.

There are also special controls to protect native species of crayfish and lobster - see the guide on non-native crayfish and lobster deposits.

Licences for keeping and introducing non-native fish

Under legislation designed to protect native fish and other wildlife, you will normally need a licence to:

  • keep controlled non-native species of fish or introduce them into an inland water - because it is granted under the Import of Live Fish (England and Wales) Act (ILFA), this is known as an ILFA licence
  • introduce all non-native fish into the wild - because it is granted under the WCA, this is known as a WCA licence

ILFA licence

The ILFA licensing system applies to any non-native fish species listed under the legislation. You can download a list of controlled non-native fish species from the Defra website (PDF, 19KB).

All those wishing to keep the listed species, including fish farmers, fish dealers, fishery owners, researchers, public display aquariums, wholesalers, retailers and hobbyists require an individual licence to do so.

If you are planning to introduce non-native fish that are not on the controlled list, you don’t need an ILFA licence. But you do need a WCA licence.

WCA licence

The WCA licence is required to introduce all non-native species of fish into the wild. If you need to obtain an ILFA licence to introduce a controlled species then that licence will also provide WCA approval. If the species is not on the controlled list, you only need a WCA licence.

‘The wild’ includes fisheries and any other waters where you couldn’t easily recover all examples of an introduced non-native species. Any water from which the fish could escape into an open watercourse, or waters connected to a continuous body of water, is also considered ‘wild’. In general it’s only small, fully enclosed sites from which fish can easily be recovered that are considered not to be the wild.

Section 30 consent

‘Section 30’ consent is written consent that you must get from the Environment Agency before you may introduce any fish - including non-native species - into inland waters other than fish farms. See fish movements into inland waters in the guide on moving fish and shellfish.

Which non-native fish licences does your business need

The licences that you need in order to keep or introduce non-native fish depend on:

  • the nature of your business and/or the waters you’re introducing the fish to
  • whether the fish are on the list of controlled non-native species - download a list of controlled non-native fish species from the Defra website (PDF, 19KB)

Licences to keep and introduce non-native fish

The licences that you may need to keep and introduce non-native fish may include:

  • the ILFA (Import of Live Fish Act) licence
  • the WCA licence
  • ‘Section 30’ consent

Introducing and keeping non-native species in the wild, including fisheries

If you introduce controlled non-native fish species into the wild you will need all of the following licences:

  • an individual ILFA licence
  • a WCA licence
  • a Section 30 consent

If the species you introduce is not on the controlled list, you will need a WCA licence but not an ILFA licence. Section 30 consent is required for movements of all fish into a river or stillwater fishery.

‘The wild’ includes fisheries and any other waters where all examples of an introduced non-native species could not easily be recovered. Any water from which the fish could escape into an open watercourse, or waters connected to a continuous body of water, is also considered ‘wild’.

Fish farms

Authorised commercial fish farms require the following licences to introduce and keep controlled species of non-native fish to their waters:

  • an individual ILFA licence
  • a WCA licence, if the waters the fish are held in are considered to be wild

If the species you introduce are not on the controlled list, you don’t need an ILFA licence. But if your waters are considered to be wild, you do need a WCA licence.

Fish farms don’t need Section 30 consent.

Ornamental fish wholesalers and retailers

If you sell controlled non-native fish species on a wholesale or retail basis you require an individual ILFA licence. The general licensing provisions do not apply in the case of wholesalers and retailers.

Garden ponds and ornamental aquaria

General licensing arrangements apply in the following situations, meaning that you don’t need an individual ILFA licence if you keep:

  • grass carp, sturgeon/sterlet and ameiurid (ictalurid) catfish in a garden pond or indoor aquarium
  • red shiners and fathead minnows (roseyreds) in an indoor aquarium

A garden pond means a self-contained and isolated body of water covering no more than one acre on private residential premises. There must be no risk that the fish could escape into the wild, and it must not be used for fishing or any commercial purpose related to fish rearing or dealing.

To keep controlled non-native fish in any other situation, including a pond or aquarium in a wholesale or retail outlet, you will need an individual ILFA licence.

Applying for non-native fish licences

You must apply for an ILFA licence and/or a WCA licence before you get your fish. To apply, you need to use form ILFA1 - the same application form covers both licences where appropriate. Download form ILFA1 from the Defra website (PDF, 88KB).

You will need to provide various details on the form, including:

  • information about yourself
  • what species the fish are
  • where the fish will come from
  • where the fish will be kept, and key details about the location

You will only be issued with a licence if the species is considered to be appropriate, and if security at your site is sufficient.

How long will the licensing process take?

How long it takes for your licence to be processed depends on your business type. If you’re a retailer, hobbyist or wholesaler applying for a licence to trade in ornamental non-native fish then your application will be processed within the timescales set by the Fish Health Inspectorate (FHI). For guidance on how long the FHI will take to process your application, download the FHI citizen’s charter from the eFishBusiness website (PDF, 51KB).

However other applications can take up to eight weeks before a licence is issued, particularly if it’s necessary to consult with organisations such as:

  • the Environment Agency
  • Natural England or the Countryside Council for Wales
  • the Centre for Environment, Fisheries and Aquaculture Science

Offences and penalties

Under the Prohibition of Keeping or Release of Live Fish (Specified Species) Order 1998, you’re committing an offence if you hold, keep or release species that are on the ILFA list without holding an ILFA licence. It’s also an offence if you fail to meet the conditions of your ILFA licence, including supplying an ILFA listed species to someone who doesn’t have an appropriate ILFA licence.

Your licence could be revoked if you fail to comply with licence conditions, and you could be prosecuted and face a fine of up to £2,500. Depending on the circumstances, illegally stocked fish may be removed and destroyed.

Under the WCA 1981, you’re committing an offence if you:

  • deliberately release any non-native fish into the wild
  • allow non-native fish to escape into the wild

You face a fine if you’re found guilty of this offence.

Applying for Section 30 consent

You need to get Section 30 consent from the Environment Agency before you move any fish into a river or stillwater fishery. See fish movements into inland waters in the guide on moving fish and shellfish.
