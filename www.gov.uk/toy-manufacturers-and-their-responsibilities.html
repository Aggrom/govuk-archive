Toy manufacturers and their responsibilities

How to produce and label toys for children in order to meet safety and warning regulations.

Introduction

As a manufacturer of toys intended for children, you must comply with the essential requirements of the Toys (Safety) Regulations 1995. These bring the European Toy Safety Directive 88/378 into UK law.

The main requirements are that toys must:

  • satisfy safety requirements (termed the ‘essential safety requirements’)
  • bear the CE marking
  • bear the required name and address details
  • be accompanied by warnings where necessary

In addition, information must be maintained for inspection by enforcement authorities.

This guide outlines your legal responsibilities as a manufacturer.

On 19 August 2011, regulations changed in this area. For more information, see the regulation update on Additional EU safety requirements for new toys (UK) at the end of this guidance.

Toy manufacture regulations

The Toys (Safety) Regulations 1995 define a toy as “any product or material designed or clearly intended for use in play by children of less than 14 years of age, but excluding those products specified in Schedule 3 [of the regulations]”.

You can find a list of products not regarded as toys under the regulations in Annex B of the toy safety guidelines on the Department for Business, Innovation and Skills (BIS) website (PDF, 100KB).

The regulations apply to anyone supplying toys in the course of any business, including:

  • manufacturers
  • importers
  • retailers
  • hirers and other suppliers of new and second-hand toys

Toys distributed free of charge in the course of business are also covered. In terms of regulation enforcement, there is no difference between a manufacturer and a supplier.

A revised European directive substantially amends the existing regulations across virtually all safety aspects and extends the definition to cover more products.

You can download the text of the new Directive 2009/48/EC from the EUR-Lex website (PDF, 995KB).

Charities and toy sales

Many charities are subject to the regulations because they have characteristics similar to those of businesses, such as:

  • money generating
  • a degree of continuity
  • regular business hours

People organising events such as jumble sales and car boot sales - which are held at infrequent and irregular intervals - are unlikely to be considered as acting in the course of business - but traders invited to sell toys there would not be exempt.

Individuals producing toys on an occasional basis to give to charities to sell are also exempt.

The person who first places the toy on the market

As a manufacturer, you, or your authorised representative supplying in the UK, must ensure that toys satisfy safety requirements and must take responsibility for the declaration the CE marking represents.

Other suppliers

Other persons in the supply chain - eg importers who import toys from another member state, wholesalers, retailers - must supply only safe toys that bear the CE marking and other specified details.

Safety standards under the Toys (Safety) Regulations

The essential safety requirements of the Toys (Safety) Regulations 1995 state that the toy user, and anyone else, must be protected against risks of injury to health when toys are used reasonably, bearing in mind the normal behaviour of children.

There are two methods by which you or your authorised representative can ensure that toys satisfy the essential safety requirements applicable to them:

  • issuing a Declaration of Conformity
  • EC type-examination

Declaration of Conformity

You certify you have manufactured in conformity with the relevant national standards - British Standard BS EN 71. A toy manufactured in accordance with BS EN 71 shall be deemed to meet the essential safety requirements if the standards cover all the elements of the essential safety requirements applicable to that particular toy.

Where this method is used, CE marking is affixed as a declaration by you that - amongst other things - the toy bearing it satisfies the essential safety requirements and has been manufactured in accordance with the relevant national standards. For more information, see the section in this guide on CE marking under the Toys (Safety) Regulations.

Although the regulations do not specify that you must carry out tests to support self certification, information supporting a CE marking declaration must be maintained, and the conformity assessment part of the required information will - in many cases - mean using the services of an independent test laboratory.

European Community type-examination certificate

You manufacture in accordance with a model that has been European Community (EC) type examined. In this case, you must submit a sample of the toy to a Notified Body for EC type examination. This can be a Notified Body in any member state. The Notified Body will perform the tests and examinations necessary to decide whether or not the toy satisfies the essential safety requirements and - if it does - the body will issue an EC type-examination certificate.

You can download a list of UK Notified Bodies from the BIS website (PDF, 40KB).

You can also search for EU notified bodies on the Europa website.

Once an EC type-examination certificate has been issued, you must ensure that your production toys comply with the sample. You can CE mark each toy with a self declaration that it has been manufactured in accordance with a specimen that is the subject of an EC type-examination certificate.

It is also recommended that manufacturers and importers undertake a hazard assessment on toys at the prototype stage to decide whether there are any aspects of safety that are outside the scope of the standards.

CE marking under the Toys (Safety) Regulations

CE marking is a visible declaration by you or your authorised representative that the toy satisfies all the provisions of the Toys (Safety) Regulations 1995. Toys bearing the mark will be understood to meet the requirements and therefore entitled to free circulation throughout the European Economic Area (EEA), provided that they do in fact satisfy those requirements.

CE marking should be affixed to the toy or - where not possible - to the:

  • packaging
  • instruction sheet
  • guarantee certificate

You may decide for practical reasons to affix it to both the product and its packaging. The mark must be visible, easily legible and in an indelible form.

By affixing CE marking to your product, you are making a statement that in your view the toy meets the requirements of all relevant directives. It does not mean that the toy cannot be challenged by an enforcement authority if they have reasonable grounds for suspecting an infringement of the Regulations.

You should also note that CE marking is not a European safety mark or quality symbol intended for consumers. Its purpose is to indicate to enforcement authorities that the toy is intended for sale in the EEA.

CE marking also confirms that your product conforms to other directives where they apply.

For more information see the guide on CE marking.

Essential information which should accompany toys

As a manufacturer, it is your responsibility to ensure that all toys have warnings and information about any precautions that must be taken during their use. There are specific requirements for certain types of toys.

Other relevant markings for toys and their packaging should also include:

  • trade marking
  • name and address details
  • abbreviation of details

Toys not intended for children under 36 months

Toys that might be dangerous for children under 36 months of age must have a warning, together with a brief indication of the specific risks. This provision does not apply to toys that are manifestly unsuitable for children under 36 months.

Slides, suspended swings and rings, trapezes, ropes and similar toys attached to a crossbeam

These toys must be accompanied by instructions drawing attention to the need to carry out the checks and maintenance, and the consequences if they are not carried out. Instructions must also be given about the correct assembly of the toy.

Functional toys

A functional toy is one that is used in the same way as - and is often a scale model of - an appliance or installation intended for adults.

Functional toys or their packaging must have the marking - “Warning: to be used under the direct supervision of an adult”. They should also have instructions, precautions and warnings about the hazards the toy could expose a child to. The hazards must be specified.

Chemical toys

The following are regarded as chemical toys:

  • chemistry sets
  • plastic embedding sets
  • miniature workshops for ceramics enamelling
  • photography and similar toys

These toys should include warnings about the dangerous nature of these substances or preparations and give details of specific precautions to be taken. The type of first aid to be given in the event of serious accidents should be mentioned. It should also be stated that the toys must be kept out of reach of very young children.

Skates and skateboards for children

These toys must bear the marking - “Warning: protective equipment should be worn”. Instructions must have a reminder that the toy must be used with caution and some indication should be given as to recommended protective equipment.

Toys intended for use in water

Toys intended for use in water must have the following warning - “Warning! Only to be used in water in which the child is within its depth and under supervision”.

Enforcement of the Toys (Safety) Regulations

Trading standards departments of local authorities in England, Scotland and Wales, and environmental health departments of district councils in Northern Ireland, are responsible for day-to-day enforcement of the Toys (Safety) Regulations.

Where the required information cannot be produced, enforcement authorities have the power to require toys to be tested at the supplier’s own expense.

It is an offence for a manufacturer, authorised representative or importer to supply toys:

  • which do not satisfy the essential safety requirements - this includes not jeopardising the safety or health of users or third parties when used as intended or in a foreseeable way, bearing in mind the normal behaviour of children
  • which do not carry the CE marking
  • for which the relevant information does not exist within the UK or elsewhere in the EC

It is an offence for a supplier other than those referred to above to supply toys which:

  • would put at risk the safety or health of users or third parties when used as intended or in a foreseeable way, bearing in mind the normal behaviour of children
  • do not bear the CE marking or the name and address details of the person who first placed them on the EC market

Penalties

Offences may result in fines of up to £5,000, or a maximum prison term of six months, or both. Where a supplier does not comply with a request to have toys tested within a reasonable time the penalties are a term of imprisonment up to three months or a fine of up to £5,000.

Safeguard procedure

If your product fails to meet safety requirements, it will either be removed from the market or prevented from being sold in the first place.

If this happens to your product within the UK, the European Commission will be notified of any action taken against your product when the non-compliance relates to safety and the toy carries the CE marking. If the Commission is satisfied that the action is justified, it will send details of the case to other member states so that they can consider taking similar action.

Additional EU safety requirements for new toys (UK)

Note: This is our original regulation update text, published on 17 August 2011. Unlike the rest of our site, this archived material is not updated if the law changes.

Relevant to: manufacturers, importers, retailers and other suppliers of toys intended for sale anywhere in the European Community - ie, anyone supplying toys in the course of any business. The rules also apply to businesses that distribute toys free of charge in their products or on their premises - eg breakfast cereal manufacturers or high street fast food outlets. Toys are defined as any product or material designed or intended whether or not exclusively for use in play by children under 14 years of age.

From 19 August 2011, toys placed on the EU market must be traceable to their manufacturer and designed and manufactured to satisfy the essential safety requirements of the new Toy Safety Directive (TSD). TSD 2009 extends existing rules, including:

  • new specific obligations for businesses involved in the supply of toys
  • choking and suffocation risks to cover any toy that a child of any age could put into their mouth (your risk assessment must consider both internal and external airway obstruction)
  • as well as testing products for compliance against standards, manufacturers must also carry out a safety assessment and make it available to Trading Standards Officers when requested
  • warning markings must also display minimum/maximum age for users at the point of sale and specific warnings on age or ability (if relevant, you must display minimum/maximum user weight and whether the toys require adult supervision)
  • if you market toys in food, you must enclose them in a package that does not present a choking hazard and that separates the toy from the food items
  • you are banned from placing on the market any toy firmly attached to a food product in such a way that the toy can only be reached by eating the food
  • you will have to show the CE symbol on a toy’s packaging if the marking on the toy itself is not visible through the packaging

Transitional periods apply to toys:

  • complying with Directive 88/378/EEC and placed on the market before 19 August 2011
  • that meet the requirements set out in Part 3 of Annex II to Directive 88/378/EEC and were placed on the market before 20 July 2013
