Flat panel display manufacturing: support and funding

The types of flat panel display, growing markets for this technology and the funding and support available.

Introduction

The term flat panel displays (FPDs) covers a growing number of technologies which manufacture video display screens used in everything from mobile phones and television screens to laptop computers. Their lightweight, thinner features use less power than previous technology and, as a result, are in great demand.

With the global market for display technologies developing rapidly, this is an exciting area with significant future potential and growth opportunities.

This guide explains the different types of FPDs and highlights growing markets - such as environmentally friendly production, and research and innovation. It also explores the funding and support available to your business if you are involved in this industry.

Manufacturing flat panel displays

UK technology is behind every FPD manufactured around the world. Materials sourced here and home-based process innovation has helped in every major milestone development of the industry.

The UK is well placed as a global leader in all aspects of flexible display technologies, including:

  • substrates
  • solution processable electro-optic effect materials
  • organic semiconductors and transparent conductive polymers and metals
  • new device architectures
  • material delivery
  • post-printing processing equipment

The first commercial FPDs were liquid crystal displays (LCDs), used in devices such as calculators and wristwatches. However, the market soon demanded:

  • larger display sizes
  • higher information density
  • lower unit cost

Over the last two decades, new materials and automated production processes have produced rapid advances in FPD technologies - with higher resolutions, greyscales and full motion video colour displays.

There are already strong, long-term relationships between the UK’s innovation base and many of the world’s major manufacturers. New relationships will inevitably arise as new countries seek to develop their own manufacturing capabilities.

Flexible displays

The development of flexible substrates - ie flexible surfaces - could lead to a major change in where future production is located. Flexible displays are currently being developed at businesses and institutions that use additive printing processes, or a combination of additive printing and coating/patterning, to deposit the various layers required to make the display device. These innovative processes will suit many types of production and significantly reduce production costs.

Entry cost for manufacturing more complex displays for very high volume applications - such as electronic paper - will be higher, but still considerably less than for a similar size of glass substrate manufacturing.

Different types of flat panel displays

There are many different types of FPDs - to suit a range of different uses.

LCD

LCDs are the established technology for a broad range of uses, including desktop PC monitors. Current production techniques enable screen sizes of up to 52” and greater to be economically produced. LCDs take up less space, weigh less and use less power than cathode ray tubes offering an equivalent screen size.

Plasma display panel (PDP)

More suitable for large area viewing, the technology of PDPs means that their visual displays are comparable to cathode ray tubes, but can be built much thinner and flatter. However, plasma technology cannot achieve small, high-resolution displays - so PDPs are not suitable for portable and handheld devices.

Light emitting diode (LED) display

LED displays are commonly used for large, outdoor screens and billboards. Each pixel is formed using a cluster of red, green and blue LEDs. You can also get single colour versions - for example, those often used nowadays as destination signs on public transport.

Organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs)

OLEDs are self-emissive, highly efficient and have excellent optical properties. There are two principle technologies of OLED:

  • vacuum deposition systems - require major investment for small molecule production
  • solution processable OLED systems - need far lower investment, and future fabrication is expected to become increasingly inexpensive

OLED devices also have potential to be mass produced on flexible as well as glass substrates. They are versatile and have a wide variety of applications - from simple monochrome large area lighting to full colour, video-capable graphic displays. However, they are susceptible to water vapour and oxygen.

Active matrix liquid crystal display

Active matrix liquid crystal displays (AM LCDs) dominate the market in volume and value. However, other FPD technologies are evolving and moving to volume production. The more data needed to be shown on a display screen, the greater the numbers of row lines needed. The common method used today addresses each pixel separately, using thin film transistors of silicon for each pixel.

Future markets for FPDs

The OLED market is expected to increase rapidly as technology matures and manufacturing costs drop - the OLED TV market alone is predicted to reach €7.5 billion by 2020. The focus will be upon larger and higher-resolution displays for TV, multimedia, computer and portable applications.

Growth is likely to be driven by demand for low power consumption and the ability to produce lightweight, thin, high-quality displays.

In the longer term, new markets for flexible all-organic interactive displays are expected to gain a larger market share, with different display technologies developing their own niche markets.

Flexible displays and OLED-based micro-displays offer good opportunities for European manufacturers. This is because Europe is at the leading edge of new technologies such as organic transistors, and new manufacturing approaches such as printing. There is already a rapidly growing market for:

  • reflective displays
  • e-readers

Projections of global sales values and volumes for commercially available FPD technologies suggest that the various forms of LCD will remain dominant for the foreseeable future. However, remaining competitive in the LCD field will require vast investment in new manufacturing plants that can handle larger substrate sizes.

The only ‘new’ display technologies expected to make any penetration are flexible and 3D displays.

FPDs and the environment

As the FPD market continues to grow, the trend towards even larger and higher-resolution displays for TV, multimedia, computer and portable applications continues.

That trend would greatly increase power consumption, so it will be vital to significantly reduce the power consumption of FPDs. This presents good opportunities for European companies to help further develop the energy efficient OLED display technology.

Organic and large area electronics (OLAE) technology has the potential to be better for the environment due to the:

  • reduced use and consumption of materials
  • lower energy demands during the manufacturing process
  • low toxic impact of OLAE devices

OLAE can significantly reduce the cycle times and cost structures, because it uses high-volume commercial manufacturing, such as roll-to-roll printing. Flexographic and screen printing is likely to feature in any future mix of low-cost manufacturing techniques.

Applying OLAE technology to create disposable devices will also require consideration. To avoid any adverse ecological effects, research and design choices will need to be supported by thorough analysis.

European developments

European research and development into organic large area displays has already led to the development of two types of technology:

  • emissive - where light is only generated when required. With increasing pressure to reduce power consumption, especially for larger area displays, this will increase the need for OLEDs
  • reflective - displays with very low power consumption. This could also reduce the need for paper - as it’s possibly a good substitute for magazines, newspapers and other printed media

Research and innovation for flat panel displays

Much of the rapid growth in the vast global display technologies market is driven by research and development (R&D) within UK universities and businesses.

Continued research in FPDs is fuelled by the need for:

  • improved performance at lower cost
  • simplified manufacturing processes
  • greater opportunity for recycling materials in the device at the end of product life

Fundamental R&D continues in the area of novel 3D display system architecture and devices, and a comprehensive range of raw materials, substrates, device architectures and process equipments to support the fast-emerging plastic electronics marketplace. For more information, read the article on printable electronics. on the CPI website.

Significant collaborative R&D activities already exist. This should be strengthened by further investment in creating R&D facilities and new manufacturing joint ventures. Europe presently holds a strong position in the R&D of organic large area displays.

The UK’s focus on innovation is also ongoing, particularly in:

  • enhanced materials
  • device architecture
  • manufacturing hardware
  • associated core intellectual property

Advice, funding and support for businesses involved with flat panel display production

There are many support organisations, trade and professional bodies and collaborative programmes and partnerships available to help FPD manufacturers.

Centre of Molecular Materials for Photonics and Electronics

Centre of Molecular Materials for Photonics and Electronics (CMMPE) offers access to the broad multidisciplinary area of organic photonics - covering liquid crystal materials research and innovative displays, through to new telecoms and datacoms devices. It also offers access to newer technologies, such as:

  • light emitting polymers
  • organic lasers
  • plastic electro-optic device technology
  • microwave switches and hybrid systems

National Microelectronics Institute

This is a semiconductor trade body supporting organisations in UK and Ireland with increasing links into Europe. National Microelectronics Institute (NMI)’s mission is to build a connected network of stakeholders as a community with common goals, interests and business opportunities.

Investment features high on their agenda - as NMI regularly creates, brokers and signposts collaborative research and business opportunities throughout its connected community.
