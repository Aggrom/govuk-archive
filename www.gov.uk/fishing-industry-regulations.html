Fishing industry regulations

What to expect from vessel inspections at harbour and at sea, changes to inshore fisheries management and how illegal fishing gets reported.

Introduction

Fisheries regulations in England and Wales help maintain a compliant fishing industry in English and Welsh waters. Marine enforcement officers of the Marine Management Organisation (MMO) carry out inspections of fishing vessels to monitor compliance with EU and UK fisheries legislation. Inshore fisheries are separately managed by inshore fisheries and conservation authorities (IFCAs).

This guide outlines the procedures you need to be aware of regarding vessel inspections, both in harbour and at sea. It also explains the changes to inshore fisheries management and the powers that IFCA enforcement officers have to tackle illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing on the high seas.

What to expect from a vessel inspection in harbour

Marine enforcement officers (MEOs) carry out inspections of fishing vessels either in harbour or at the time of landing to comply with EU and UK fisheries legislation. Inspections may be conducted at any time of the day or night.

The MMO inspects vessels in a fair, courteous and professional way. During an inspection, MEOs will:

  • identify themselves when meeting you to inspect your vessel
  • only board a vessel when they consider it safe to do so
  • conduct a catch composition inspection on landing including a full box count and/or a check of weighing by species
  • undertake a vehicle inspection if the catch is to be transported to another port

What you need to show an inspector

Fishing vessel masters are expected to show full fisheries related documentation, catch and fishing gear. An inspector will check:

  • documents such as logbook, licence and variations and permits, fishroom plan and Certificate of Registry
  • catch in the fishroom and compare this with the logbook
  • gear on deck, as it must conform to appropriate mesh sizes and dimensions for the fishing area and target species
  • an onshore box count and of weighing methods
  • transport documents which must be carried for transportation of fish
  • landing declaration for hake, cod and sole

Download the code of conduct for vessel inspections in harbour from the MMO website (PDF, 45KB).

After an inspection, an MEO will advise fishers on fisheries regulations as appropriate. MEOs should investigate any apparent offences in a fair, impartial and professional manner, and avoid interfering unnecessarily with the landing of catches.

What to expect from a vessel inspection at sea

MEOs carry out inspections of fishing vessels at sea to comply with EU and UK fisheries legislation. Inspections at sea may happen during the day or night.

The MMO inspects vessels in a fair and professional way and boarding teams aim to conduct inspections quickly so they do not hold up fishing operations.

During an inspection, fishing vessel masters should continue to use marine very high frequency (VHF) radios and monitor VHF channel 16 (the international calling and distress signal). They should respond when hailed.

Inspectors will usually give notice of an inspection and boarding, unless communication is not possible. Masters of fishing vessels should:

  • ensure the safe embarkation of boarding teams
  • provide a an appropriate boarding ladder 
  • provide a lee as necessary

What you need to show an inspector

Fishing vessel masters are expected to show full fisheries-related documentation, catch and fishing gear. This includes checking:

  • documents such as logbook, licence and variations and permits, fishroom plan and Certificate of Registry 
  • assessment of catch in fishroom and comparison with logbook and weighing check
  • gear for appropriate mesh sizes and dimensions

Download the code of conduct for vessel inspections at sea from the MMO website (PDF, 43KB).

Inshore fisheries management

Under the Marine and Coastal Access Act 2009, ten inshore fisheries and conservation authorities (IFCAs) have replaced sea fisheries committees (SFCs).

Each IFCA manages an IFC district that covers part of the English coast that goes out to six nautical miles and its inland boundaries align with those of its constituent local authorities. IFCAs also manage sea fisheries resources in estuaries that fall within their districts. Download a map of the IFCA districts from the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website (PDF 589KB).

IFCAs have powers in regards to:

  • boarding and inspecting vessels
  • entering and inspecting premises, dwellings and vehicles
  • inspecting documents

IFCAs have powers to make byelaws to manage sea fisheries resources within their districts and for wider environmental purposes - eg the protection of species and habitats from fishing activity.

Each IFCA governing committee is made up of:

  • one seat each for representatives from the Marine Management Organisation (MMO), the Environment Agency and Natural England
  • elected representatives of county and unitary local authorities in the district
  • members who understand the needs and opinions of the local fishing community, and who have knowledge of marine environmental matters
