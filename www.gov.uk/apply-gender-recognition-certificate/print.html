Applying for a Gender Recognition Certificate, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Changing your gender

By law you remain your birth gender unless you apply for a Gender Recognition Certificate to change it legally. To do this you must:

  • be 18 or over
  • be diagnosed with gender dysphoria (unhappiness with your birth gender)
  • have lived as your new gender for 2 years (or changed gender legally abroad)
  • intend to live in your changed gender for the rest of your life
  • apply to the Gender Recognition Panel

Pensions

UK law sometimes treats men and women differently, eg women can claim the State Pension earlier. Changing your gender could affect your company or personal pension schemes.

Download Guidance on benefits and pensions (PDF, 192KB)

Marriages and civil partnerships

If you legally change your gender you’ll need to end your marriage or civil partnership.

Download Guidance for married people or those in civil partnerships (PDF, 150KB)

You have legal rights as a transgender person.

If you’ve changed your gender abroad

If you legally changed your gender in the European Union (EU) or the European Economic Area (EEA), this is recognised in the UK.

If the country you changed it in is an ‘approved country’ outside the EU or EEA (check the list below), it’s partially recognised in the UK but you’ll need to re-apply using a simplified process.

If the country isn’t approved, you’ll need to follow the full application process using the standard form above.

How the Gender Recognition Panel decides

  1. The panel may ask you for more information or evidence, eg medical reports.

  2. If you’re successful, the panel will contact the General Register Office to make an entry in the Gender Recognition Register.

  3. You’ll get a new short birth certificate from the General Register Office (listing name, sex, date of birth, district and sub-district of birth). The office will explain how to apply to them for a full birth certificate.

  4. If you’re unsuccessful, the panel will write to you with their reasons.

  5. You have the right to appeal - the panel will explain how.

Part 3: Legal documents following a gender change

Certificates

You’ll get a new short birth certificate from the General Register Office (listing name, sex, date of birth, district and sub-district of birth).

The office will explain how to apply to them for a full birth certificate.

You’ll need to tell certain government departments about your gender change - the Gender Recognition Panel will tell you which ones.

Marriages and civil partnerships

You’ll get an interim Gender Recognition Certificate if you’re married or in a civil partnership so you can easily end it.

You’ll then get a full Gender Recognition Certificate so you can marry or partner the other person depending on their legal gender.

Download Guidance for married people or those in civil partnerships (PDF, 150KB)

Your children are not legally affected by your legal gender change - you’re still their mother or father as stated on their birth certificate.
