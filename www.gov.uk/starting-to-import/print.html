Starting to import, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If you want to import goods into the UK you have to:

  • find the correct commodity code for your goods
  • pay VAT in some cases
  • fill in a VAT return if you’re VAT registered
  • register with CHIEF system for importers and exporters if you’re exporting outside the EU
  • declare the goods you import using the CHIEF system
  • pay duty in some cases
  • get a licence for some goods (eg firearms)

The exact rules for importing depends on whether you’re importing from another country in the European Union (EU) or from overseas.

Some businesses use freight forwarding agents to help them with their import procedures.

Part 2: Importing from the EU

Commodity codes

You need a commodity code for all imports from other EU countries. The code classifies your goods for tax and regulations.

Paying VAT for EU imports

If you import from another European Union (EU) country you must add these acquisitions to your usual VAT return and submit your return online every quarter.

If you import goods worth more than £250,000 you’ll also have to fill in an Intrastat Declaration.

You’ll have to pay VAT at UK rates for goods you import from another EU country.

Paying duty on imports from EU countries

You don’t have to pay duty on goods that have been produced in the EU and are then imported to the UK. These goods are ‘in free circulation’.

This also covers goods from outside the EU if duty has already been paid on them. For example, a French company imports bicycles from China and pays duty on them. They are now in free circulation and you can import them to the UK without paying further duties.

Part 3: Importing from non-EU countries

Commodity codes

You need a commodity code for all imports from non-EU countries. The code classifies your goods for tax and regulations.

Import declarations to customs

All imports from outside the European Union (EU) must be declared to HMRC, using form C88.

You submit your form through CHIEF (Single Administrative Document).

You’ll need to classify your goods according to the UK Trade Tariff which also lists the duty charges, tax, custom rules and paperwork for imports from non-EU countries.

You can use a use freight forwarding agent to make the declaration for you.

If your goods go through another EU country before coming to the UK, your supplier or the business responsible for transport must make an Entry Summary Declaration in the country. Check that this is done because in some cases the receiving business can be responsible for it.

In some cases you can apply for simplified import procedures through Community transit.

Detailed information on importing procedures

Paying duty and VAT on imports from non-EU countries

You normally have to pay duty on goods imported from non-EU countries when they’re first brought into the EU.

The amount of duty you pay depends on how the goods are classified under the UK Trade Tariff and how they’ll be used.

You may be able to apply for reduced or zero rate duty for goods from certain countries as long as you can prove their origin.

Your goods will not be released by customs until you’ve paid all duty and UK VAT.

It is possible to import goods from non-EU countries without paying duty as long as they stay in a ‘bonded warehouse.’ These warehouses are places exempt from duty. For example, you could import goods from the USA, store them in a bonded warehouse in the UK and move them into a bonded warehouse in Spain without paying duty.

More on customs warehousing.

There are also some special import reliefs.
