Agricultural workers' rights , a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If you work in agriculture, some of your employment rights are different from other workers.

Before you begin work, your employer must give you an Agricultural Wage Order. This states the terms and conditions of your employment.

You’re normally entitled to:

  • overtime pay
  • paid holiday
  • Agricultural Sick Pay
  • bad weather pay
  • night work pay

If you’re a trainee, your rights are different - eg you do not get paid holidays. Speak to your employer or contact the Pay and Work Rights Helpline.

Rest breaks

If you’re aged 18 and over and work more than 5½ hours per day, you’re entitled to an unpaid 30-minute rest break.

It should be:

  • uninterrupted
  • spent away from your immediate place of work
  • not be taken at the beginning or the end of the working day

Agricultural Sick Pay

To qualify for Agricultural Sick Pay you must have been continuously employed by the same employer for at least 52 weeks.

Bad weather pay

If bad weather stops you from working, and your employer can’t give you other work, you should still be paid.

If you work for a gangmaster

You might work for an agency classed as a ‘gangmaster’. Most need a licence from the Gangmasters Licensing Authority.

You can check if a gangmaster holds a licence.

There are some circumstances when a gangmaster doesn’t need a licence. You can find out more:

  • from the Gangmasters Licensing Authority
  • by contacting the Pay and Work Rights Helpline

Part 2: Agricultural worker categories and grades

There are different types of agricultural worker - split into 4 categories and 6 grades.

Categories

You’re a full-time flexible worker if you work:

  • a 39 basic hour week - the hours you work can vary over different days
  • set working hours and working days, which can’t be changed unless agreed with your employer
  • on a Sunday when needed

Part-time flexible worker

You’re a part-time flexible worker if you work:

  • less than 39 basic hours a week - the hours you work can vary over different days
  • set working hours and working days, which can’t be changed unless agreed with your employer

Trainee

You’re a trainee if you’re:

  • on work experience as part of a Business, Innovation and Skills-approved training scheme
  • on work experience in agriculture as part of the Diploma in Environmental and Land-Based Studies for 14- to 19-year-olds
  • taking part in the second phase of the European Leonardo da Vinci Programme

Apprentice

If you’re an apprentice, you can find out more about your rights in the Apprenticeships.

Grades

Your grade is based on your skills and responsibilities.

Grade 1

A grade 1 initial worker is usually supervised and works on simple tasks - eg harvesting or packing.

You have the right to be trained to become a grade 2 worker once you’ve worked for the same employer continuously for 30 weeks.

Grade 2

You’re a grade 2 standard worker if you hold one of the following:

  • a vocational qualification of at least NVQ at level 2
  • a certificate of competence for the agricultural sector you work in

You’re also a grade 2 worker if you:

  • work mainly unsupervised
  • work with animals
  • use powered machinery
  • drive a tractor

Grade 3

To be a grade 3 lead worker you should have worked in agriculture for at least 2 of the past 5 years, and have either:

  • a National Certificate in agriculture or horticulture
  • 4 certificates of competence or non-accredited competencies, for the agricultural sector you work in

Or if:

  • you manage a team - but not discipline team members
  • your employer views you as a grade 3 team leader and you’ve completed a 1 month (maximum) trial period

Grade 4

To be at grade 4 you should have worked in agriculture for at least 2 of the past 5 years, and have either:

  • an NVQ level 3 vocational qualification
  • 8 certificates of competence for the agricultural sector you work in

You should also have worked for the same employer continuously for 12 months since getting the grade 4 qualification.

There are other qualifications for the grade 4 - contact the Pay and Work Rights Helpline.

Grade 5

You’re a grade 5 supervisory worker if you’re responsible for either:

  • supervising work on a farm on a daily basis
  • instructing, supervising and disciplining staff

Grade 6

You’re a grade 6 farm management worker if you have either:

  • management responsibility for a farm - or part of a farm if it’s run as a separate business
  • responsibility for employing, disciplining and dismissing staff

Part 3: Pay grades and overtime

Your pay depends on your job category and grade - but you must be paid at least the Agricultural Minimum Wage (AMW) rate for your grade.

If the National Minimum Wage (NMW) rates are higher, you should be paid that instead.

Trainees

If you’re a trainee you’re not entitled to be paid for:

  • the hours you’re being trained
  • holidays

You should be paid for any work you do as part of a separate contract.

Training

If you’re on a training course, you should be paid at least your normal wage - including the time spent travelling to and from the training.

Overtime

You should be paid overtime if you work:

  • more than 39 basic hours in a week
  • more than 8 hours in a day
  • any hours over the normal working hours in your employment contract
  • on a public or bank holiday
  • on a Sunday - if your contract started before 1 October 2006

Being paid on completing a task

If you’re paid by the tasks you complete - eg per carton of picked fruit - your wage should not be less than the AMW (including overtime) for the hours you work.

Night work

Night work is any work done between 7pm and 6am. Your employer must pay you £1.33 per hour more than your basic pay rate.

Dog allowance

If you keep 1 or more dogs for your job, you’re entitled to £7.42 per week per dog.

If you’re given somewhere to live as part of your job

If your employer provides you with a house or ‘self-contained accommodation’ - they can deduct £1.50 per week from your AMW.

If you live in accommodation that isn’t a house - eg a caravan - they can deduct £4.73 for each day the accommodation is provided.

Your accommodation must be safe, warm, secure - and have toilet and washing facilities and fresh drinking water.

Part 4: Holiday leave and pay

The annual leave year starts on 1 October and ends on 30 September the following year.

The amount of leave you can take depends on the number of days you work each week.

Below is the annual leave entitlement for workers who work a complete annual leave year for the same employer.

Days worked each week Annual leave (days)
More than 6 38
More than 5 but not more than 6 35
More than 4 but not more than 5 31
More than 3 but not more than 4 25
More than 2 but not more than 3 20
More than 1 but not more than 2 13
One or less 7.5

Guaranteed overtime hours can be included in the number of days you work each week as long as:

  • you have to work them as part of your contract
  • you’re paid for this overtime even if there’s no work for you to do

Any other overtime that you work can’t be counted.

If you work a different number of days a week

If you work a different number of days each week you need to calculate your annual leave entitlement separately.

‘Qualifying days’ are the number of days you work (including guaranteed overtime) or take:

  • annual leave
  • statutory ordinary adoption leave
  • statutory ordinary maternity leave
  • statutory paternity leave
  • sick leave (paid or unpaid)

To calculate your leave entitlement you should:

  1. Work out the number of weeks from 1 October to the day before you start your holiday.

  2. Work out the number of qualifying days in that time.

  3. Divide the number of qualifying days by the number of weeks in step 1 - this gives you the number of days you worked each week.

  4. Work out your annual leave entitlement using the above table.

Example

Before going on leave, you work for 25 weeks.

In that time you’ve worked 125 days, taken 2 days’ sick leave and taken statutory paternity leave of 10 days.

Divide total qualifying days (137) by weeks worked (25) = 5.48 days worked on average per week.

Annual leave entitlement = 35 days.

Incomplete leave years

If you’ve not been employed by the same employer for an entire leave year, you’ll need to calculate your annual leave differently.

  1. use the table above to work out what your annual leave would be for a year

  2. work out the number of weeks you’ve worked continuously

  3. divide the number of weeks you have worked continuously by 52

  4. multiply the number of days from step one, by the answer to step three

This gives you the amount of leave you have.

Example

You work more than 5 days but less than 6 days a week = 35 annual leave.

You’ve worked for 30 weeks continuously.

30 weeks divided by 52 = 0.576.

35 days multiplied by 0.576 = 20 days.

Bank holidays

Bank holidays are included in your annual leave entitlement.

If a public holiday falls on a day you normally work, your employer can either ask you:

  • not to work - the day is an annual leave day
  • to work - you should be paid overtime for the hours you worked (the day remains as annual leave to be taken in the future)
