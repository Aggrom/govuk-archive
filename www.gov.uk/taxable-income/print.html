Taxable income, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

For amounts above your annual Personal Allowance, you must always pay Income Tax on:

  • income from work, including full-time, part-time and temporary jobs
  • profits you make if you’re self-employed
  • bank and building society interest (but not from Individual Savings Accounts (ISAs) or National Savings accounts or bonds)
  • pensions (including the State Pension, company and personal pensions)
  • retirement annuities
  • dividends from company shares (unless you hold them in an ISA)
  • rental income from property (unless it’s less than £4,250 for a room in the same property you live in)
  • pensioner bonds
  • income from a trust

Part 2: Tax-free income

You don’t pay Income Tax on:

  • income from all ISAs
  • National Savings Certificates
  • the first £4,250 a year from a lodger in your home
  • wins on Premium Bonds or the National Lottery

Part 3: State benefits that are taxable

The most common benefits that you pay Income Tax on are:

  • the State Pension
  • Jobseeker’s Allowance
  • Carer’s Allowance
  • Employment and Support Allowance (contribution based)
  • Incapacity Benefit (after you’ve been getting it for 29 weeks)
  • Bereavement Allowance
  • Pensions paid by the Industrial Death Benefit scheme
  • Graduated retirement benefit
  • Widowed Parent’s Allowance
  • Widow’s pension

Part 4: Tax-free state benefits

The most common state benefits you don’t have to pay Income Tax on are:

  • Housing Benefit
  • Employment and Support Allowance (income based)
  • Income Support - though you may have to pay tax on Income Support if you’re involved in a strike
  • Working Tax Credit
  • Child Tax Credit
  • First 28 weeks of Incapacity Benefit
  • Disability Living Allowance
  • Child Benefit
  • Guardian’s Allowance
  • Attendance Allowance
  • Pension Credit
  • Winter Fuel Payments and Christmas Bonus
  • Free TV licence for over 75s
  • Lump sum Bereavement Payments
  • Maternity Allowance
  • Industrial Injuries Benefit
  • Severe Disablement Allowance
  • War Widow’s Pension
  • Young Person’s Bridging Allowance

Part 5: How you pay tax on your income

For most people, Income Tax is taken automatically, eg from your wages, personal pension or many state benefits.

If your finances are more complex, or you’re self employed, you must pay Income Tax through the Self Assessment system.

If you have taxable income that you’re not paying Income Tax on, you must tell HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).
