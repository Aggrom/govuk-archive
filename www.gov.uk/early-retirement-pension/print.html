Early retirement and your pension, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: State Pension

When you can get State Pension

Retiring early won’t have an effect on when you can get your State Pension. The earliest you can get your State Pension is when you reach your State Pension age.

How much State Pension you get

Your State Pension is based on how much National Insurance you have paid or been credited. You qualify for National Insurance when you’re working or if you’re a parent, carer or claiming certain benefits.

Part 2: Personal and workplace pensions

When you get your personal or workplace pension

When you can get your pension will depend on your pension rules, but it must be after you’re 55.

You may be able to take your pension before this age if you’re retiring because of ill-health.

You can retire at any age if you have serious ill-health and you have less than a year’s life expectancy. You can take up to 100% of your pension fund as a tax-free lump sum. If you’re married or a civil partner when you take the payment, the pension scheme must keep at least 50% of your pension pot to provide a pension for your husband, wife or civil partner.

How much personal and workplace pension you get

There are 2 types of workplace and personal pension. These are called ‘money purchase’ and ‘salary related’ pensions. Check your pension to find out which type you have.

How much your money purchase pension is worth

The value of your pension will be depend on how your investment has performed. Your money purchase pension will be smaller if you retire early because:

  • you’ve had fewer years to pay into your pension
  • your pension will need to last longer

How much your salary related pension is worth

Your salary-related pension is usually based on a fraction of either your final salary or your career average salary, depending on which type of scheme you are a member of. If you’re considering early retirement you’ll probably receive a smaller pension.
