Become an LGV driving instructor, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

You can apply to join the voluntary register of large goods vehicle (LGV) instructors if you want to provide training to people learning to drive lorries.

How to qualify

To qualify you’ll have to pass 3 qualifying tests.

You must pass all 3 tests within 1 year of the date that you first take the theory test.

You have to retake the theory test and pass each part in order to join the register if you don’t complete the process within this time.

You have up to 3 attempts at each test. If you don’t pass in the 3 attempts, you must wait until the end of the 1-year period before you can start the qualifying process again.

When you’ve qualified

When you’ve qualified and joined the register:

  • your details will be given to people looking for LGV training
  • you can advertise yourself as a ‘Driving Standards Agency registered LGV instructor’

Your registration as an LGV instructor will last for 4 years.

Part 2: Eligibility

To start the qualifying process, you must have had a current full large goods vehicle licence for the category of lorry you’re instructing in for at least either:

  • 3 years
  • 1 year and have had the equivalent full passenger carrying vehicle licence for at least 3 years

Example

You must have had a full category D+E licence for 3 years if you’ve only had a full category C+E licence for 1 year.

Both of these count towards the qualifying period:

  • a vocational licence issued outside the UK
  • any unexpired part of a provisional licence after passing your test

Motoring offences and convictions

You must not have been banned from driving during the 4 years before you join the register.

Motoring offences and convictions still in force when you apply will be considered with your application.

Part 3: Applying to join the register

To apply to start the qualifying as a large goods vehicle (LGV) instructor, you need to buy the starter pack from the Driving Standards Agency (DSA).

You can get this by writing to DSA and sending a cheque or postal order made payable to ‘Driving Standards Agency’ for £6.99.

Part 4: Preparing for your tests

You can prepare for your large goods vehicle (LGV) instructor tests with the help of a range of books, guides and DVDs.

You can buy these from The Stationery Office and most high street and online book shops.

You can also get a copy of the 9 pre-set exercises used during the instructional ability test from the Driving Standards Agency (DSA).

Part 5: Taking the theory test

You can book your instructor theory test when the Driving Standards Agency has accepted your application to join the register. It costs £66.

Documents to take to your test

You must bring both parts of your driving licence - the photocard and the paper counterpart. You must take your signed driving licence and a valid passport if you have an old-style paper licence.

Your test won’t go ahead and you’ll have to pay again if you don’t bring the right documents.

Multiple-choice questions

You’re allowed up to 90 minutes for the multiple-choice part of the test.

You’ll be asked 100 questions in total. There are 25 questions in 4 categories. To pass the multiple-choice part you must get both:

  • an overall score of at least 85 out of 100
  • at least 20 out of 25 in each of the 4 categories

You will fail if you get an overall mark of 85 or higher, but don’t score high enough in each category.

Hazard perception

You’ll then be shown 14 clips that feature every day road scenes. There will be:

  • one developing hazard in 13 clips
  • 2 developing hazards in one clip

A developing hazard is something that may cause you to take some action, such as changing speed or direction.

You can get up to 5 points for each clip. The earlier you notice a developing hazard and make a response, the higher you score. You need to score at least 57 points out of 75 to pass this part.

Part 6: Taking the driving ability test

To book the driving ability test, use the application form you’re given when you pass the theory test.

The test lasts around 1 hour and 30 minutes. It costs £142.80.

What to bring to your test

You must bring:

  • your valid Great Britain or Northern Ireland driving licence
  • a suitable vehicle

You need to bring both parts of your driving licence - the photocard and the paper counterpart.

You must take your signed driving licence and a valid passport if you have an old-style paper licence.

Your test won’t go ahead and you’ll lose your fee if you don’t bring the right documents and a suitable vehicle.

Rules for the vehicle you use

You must bring a large goods vehicle that:

  • is roadworthy and legal
  • is a category C1, C1+E, C or C+E vehicle (depending on the instructor certificate you want)
  • has normal performance for its type
  • is not carrying any goods or burden
  • is a right-hand drive
  • has a seat for a forward-facing passenger
  • has seatbelts fitted for the examiner’s use
  • has external nearside and offside mirrors for use by the examiner during the test
  • does not display ‘L’ plates during the test

How the test works

You’ll drive in varying road and traffic conditions, including motorways or dual carriageways where possible.

You must prove to the examiner that you can move off safely and smoothly:

  • uphill
  • downhill
  • ahead normally
  • at an angle

You must also prove to the examiner that you can safely:

  • meet other vehicles
  • overtake
  • cross the path of other vehicles
  • keep a safe separation distance
  • handle various types of roundabouts
  • exercise correct lane discipline

You must show that you:

  • can use all mirrors effectively
  • use all signals correctly
  • have alertness and anticipation
  • can obey speed limits
  • are aware of how the vehicle is running

Independent driving

You’ll have to drive without turn-by-turn directions from the examiner for around 10 minutes. You’ll have to follow either:

  • traffic signs
  • a series of directions given to you before you set off
  • a combination of both

Off-road exercises

You must safely complete an off-road reversing exercise at the driving test centre, and an uncoupling and recoupling exercise if you want to instruct drivers of C1+E and C+E vehicles.

Your test result

The examiner will give you the result at the end of the test.

You’ll pass the driving ability test if you make 6 driving faults or less.

You’ll fail if you make a serious or dangerous fault, or make more than 6 riding faults.

Failing the first or second attempt

You can take the test again if you fail the test at either your first or second attempt.

Failing the third attempt

You have to retake and pass the theory test again if you fail the driving ability test 3 times.

You have to wait 1 year from when you first passed the theory test before you can take it again.

Part 7: Taking the instructional ability test

To book the instructional ability test, use the application form you’re given when you pass the driving ability test.

It costs £142.80.

What to bring to your test

On the day, you’ll need to bring:

  • both parts of your driving licence - the photocard and the paper counterpart
  • the pass letter from the driving ability test
  • a suitable vehicle

You must take your signed driving licence and a valid passport if you have an old-style paper licence.

Rules for the vehicle you use

You must bring a large goods vehicle (LGV) that:

  • is roadworthy and legal
  • is a category C1, C1+E, C or C+E vehicle (depending on the instructor certificate you want)
  • has normal performance for its type
  • is not carrying any goods or burden
  • is a right-hand drive
  • has a seat for a forward-facing passenger
  • has seatbelts fitted for the examiner’s use
  • has external nearside and offside mirrors for use by the examiner during the test
  • displays ‘L’ plates during the test

How the test works

The examiner will begin by explaining which of the 9 possible pre-set exercises will be used.

The examiner will play the role of one of the following types of pupil:

  • someone learning to drive either a category C1 or C
  • someone learning to drive either a category C1+E or C+E
  • someone who has already failed LGV test and needs extra training

The examiner will drive the vehicle and simulate faults. You should treat the examiner as though they are your pupil.

You can use lesson plans and training aids, and you can bring brief notes or subject headings along with you.

What you have to do

During the test, you have to:

  • tailor your instruction to the standard of the pupil and the time available
  • give your pupil a chance to show what you’ve taught them and correct any faults
  • keep control of the lesson, be patient and tactful and encourage the pupil

What the examiner assesses

The examiner will assess you on:

  • the method of your instruction and how clear, thorough and correct it is
  • how well you observe and correct the pupil’s driving errors
  • your general manner and attitude when dealing with the pupil

Your test result

The examiner will give you the result at the end of the test.

Failing the first or second attempt

You can take the test again if you fail the test at either your first or second attempt.

Failing the third attempt

You have to retake and pass the theory test and driving ability test again if you fail the instructional ability test 3 times.

You have to wait 1 year from when you first passed the theory test before you can take it again.

Part 8: Managing your registration

Your name will be added to the large goods vehicle instructor (LGV) register when you’ve passed the 3 qualifying tests and paid the registration fee of £120.

You’ll then be sent your registration certificate, which lasts for 4 years.

You should display your certificate of registration in the bottom left-hand corner of the front windscreen of the vehicle when you’re giving instruction.

Staying on the register

You have to retake and pass the driving ability test and instructional ability test during the last 12 months of your registration if you want to stay on the register.

You’ll have to pay to take the tests, and then pay the re-registration fee.

You’re responsible for remembering when your registration runs out and making sure you start the re-qualifying process in enough time.

You can contact the Driving Standards Agency (DSA) to get the forms to apply to take the tests again.
