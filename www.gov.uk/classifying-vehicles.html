Introduction

How you classify a vehicle under the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff) depends on whether or not it is designed to transport passengers and, if so, whether it is used to carry fewer or more than ten people, including the driver. Classification also depends on the type of fuel your vehicle uses - eg petrol, diesel or electric motors - the engine size and whether it is new or used.

You would typically classify an incomplete or unfinished classic vehicle as you would a finished one if it still has its essential character - eg a motor vehicle not yet fitted with its engine or interior fittings, wheels or tyres. Tools, articles and accessories usually have the same tariff classification as a vehicle if they are needed for, presented with, or normally sold with that vehicle.

Another important reason for identifying the commodity code of vehicles is to find out whether they are considered dual-use goods - ie goods that can be adapted and put to strategic or military use. Vehicles classed as dual-use goods require an export licence.

Classifying cars

According to the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff), the term ‘cars’ includes motor cars and other motor vehicles principally designed for the transport of persons.

For most cars, engine size and whether the car is new or used will dictate their classification under subheading 8703 in Chapter 87 of the Tariff. The exception is for all terrain vehicles (ATVs) and quad bikes, which you will need to further evaluate to determine whether they belong under 8703 or 8701 classification. You can download Tariff Notice 23/09 on clarification on how to classify ATVs from the HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) website (PDF, 23K).

Cars for the transport of fewer than ten passengers - including the driver - come under heading 8703 and include:

  • station wagons (estate cars)
  • racing cars
  • ATVs and quad bikes that meet certain criteria
  • adult go-karts
  • mobility scooters
  • trike bikes - ie three-wheeled motorised vehicles with no body work for the transport of two passengers

These 8703 cars are further classified by the type of fuel they use:

ATVs and quad bikes can be classified under 8703 2110 or 8701 9011 to 8701 9090 classifications. Their correct classification depends on the vehicle specifications, eg unbraked towing capacity.

Classifying goods vehicles, including chassis fitted with engines, bodies and cabs

Goods vehicles include chassis fitted with engines, bodies - including cabs - and multi-purpose vehicles that can transport both persons and goods, eg pick-up vans with more than two axles. These vehicles are classified in Chapter 87 of the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff).

For most goods vehicles, their gross weight, engine size, the type of fuel they use and whether the vehicle is new or used will dictate their classification under 8704:

Classification of petrol goods vehicles

Petrol New/Used commodity code
Of a gross weight less than 5 tonnes, not exceeding 2,800cc New 8704 3191 00 / Used 8704 3199 00
Exceeding 2,800cc New 8704 3131 00 / Used 8704 3139 00
Of a gross weight more than 5 tonnes New 8704 3291 00 / Used 8704 3299 10

Classification of diesel/semi-diesel goods vehicles

Diesel/semi-diesel New/Used commodity code
Of a gross weight more than 5 tonnes but not more than 20 tonnes New 8704 2291 00 / Used 8704 2299 00
Of a gross weight exceeding 20 tonnes New 8704 2391 10 / Used 8704 2399 10

The 8706 chapter headings apply to chassis classified under headings 8701 to 8705 - ie to chassis that are fitted with engines. Heading 8707 covers bodies - including cabs - for the motor vehicles of 8701 to 8705. Motor chassis fitted with cabs are excluded from 8706 and should be classified under 8703 or 8704.

Pick-up style vehicles of classification heading 8703/8704

Double cab pick-up vehicles are generally classified under either tariff heading 8703 or 8704. The tariff heading 8703 includes motor cars and other motor vehicles principally designed for transporting people, such as those having characteristics mentioned in Tariff Notice 07/08. Read Tariff Notice 07/08 on the correct way to classify double cab pick-up vehicles on the HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) website.

The tariff heading 8704 covers motor vehicles for transporting goods, eg single cab pick-up vehicles having a load-bay length of more than 50 per cent of the wheelbase of the vehicle, or having two or more axles. Find more information on classifying goods vehicles in Tariff Notice 06/07 on the HMRC website.

Classifying tractors

Tractors are classified under subheading 8701 in Chapter 87 of the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff).

Tractors are vehicles constructed essentially for hauling or pushing another vehicle, appliance or load, whether or not they include an integral container to transport tools, seeds, fertilisers or other goods, where these goods are required as part of the tractor’s main work.

Tractors include wheeled vehicles used for forestry or agriculture, road tractors for semi-trailers and pedestrian-controlled tractors.

Their classification under 8701 primarily depends on their being used for agriculture or forestry, their engine power and whether the tractor is new or used.

Classification of tractors

Tractors (other than those of 8709 Agricultural and forestry, wheeled) Engine size Commodity code
New engine power not exceeding 18 kilowatts 8701 9011 00
New exceeding 18 kilowatts but not exceeding 37 kilowatts 8701 9020 00
New exceeding 37 kilowatts but not exceeding 59 kilowatts 8701 9025 00
New exceeding 59 kilowatts but not exceeding 75 kilowatts 8701 9031 00
New exceeding 75 kilowatts but not exceeding 90 kilowatts 8701 9035 00
New exceeding 90 kilowatts 8701 9039 00
Used n/a 8701 9050 00

Road tractors for semi-trailers are classified according to whether they are new (8701 2010 00) or used (8701 2090 00).

Pedestrian-controlled tractors are all classified under a single subheading - 8701 1000 00.

The following are classified under subheading 8709:

  • self-propelled works trucks, not fitted with lifting or handling equipment, of the type used in factories, warehouses, dock areas or airports for the short-distance transport of goods
  • tractors of the type used on railway station platforms
  • parts of the above vehicles

Machines and working tools designed for fitting to tractors of heading 8701 as interchangeable equipment remain classified in their respective headings even if they are presented with a tractor, and whether or not they are mounted on it.

Classifying special purpose motor vehicles

Special purpose motor vehicles, other than those principally designed for the transport of persons or goods, are classified under subheading 8705 in Chapter 87 of the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff).

‘Special purpose motor vehicles’ cover a wide range of specialist wheeled and motorised trucks, lorries, derricks and mobile medical units. They are classified as follows:

  • 8705 10 00 00 - crane lorries
  • 8705 20 00 00 - mobile drilling derricks
  • 8705 30 00 00 - fire-fighting vehicles
  • 8705 40 00 00 - concrete-mixer lorries
  • 8705 90 10 00 - breakdown lorries
  • 8705 90 30 00 - concrete-pumping vehicles
  • 8705 90 90 00 - other

Self-propelled works trucks that are not fitted with lifting or handling equipment are classified under heading 8709. These vehicles are of the type used in factories, warehouses, dock areas or airports for short distance transport of goods. Heading 8709 also covers tractors of the type used on railway station platforms as well as parts of the vehicles in this classification.

Classifying tanks and other armoured vehicles

Tanks and other armoured, motorised fighting vehicles, whether fitted with weapons or not, and parts of such vehicles are classified as 8710 00 00 00 in the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff).

You are advised that prohibitions and restrictions apply to the import and export of these vehicles and the trade in arms generally.

Read about the prohibitions and restrictions on importing this commodity on the Department for Business, Innovation & Skills (BIS) website.

For more information about exporting restrictions, see our section on the Export Control Organisation.

Classifying classic vehicles and parts

Chapter 87 includes classification of classic or collectors’ cars and their authentic parts for vehicles unless they meet the prescribed criteria found in the Combined Nomenclature Explanatory Notes. Download a copy of the explanatory notes to the combined nomenclature of the European Communities (C 272/8) from the EUR-Lex website (PDF, 700K).

Racing cars of any age may be under Chapter 97, which covers works of art, collectors’ pieces and antiques if they have achieved significant sporting successes.

In order for a vehicle to be considered as eligible for historic vehicle status, you will have to provide extensive information dating back to the vehicle’s year of manufacture - including production figures of that make/model and when production ceased. You must also state whether substantial changes have been made to the original vehicle. Note that cars less than 30 years old are generally classified to heading 8703.

If you wish to import a vehicle, which is over 30 years old without any major changes to the chassis, steering or braking system, engine etc, and you believe it to be a collector’s piece of historic interest, you should attempt to obtain a Binding Tariff Information ruling.

Classifying motorcycles, mopeds and cycles with a motor

Subheading 8711 in Chapter 87 of the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff) includes motorcycles, mopeds and cycles fitted with an auxiliary motor whether or not they come with an attached sidecar. Sidecars are also classified under 8711.

Mobility scooters are not included in this classification and are covered under subheading 8703 10.

Scooters and electric bikes

  • Scooters may be presented as unassembled or disassembled but as long as they are to be powered by a petrol engine of up to 50cc, they are to be classified under 8711 10 00 10.
  • Electric scooters and bikes are to be classified under 8711 90 00 00.
  • Non-powered scooters - ie foot-propelled scooters - are classified in Chapter 95.

Motorcycles

The classification of motorcycles is based purely on their engine size. Those that do not exceed 50cc are classified under 8711 1000 90. Larger motorcycles are classified as follows:

Motorcyles Commodity code
Engine size exceeding 50cc but not exceeding 80cc 8711 20 91 00
exceeding 80cc but not exceeding 125cc 8711 20 93 00
exceeding 125cc but not exceeding 250cc 8711 20 98 00
exceeding 250cc but not exceeding 380cc 8711 30 10 00
exceeding 380cc but not exceeding 500cc 8711 30 90 00
exceeding 500cc but not exceeding 800cc 8711 40 00 00
exceeding 800cc 8711 50 00 00

This classification does not include bicycles and other unmotorised cycles.

Vehicles and automotive components are subject to type approval and, among others, environmental regulations and registration requirements, eg their fuel consumption and emission rates, and whether or not they are permanent or temporary imports/exports.

Classifying bicycles and other non-motorised cycles

Bicycles and other non-motorised cycles - including children’s bicycles and delivery tricycles - are classified under subheading 8712 in Chapter 87 of the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff).

A bicycle is to be classified as a complete or finished vehicle provided it has the essential character of a bicycle.

This means that the frames and forks must be present, plus any three of the following major components - wheels, saddle, handlebars (being complete with stems), brake transmission (pedals, cranks, sprockets) and gears.

Bicycles without ball bearings should be classified under 8712 00 20 00. Those that have ball bearings, should be classified under one of three subheadings as follows:

  • bicycles - 8712 00 30 00
  • unicycles - 8712 00 80 10
  • other cycles - 8712 00 80 90

This classification does not include motorcycles, mopeds, scooters and cycles with motors.

Non-powered scooters and children’s cycles other than bicycles are classified in Chapter 95.

Classifying vehicle parts and accessories

Vehicle parts and accessories can be classified in various chapters in the UK Trade Tariff (the Tariff), including but not limited to Chapter 87. The list is an extensive one to help you make the initial check, or at least to establish the correct term for the part before taking your search any further.

Many parts and accessories for vehicles of headings 8701 to 8705 should be classified under 8708, eg 8708 30xx xx for hydraulic brakes systems complete for motor vehicles. Similar parts and accessories for use in vehicles under headings 8711 to 8713 will more often be classified under 8704, eg 8714 1900 00 where the hydraulic brake system in question is for motor cycles.

The expression ‘parts and accessories’ does not apply to certain parts - such as joints or washers or to parts of general use - which are made of base metal or plastics. For a full exclusion list, see note 2 in Section XVII in the Tariff.

Where a part or accessory answers to a description in two or more of the headings of those chapters, it is to be classified under that heading which corresponds to its principal use.

You are advised to check the correct term in the list, and then use our UK Trade Tariff tool to establish a full or partial code. The following examples serve only to highlight the range of headings to which parts and accessories can be classified:

  • 8526 9120 90 - global positioning devices (GPS)
  • 8413 3020 90 - complete injection pumps for fuel
  • 4016 9300 90 - brake cylinder rubber seals
  • 8412 3100 90 - pneumatic cylinders
  • 6813 81xx xx - disc brake pads, excluding mounting

Where a full ten-digit code is not listed - ie part of the code contains ‘x’ as in the disc brake example above - you will need to have further information about the material and intended use of the part or accessory in order to select the most appropriate ten-digit code. To do so, you should key in the known digits of the code on our UK Trade Tariff.

Find commodity codes and other measures applying to imports and exports by accessing our online UK Trade Tariff tool.

Certain vehicle parts and accessories may also be subject to type approval, environmental and other regulations, eg catalytic converters.
