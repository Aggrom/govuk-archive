Introduction

The Department for Business, Innovation & Skills (BIS) is responsible for the Export Control Organisation (ECO) licensing process - issued via SPIRE.

This guide provides information on how to find out whether your export goods are controlled, the stages you need to be aware of when applying for an export control licence and what ECO licence you may require.

Step 1 - are the goods you are exporting controlled by the ECO?

The ECO is responsible for issuing export licences for ‘strategic’ items.

The main types of goods, software and technologies that require a licence are:

  • dual-use goods - goods used for commercial purposes and not originally designed for military purposes but with such a high technical specification that they could be used for military purposes
  • military equipment - such as arms, ammunition, bombs, tanks, imaging devices, military aircraft and warships
  • nuclear-related goods - including nuclear materials, nuclear reactors and nuclear processing plants
  • chemical weapons precursors and related equipment and technology - including certain micro-organisms, biological equipment and technology
  • goods used for torture and capital punishment
  • radioactive sources
  • goods used in programmes involving weapons of mass destruction and missiles used for their delivery

The controls also extend to the licensing of electronic transfers of controlled ’technology’.

There are main reason why your goods might be controlled for export purposes is because the items are listed on a UK Strategic Export Control List. See the guide about the UK Strategic Export Control Lists - the consolidated list of strategic military and dual-use items. 

If your goods are not listed on a Control List, you might still need to apply for a licence because of concerns about the destination or end-use. See the section below on ECO controls on the destination of your goods.

If you are unsure whether your items need a licence, then you might find it helpful to read the guide on how to determine if your goods need an export licence. This guide explains how to ‘self-rate’ your goods and identify the Control List reference entry number or ‘rating’ and about advisory services and database tools offered by the ECO to help you further. 

Step 2 - are there any ECO controls on the destination of your goods?

Goods are also controlled depending on their destination - either the destination country or end user of the goods. They may be controlled if:

  • the UK has concerns about the goods’ ultimate end use or end user - see the guide: Military End-Use Control guidance notes or Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD) End-Use Control.
  • the goods are to be exported to destinations subject to UN or EU trade sanctions and arms embargoes implemented in the UK - for more information see the guide on current arms embargoes and other restrictions

If you are still unsure of whether your goods require a licence, see the guide on how to determine if your goods need an export licence. 

Step 3 - what type of Export Control Organisation licence do you need?

The Export Control Organisation issues various categories of licence which you might be able to use to export your goods. These are as follows:

The main categories of licences are:

  • Open General Export Licences (OGELs) - these licence controlled goods that are of a less restricted nature or being exported to less sensitive destinations. Using an OGEL can potentially save you time and money. You are therefore advised to register for an appropriate licence if you can fulfill all the stated terms and conditions. To determine which OGEL is appropriate for your export, you can use the OGEL Checker on the OGEL Checker website. See the guide: an introduction to OGELs.
  • Standard Individual Export Licences (SIELs) - if your goods are controlled and an OGEL is not suitable for your export, you will need to apply for our standard licence type, known as a SIEL. See the guide on SIELs
  • Open Individual Export Licence (OIELs) - this is a ‘concessionary’ licence for exporters with either a track record in export licensing or with a business case. It is designed to cover long-term contracts, projects or repeat business. See the guide on OIELs

Other specific licences issued by the ECO for particular trading activities (such as ’trafficking and brokering’ or transhipment) are:

  • Trade Control Licence - see the guide
  • Transhipment Licence - see the guide
  • Global Project Licence (GPL) - see the guide

Step 4 - register and apply for an Export Control Organisation licence

You will need to register with the Export Control Organisation’s SPIRE licensing system to use OGELs - and to apply for other licence types. You can apply for an export licence on SPIRE with the Department for Business, Innovation & Skills (BIS).

If you are new to using SPIRE, then you can download guidance documents about how to use the system by accessing the guide, SPIRE - Background information.  

All licences are assessed against the Consolidated European Union and National Arms and Export Licensing Criteria. To find out more about how the government make decisions on export licence applications see the guide: assessment of export licence applications - criteria and policy.

When applying for a licence you should be aware that inaccurate or missing information on licence applications can cause delays.

You should remember that when completing your SPIRE application, you will need a valid EORI (European Operators Registration and Identification) number. This is an EU unique identification system for interaction with customers. This number connects ECO’s SPIRE system to HM Revenue & Customs’ CHIEF system, via the Automatic Licence Verification process. For more details see the guide on the EORI Scheme.

Step 5 - supply supporting documentation for Export Control Organisation licences

In most cases you will need to provide supporting information such as end user certificates or technical specifications to support your export licence application. Supporting documentation should be submitted via SPIRE along with the completed licence application details.

For more information see the guide on End-User and consignee undertakings for SIELs and OIELs.

If you provide insufficient technical or product information, there may be delays in processing your licence.

For more information see the guide on common pitfalls to avoid in export licence applications.

Step 6 - your licence is issued via SPIRE

Your licence will be issued to you via SPIRE.

It is important that you check your licence when you receive it - and that you comply with any terms and conditions it may have.

Export licences are not transferable and are not usually allowed to be altered. However, the ECO may allow some minor changes.

Step 7 - complete an export declaration

You will need your export licence number to complete a customs declaration notice.

Requirements for declaring need for an export licence if items are exported outside the EU

Exports to end-users/consignees based in the EU are known in trading terms as an intra-community transfer (ICT).

If you are exporting to another EU country, your commercial documentation needs to clearly state whether the items are controlled (and a licence is required) if exported outside the EU. Relevant commerical documents includes sales contracts, order confirmations, invoices or dispatch notes. This requirement is specified in Article 22 (10) of the EU Dual Use Regulation. For more details about the regulation, see the guide on Controls on dual-use goods (EU Dual-Use Regulation).

Open General Licence holders

All exporters registered to use Open General Licences must quote the full title of the licence(s) and the unique licence reference number(s) on export documentation, including in Box 44 of the Single Administrative Document (SAD).

See the guide on Compliance and enforcement of export controls and An Introduction to Open General Licences (OGLs).

Standard Individual Export Licence holders

If you are exporting to the EU (known as an intra-community transfer), you should be aware that there is no routine processing of documents because the EU is a customs union with no internal frontiers. As a result UK Border Agency (UKBA) officers at ports and airports are not able to check licences and documentation for goods being exported to other EU member states. However, you should adhere to the procedures in place to ‘present’ your licence to HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).

Before physically exporting controlled (licensable) goods to the EU, it is a condition of holding a SIEL that you ‘present’ the licence to HMRC. This means that you must:

  • quote the licence reference number electronically to the appropriate HMRC office at least three working days before the date of proposed export.
  • identify a location where the listed items can be inspected at any time during the three day period
  • comply with alternative arrangements - as agreed with an HMRC officer

Additionally, you must:

  • maintain records of all transactions carried out under the licence
  • show a copy of these records each time the licence is ‘presented’ to HMRC
  • allow these records to be inspected and copied by any person authorised by the Secretary of State or the Commissioners
  • inform the ECO when the licence is exhausted by emailing eco.help@bis.gsi.gov.uk 

Depending on the size of your business, ‘presenting’ a SIEL needs to be done in different ways. Large businesses should contact their HMRC Client Relation Manager. Small and medium sized businesses who do not have an HMRC Client Relation Manager should contact the central HMRC office responsible for dealing with SIELs for intra community movements. This is:

Open Individual Export Licence (OIEL) holders

OIELs do not need to be presented to HMRC at the point of export. However, except in the case of intra-EU trade, Box 44 of the SAD must show the relevant document and status code. The OIEL licence number must be shown in the document reference box. The associated documentation should also refer to the licence used.

For more information see the guide about OIELs 

Step 8 - comply with Export Control Organisation licence responsibilities

As the holder of an export licence you have various responsibilities, and are required by law to:

  • adhere to all licence terms and conditions
  • keep sufficient records
  • be aware of updates and changes view the Export Control Organisation (ECO) Notices to Exporters on the Department for Business, Innovation and Skills (BIS) website and view information on ECO training and seminars on the BIS website
  • be subject to regular compliance visits

See the guide on compliance and enforcement of export controls.

Step 9 - Export Control Organisation licence processing times

The ECO is conscious that exporters require quick, clear licensing decisions to be commercially competitive.

The ECO works to clearly defined targets in processing licences, aiming to:

  • process 70 per cent of Standard Individual Export Licences (SIEL) and Standard Individual Trade Control Licence (SITCL) applications in 20 working days
  • process 95 per cent of SIEL and SITCL applications in 60 working days
  • process 90 per cent of non-circulated rating enquiries in 10 days
  • process 90 per cent of circulated rating enquiries in 20 days
  • process appeals within 20 working days
  • process appeals within 60 working days

Find ECO performance reports and statistics on the Department for Business, Innovation & Skills (BIS) searchable database website.

You can reduce the chances of your export licence application being delayed by:

  • making sure your application is complete
  • providing as much technical or product information as possible
  • providing full street address details for consignees
  • ensuring you have sought and completed third party clearances
  • being aware that export involves the release of classified information
  • providing end user certification

Licence appeals process

Exporters whose applications for SIELs have been refused have the right to appeal to the ECO.

If you wish to appeal, you must do so in writing within 28 days of the refusal letter.

You cannot appeal against refusals of OIELs. This is because these are concessionary forms of export licences for exporters with a track record in making export licence applications.

How the appeals process works

Appeals are reviewed to assess whether the appellant has submitted new information which was not available at the time of the original refusal decision and which could materially affect the decision to refuse.

The ECO will consider requests from exporters for meetings to discuss an appeal. However this would normally only occur if it is not possible for the exporter to present his appeal in writing.

If a meeting is arranged, the exporter should present his case including any additional information not already provided. The departmental representative(s) would then clarify issues related to the appeal. This might include a request for written clarification. A record of the meeting may be circulated, as appropriate, to advisory government departments.

A decision on whether to refuse or uphold an appeal will not be made at this meeting but will follow circulation of the appeal to other departments.

Appeals may be circulated to advisory departments, depending on whether new information has been provided, or it is felt that their advice is required. The appeals also go to those departments who provided recommendations on the original application. In each case the appeal will be considered by a government official who was not involved in the original case and he/she will also be senior to the original official.

Targets on processing of appeals

Exporters should bear in mind that the government’s targets for processing appeals for SIELs are as follows:

  • 60 per cent of cases within 20 working days
  • 95 per cent within 60 working days

The target does not apply to appeals concerning destinations subject to United Nations sanctions.

You can find information about ECO targets and performance in terms of licence processing on the ECO Reports and Statistics website.

Annual report targets

ECO is committed to processing export licence applications as efficiently and speedily as possible. This commitment is balanced against the ECO’s role in making a considered risk assessment of all applications on a case by case basis. Further details about the criteria for assessing export licences are published in the guide on Assessment of Export Licence applications - Criteria and Policy.

In making a licence application, you are reminded that the targets are a guide. Licences to “sensitive” destinations, such as embargoed countries, are likely to take significantly longer than the target timescale. Before making a licence application you are advised to check the latest quarterly statistics which list the average processing time by destination.

Find information about the ECO’s targets and performance in terms of licence processing on the ECO Reports and Statistics website.

For more details about the ECO’s commitments to exporters and your responsibilities, you can download the ECO Service and Performance Code on the Department for Business website (PDF, 127K).
