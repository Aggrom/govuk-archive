Lasting Power of Attorney, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

A Lasting Power of Attorney lets you appoint someone to make decisions on your behalf.

It’s normally used when someone is unable to make their own decisions.

There are 2 types:

  • health and welfare
  • property and financial affairs

You can choose to make one type of Lasting Power of Attorney or both.

Health and welfare Lasting Power of Attorney

This allows you to choose one person or more to make decisions about things like:

  • your daily routine (eg eating and what to wear)
  • medical care
  • moving into a care home
  • refusing life-sustaining treatment

Property and financial affairs Lasting Power of Attorney

This lets you choose one person or more to make decisions about money and property for you, eg:

  • paying bills
  • collecting benefits
  • selling your home

You can appoint someone to look after your property and financial affairs at any time.

Part 2: Choose your attorney

You can choose one person or more to make decisions on your behalf, as your ‘attorney’.

When choosing an attorney, think about:

  • how well the person looks after their own affairs, eg their finances
  • how well you know them
  • if you trust them to make decisions in your best interests
  • how happy they will be to make decisions for you

Who can be your attorney

Your attorney can be anyone 18 or over, eg:

  • a relative
  • a friend
  • a professional (like a solicitor)
  • your husband, wife or partner

Who can’t be your attorney

You can’t choose someone who:

  • is under 18
  • is unable to make decisions
  • has been declared bankrupt (if you want them to look after property or money)

Part 4: Register a Lasting Power of Attorney

A Lasting Power of Attorney can only be used once it’s been registered with the Office of the Public Guardian.

You can register a Lasting Power of Attorney if you’re:

  • the person who needs the attorney - the ‘donor’
  • an attorney

If you’re the donor, you must be able to make your own decisions when you register.

Download ‘Guidance notes on how to register a Lasting Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 590KB)

It can take up to 10 weeks to register a Lasting Power of Attorney if there are no mistakes in the application.

Inform people who need to be told

Before you register, send a copy of the form below to all the ‘people to be told’ listed in the Lasting Power of Attorney.

Download ‘Notice of intention to apply for registration of a Lasting Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 360KB)

This will tell them the Lasting Power of Attorney is being registered, and give them 5 weeks to raise any concerns.

Fill in form to register

You then need to fill in the ‘application to register’ form:

Download ‘Application to register a Lasting Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 780KB)

Download ‘Guidance notes for completing the application to register form’ (PDF, 140KB)

Send this form to the Office of the Public Guardian.

Make sure you include:

  • the original Lasting Power of Attorney form - or a certified copy
  • the fee, unless you’re applying for help with fees

Part 5: How much it costs

It costs £130 to register each Lasting Power of Attorney.

Registering both types of Lasting Power of Attorney costs £260.

Make cheques payable to ‘Office of the Public Guardian’ for the full amount.

Write the donor’s name on the back of the cheque, as well as the case and fee numbers, if you know them.

If your form is returned because it’s incorrect, it costs £65 to register again.

Pay by credit or debit card

You can pay by credit or debit card by calling 0121 450 6686.

Say how you’re going to pay on the form or covering letter. Include your phone number.

Get a discount or help with fees

You may not have to pay the fees if you’re on means-tested benefits or a low income.

Read the document below and fill in the form at the end to apply for help with fees.

Download ‘Lasting Power of Attorney fees’ (PDF, 80KB)

Part 6: Object to a Lasting Power of Attorney

You can object to a Lasting Power of Attorney when it’s registered if you’re:

  • the donor - the person who needs the attorney
  • an attorney
  • one of the ‘people to be told’ listed in the Lasting Power of Attorney

To object, fill in one of the forms below.

If you’re the donor, fill in the first form. Otherwise, use the second form.

Download ‘Objection by donor to the registration of a Lasting Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 307KB)

Download ‘Objection to a proposed registration of a Lasting Power of Attorney on factual grounds’ (PDF, 348KB)

Other objections

If you’re not one of the people listed above, you’ll have to pay a £400 court fee.

First, tell the Office of the Public Guardian that you want to object, using the form below:

Download ‘Notice of an application to object to registration of a Lasting Power of Attorney made to the Court of Protection’ (PDF, 358KB)

You’ll then need to apply to the court.

To do this, fill in the Court of Protection application forms.

Return them to the Court of Protection, with:

  • the documents listed on the forms
  • a cheque - made payable to the ‘Court of Protection’

Part 7: Cancel or end a Lasting Power of Attorney

If you’re the donor, you can cancel a Lasting Power of Attorney at any point, even if it’s been registered.

To do this, you must complete a statement called a ‘deed of revocation’.

You can use the wording from the example, below:

Example of a ‘deed of revocation’

This deed of revocation is made by [donor’s name] of [donor’s address].

  1. I granted a Lasting Power of Attorney for Property and Financial Affairs/Health and Welfare [delete as appropriate] on [date donor signed the Lasting Power of Attorney] appointing [name of first attorney] of [address of first attorney] and [name of second attorney] of [address of second attorney] to act as my attorney(s).

  2. I revoke the Lasting Power of Attorney and the authority granted by it.

Signed and delivered as a deed [donor’s signature]
Date signed [date]
Witnessed by [signature of witness]
Full name of witness [name of witness]
Address of witness [address of witness]

You must be able to make your own decisions when you cancel.

Send the deed of revocation to the attorneys and the Office of the Public Guardian with the original documents.

If you don’t want to be an attorney

If you don’t want to be an attorney, you can give up or ‘disclaim’ the responsibility.

To do this, fill in the form below and return it to the Office of the Public Guardian with the original documents:

Download ‘Disclaimer by a proposed or acting attorney under a Lasting Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 317KB)

Other ways a Lasting Power of Attorney can end

A Lasting Power of Attorney will end automatically if the attorney:

  • dies
  • loses the ability to make decisions - ‘mental capacity’
  • divorces or ends a civil partnership with the donor

It can continue if:

  • there are other attorneys
  • they can make individual decisions on behalf of the donor

If the attorneys have to make decisions together and ‘act jointly’, the Lasting Power of Attorney will come to an end.

A property and financial affairs Lasting Power of Attorney will end if the attorney becomes bankrupt, unless there are other attorneys in place.

Part 8: If you have an Enduring Power of Attorney

Enduring Powers of Attorney have been replaced by Lasting Powers of Attorney.

They can still be used if they were made and signed before October 2007.

You must register the Enduring Power of Attorney if the donor is losing, or has lost the ability to make their own decisions.

To do this, fill in the forms below:

Download ‘Notice of intention to apply for registration of an Enduring Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 140KB)

Download ‘Application for registration of an Enduring Power of Attorney’ (PDF, 310KB)

Download ‘guidance notes for completing the application for registration form’ (PDF, 143KB)

Cancel an Enduring Power of Attorney

If you’re the donor, you can cancel an unregistered Enduring Power of Attorney at any time.

If it has been registered, you’ll need to get the permission of the Court of Protection.

You’ll need to prove that you’re able to make your own decisions.
