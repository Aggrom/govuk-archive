Fixed penalties and vehicle immobilisation for commercial drivers, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: The fixed penalty scheme

If you’re a commercial driver, you could be stopped by an officer from the Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA) or the police to inspect your vehicle.

They’ll also make sure you’re not committing an offence. Examples of this can include:

  • driving an overloaded vehicle
  • breaking the rules on drivers’ hours and tachographs

VOSA officers and the police can issue fixed penalties if you commit an offence. Some of these are graduated depending on the circumstances and seriousness of the offence.

Example

If your vehicle exceeds its maximum permitted axle weight, the penalties are:

Vehicle overweight by Penalty
5% to 10% £60
10% to 15% £120
15% to 30% £200
more than 30% court summons

For drivers’ hours offences, the penalties are:

Maximum daily driving time exceeded by Penalty
up to an hour £60
1 to 2 hours £120
2 or more hours £200

VOSA has published a guide to fixed penalties as well as a policy for road traffic offences, which includes a list of offences and penalty fine levels.

Endorsements to your driving licence

For some offences, you’ll get points added to your licence (endorsements) as well as a fine. For example, if your vehicle has defective brakes, you’ll be given a £60 fine and 3 points on your licence.

If the offence is endorsable, you must present your driving licence within 14 days.

Part 2: Fixed penalty notices

If you have a UK address, you’ll have 28 days from when you’re given a fixed penalty notice to either:

  • pay the fine
  • ask for a court hearing if you want to appeal

In Scotland, you’ll get a ‘conditional offer’ instead of a fixed penalty notice. You get 28 days from the date of issue to pay the fine. After this, you could be prosecuted if you don’t pay.

If you don’t have a UK address

You’ll have to pay a ‘financial penalty deposit’. This will be either:

  • the total of all fixed penalty notices issued (if you want to appeal, you must do so within 28 days)
  • £300 per offence if you go to court (this depends on the seriousness and number of offences - you’ll have to pay on the spot and and go to court later)

In both cases the maximum amount you’ll have to pay as a deposit is £900. You have to pay this straight away - if you don’t, you might not be allowed to drive your vehicle.

The money from deposits is used to pay any fixed penalties or court fines.

You’ll be refunded any money left over from your deposit after fines have been paid.

If you don’t pay the deposit, your vehicle will be immobilised.

Part 3: What happens if your vehicle is immobilised

If you’re stopped by a Vehicle and Operator Services Agency (VOSA) officer or the police, they might immobilise your vehicle.

This might happen if you’ve committed a serious enough offence to get an ‘immediate prohibition’.

An immediate prohibition is used to prevent risks to road safety (eg an unroadworthy vehicle or a tired driver). It means you won’t be able to drive your vehicle until the problem is sorted out.

When your vehicle could be immobilised

VOSA or the police can also immobilise your vehicle at the roadside if an immediate prohibiton is issued for any of the following reasons:

  • you’ve broken the rules on drivers’ hours and tachographs
  • your vehicle isn’t roadworthy
  • your vehicle is overloaded
  • you’ve been given a fixed penalty notice but can’t or won’t pay a financial penalty deposit

Not all vehicles given an immediate prohibition will be immobilised. Special circumstances will be considered, eg:

  • the type of load you’re carrying
  • if you’re carrying passengers on a public service vehicle who’d be inconvenienced if it was immobilised

How your vehicle is immobilised

VOSA and the police use a steel cable secured by a padlock to immobilise vehicles. It’s fitted around the wheels of the vehicle and a warning notice is attached to the vehicle.

The notice tells you how to get the vehicle released. You have to:

  • satisfy VOSA that the causes of the immediate prohibitions have been dealt with
  • pay an £80 release charge

It’s an offence to remove the warning notice or interfere with the immobilising device.

VOSA has produced a guide to vehicle immobilisation with more information, including advice on how to avoid it.
