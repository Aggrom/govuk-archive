Offshore cables and pipelines

You need consent to lay cables and pipelines on or under the seabed; power cables may need a licence, dredging will need a pre-sweep licence.

Introduction

Laying cables and pipelines in the marine environment usually requires consent and/or a licence. Cables that are laid on or under the seabed require consent under the Coast Protection Act 1949 (CPA) or the Telecommunications Act 1984. But they are usually exempt from the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 (FEPA) licensing requirements unless they are power cables over a certain capacity.

Sub-sea pipelines usually require CPA consent and authorisation under the Petroleum Act, but normally they are exempt from FEPA licensing.

When cables and pipelines are laid, a FEPA licence may be required for related works like rock armouring or mattressing. If dredged material is deposited anywhere other than a designated disposal site, a FEPA ‘pre-sweep’ licence is required.

This guide outlines the consents and licences you need depending on your activities. It also explains how to apply for them, how much they cost and the factors to consider before applying.

Laying cables

Before laying cables in tidal waters you will need to obtain consent under Section 34 of the CPA. If you are a ‘code operator’ - which means that Ofcom has applied the Electronic Communications Code to you - then you need to get consent under the Telecommunications Act 1984 rather than CPA consent.

Limits of the consents

CPA consent covers the entire length of the cable across the UK continental shelf. Telecommunications Act consent only applies to the 12 nautical mile limit of UK territorial waters. But if the route of the cable passes within 500 metres of an offshore installation, then the consent extends to the UK continental shelf.

Cables and FEPA licensing

Most sub-sea cables are exempt from the FEPA licensing requirements. But you may need to get a FEPA construction licence for any works associated with laying the cable.

The cable you are laying may be an integral part of a larger scheme, such as an offshore power generation project. If a FEPA construction licence is required for the scheme then the terms of the licence must cover the laying of the cable.

Since April 2010, power cables are no longer exempt from FEPA licensing. So you need a FEPA licence for offshore transmission cables of 132 kilovolts capacity or more, and associated equipment used to transmit electricity from a generating station constructed on the sea bed - for example an offshore wind farm.

You can check the cost of a FEPA construction licence on the Marine Management Organisation (MMO) website.

The future of marine licensing

From spring 2011, the marine licensing system will be simplified and the existing CPA and FEPA systems will be replaced by a single licensing system. The new system will also remove the requirement to obtain separate consent under the Telecommunications Act. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Further consents

Offshore generating stations of 1 megawatt or more installed capacity (or 50 megawatts if they are in the Renewable Energy Zone) need consent under Section 36 of the Electricity Act. Section 36 consent includes the ‘inter-array’ cable between generating devices. Download guidance on the offshore wind farm consents process from the Department of Energy and Climate Change (DECC) website (PDF, 211K).

If you are planning to lay a cable across the inter-tidal zone, you are likely to need further consents such as:

  • approval from the Environment Agency
  • approval from the land owner - in many cases this will be the Crown Estate
  • planning permission

Laying sub-sea pipelines

When you lay sub-sea pipelines you must have a relevant and valid authorisation under the Petroleum Act 1988. These are issued by DECC. The authorisation also covers deposits into the sea of material associated with laying a pipeline, so you don’t need to obtain a separate licence for this.

As well as Petroleum Act authorisation, you also normally need to obtain consent under the CPA.

Pipelines and FEPA licensing

Laying sub-sea pipelines is generally exempt from the FEPA licensing requirements as long as you have a valid Petroleum Act authorisation. But if you also lay protection for the pipeline - rock armouring or mattressing, for example - you will usually need a FEPA licence.

Pipelines that are not used for oil and gas - for example intakes and outfall pipes for effluent or cooling waters - need a FEPA licence and CPA consent, rather than Petroleum Act authorisation.

The future of marine licensing

From spring 2011 the marine licensing system will be simplified and the existing CPA and FEPA systems will be replaced by a single licensing system. Oil and gas sub-sea pipelines will continue to be regulated by the Petroleum Act but other types of pipeline will be brought within the new marine licensing regime. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Further consents

If you are planning to lay a pipeline or outfall across the inter-tidal zone, you are likely to need further consents such as:

  • approval from the Environment Agency
  • approval from the land owner - in many cases this will be the Crown Estate
  • planning permission

Pre-sweep, abandoned well and pipeline licences

Although works associated with laying pipelines are exempt from FEPA licensing, if you re-deposit material at sea anywhere other than at an existing designated disposal site, you need a FEPA ‘pre-sweep’ licence.

Work carried out to older pipelines

Maintenance work on pipelines installed after the Petroleum and Submarine Pipelines Act 1975 (superseded by the Petroleum Act 1998) was enacted is exempt from FEPA licensing. But all work carried out on pipelines installed before then needs a FEPA licence.

Abandoned wells and pipelines

Depositing material once a pipeline or associated structure is decommissioned also requires a FEPA licence. For example, you may need to seal a well or pipe, or dump rock to prevent scour or minimise hazards to others.

You can check the cost of FEPA licences to carry out work to pipelines on the MMO website.

The future of marine licensing

From spring 2011 the marine licensing system will be simplified and the existing FEPA licensing system will be replaced by a single licensing system. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

CPA consents and FEPA licences

The current system of CPA consents and FEPA licensing is used to protect the offshore environment.

CPA consents

CPA consent is required for:

  • the construction, alteration or improvement of any works that are on, under or over any part of the seashore lying below the level of mean high water springs
  • the deposit of any object or materials below the level of mean high water springs
  • the removal of any object or materials from the seashore below the level of mean low water springs - for example by dredging

CPA consent normally lasts for three years. However, if you need a FEPA licence as well as CPA consent, you can choose to have the duration of the consent changed so that they both expire at the same time. You can find out about CPA jurisdiction and what’s considered before consent is granted on the MMO website.

FEPA licences

FEPA licences are mainly used to permit construction within the marine environment and to control deposits of articles into the sea or tidal waters. FEPA licences for works associated with laying cables and pipelines usually last for one year. You can get details of the types of construction work a FEPA licence is needed for on the MMO website.

FEPA licence exemptions

There is a range of activities that involve depositing materials into the sea which are exempt from the FEPA licensing requirements. These include deposits of:

  • scientific instruments
  • fishing gear
  • material at an oil or gas drilling site

You can read a detailed list of exemptions from FEPA licensing on the MMO website.

The future of marine licensing

From spring 2011, the marine licensing system will be simplified and the existing CPA and FEPA systems will be replaced by a single licensing system. You can find out about the new marine licensing system on the MMO website.

Applying for CPA consent and FEPA licences

Before you apply for a FEPA licence and/or CPA consent you need to decide if you have to:

  • contact the MMO - for large, novel or contentious projects you’re advised to get in touch with the MMO before you apply
  • provide an environmental statement - you can find out about environmental impact assessments on the MMO website
  • consider any ‘beneficial use’ - you can get information about FEPA licences for disposing of material in a beneficial way on the MMO website

Conservation regulations

Depending on the location of your proposed works, your application may be subject to either of the following:

  • conservation regulations that apply to works within 12 nautical miles offshore - find out about protected conservation areas on the MMO website
  • offshore marine conservation regulations that apply to works beyond 12 nautical miles - read about European protected sites on the MMO website

Fees

The cost of a FEPA licence varies depending on its type. The fee charged can also depend on the licence band, which is decided by the overall cost of your construction project. You can find out how much your FEPA licence will be on the MMO website.

The application process

You can apply for CPA consent and/or a FEPA licence by:

  • filling in an online application form
  • downloading - or requesting - an application form and sending the completed form to the MMO

You can get application forms and find out how to apply for CPA consent and FEPA licences on the MMO website.
