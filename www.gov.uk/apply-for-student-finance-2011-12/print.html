Student finance 2011 to 2012, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Tuition fees

If your course starts before 1 September 2012 (academic year 2011 to 2012), you won’t pay the new higher tuition fees.

If your course starts from 1 September 2012 (academic year 2012 to 2013) you’ll pay higher tuition fees and will get the new student finance package for 2012 to 2013.

How much you’ll pay

If your course starts before 1 September 2012 UK universities and colleges can charge:

  • up to £3,375 a year for full-time students
  • no limit for part-time students
  • no limit if you go to a private university or college

Apply for student finance 2011 to 2012.

Part 2: Tuition fees - student loans and grants

Full-time students

If you’re a new or continuing full-time student you can apply for a Tuition Fee Loan to help with the cost of tuition.

The maximum Tuition Fee Loan for new and returning full-time students applying for 2011/12 is £3,375.

Tuition Fee Loans are paid directly to your university or college.

Loans may not cover the full cost of your tuition if you’re studying at a private university or college.

Part-time students

If you’re a part-time student applying for 2011 to 2012 you need to apply for a Fee Grant.

This is paid directly to your university or college and doesn’t have to be paid back.

How much you get depends on your circumstances, household income and course ‘intensity’ (how long the course takes to complete compared with an equivalent full-time course).

Part 3: Living costs - student loans and grants

Student loans and grants are available to help with living costs.

Full-time students

Full-time students can get the following:

  • Maintenance Loan
  • Maintenance Grant

Maintenance Loan

The Maintenance Loan has to be repaid. How much you get depends on:

  • your household income
  • where you live
  • how much Maintenance Grant you get
  • whether you’re in your final year (when you get slightly less)

You can get 72% of the loan without having your household income assessed - to get the full loan your household income needs to be assessed.

You can get the following:

Where you live when studying The maximum loan
At home £3,838
Away from home outside London £4,950
Away from home in London £6,928

The money is paid to the university or college at the beginning of term once you’ve registered on your course.

Maintenance Grant

The Maintenance Grant doesn’t have to be repaid. How much you get depends on your household income so you need to have this assessed when you apply.

You can get:

Household income Maintenance Grant
Under £25,000 £2,906
£25,001-£50,020 Partial grant
Over £50,020 No grant

If you get a Maintenance Grant you get a smaller Maintenance Loan, meaning you have less to repay.

The money is paid into your bank account each term once you’ve registered on your course.

Download ‘A guide to financial support for continuing full-time students’ (PDF, 100KB)

Part-time students

Part-time students can apply for a Course Grant.

How much you get depends on:

  • your household income
  • whether you have children
  • whether you have a partner

The Course Grant is paid into your bank account each term once you’ve registered on your course.

If you’re single without children you can get up to:

Household income Course Grant
Up to £26,029 £265
£26,030-£28,065 £50-£265
Over £28,065 No grant

If you have a partner or children you can still get the grant if you earn an extra:

  • £2,000 if you have a partner whose income is taken into account
  • £2,000 for the first of your or your partner’s dependent children
  • £1,000 for any other dependent children

Part 4: Extra financial help

If you have children

If you have children you could get the following depending on your circumstances:

  • Childcare Grant
  • Parents’ Learning Allowance
  • Access to Learning Fund
  • Child Tax Credit

If you’re disabled

If you’re disabled you could get the following depending on your circumstances:

  • Disabled Students’ Allowances
  • Access to Learning Fund
  • Disability Living Allowance
  • Employment and Support Allowance
  • Incapacity Benefit

You may qualify for this help if you have a:

  • long-term illness
  • mental-health condition
  • specific learning difficulty like dyslexia

If you’re in financial hardship

If you’re in financial hardship you could get Access to Learning Fund.

Part 5: Bursaries and scholarships

You may be able to get a bursary from your university or college to help with tuition fees and living costs.

Who qualifies is decided by the university or college. Bursaries don’t have to be paid back.

If you get the full Maintenance Grant and your tuition fees are £3,375 you can get a bursary of at least £338.

Contact your university or college to apply or find out more.

Part 6: EU students

Full-time students

Full-time students can apply for a Tuition Fee Loan of up to £3,375 a year to help with tuition costs.

This is paid direct to the university or college and has to be paid back.

Download ‘EU11N - Tuition Fee Loan Application Form for new full-time EU students’ (PDF, 212KB)

Download ‘EUPR1a - Tuition Fee Loan Application Form for continuing full-time EU students’ (PDF, 120KB)

Part-time students

Part-time students can apply for a Fee Grant of up to £1,230 a year to help with tuition costs.

This is paid directly to the university or college and doesn’t have to be paid back.

How much you get depends on:

  • your household income
  • whether you have a partner
  • whether you have children
  • the ‘intensity’ of your course (how long it takes to complete compared to a full-time course)

If you’re a part-time EU student you need to apply for a Fee Grant using form EUPTG1.

You need to enrol on your course before you send the form - your university or college need to stamp and sign it first.

Part 7: Repayments

When repayments start

You start paying back your student loan in the April after leaving your course when you earn more than £15,000 a year. The threshold will increase to £15,795 from 6 April 2012.

If your income falls below £15,000 a year, your repayments stop.

Different rules apply if your course starts after 1 September 2012 - see paying back your student loan.

How much interest you pay

You’re charged interest on your loan from the first payment until the loan is paid back in full. This means the amount you pay back is more than the amount you took out in loans.

For the year starting 1 September 2011 the interest rate is 1.5% a year.

Your monthly repayments

You pay 9% of your income over £15,000.

Example: if you earn £21,000 this is £6,000 over the £15,000 threshold. You pay 9% of £6,000 which is £540 per year or £45 per month.

Your income per year Monthly repayments (including interest)
Up to £15,000 No repayments
£16,000 £7.50
£21,000 £45
£25,000 £75
£30,000 £112.50
£40,000 £187.50
£50,000 £262.50
£60,000 £337.50

How you repay your loan

If you’re an employee

Your employer will work out your repayment and take it off your salary every month along with your tax through Pay As You Earn (PAYE). Find out more about repaying your loan through PAYE.

Check your pay slip to see how much has been taken to pay your student loan.

If you haven’t worked since leaving your course or earn less than £15,000, you won’t make any repayments.

If you’re self-employed

You need to work out and make your own repayments if you earn more than £15,000 a year.

This is like working out your tax and National Insurance payments. Find out more about repaying your loan through self assessment.

If you move abroad

If you’ll be abroad for more than 3 months, you need to contact the Student Loans Company who will calculate how much you need to repay.

You need to make any repayments from abroad directly to the Student Loans Company each month.

Find out how much you owe

You’ll get statements each year after you leave your course from the Student Loans Company and can check your balance on the Student Loans Company repayment website.
