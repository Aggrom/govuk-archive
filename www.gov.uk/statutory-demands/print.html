Part 1: About statutory demands

Statutory demands are a formal way of asking for a debt to be paid by an individual or a business. They state:

  • how much must be paid
  • when payment must be made
  • what will happen if the demand is ignored
  • details of who the debtor can contact about the demand
  • the debtor’s right to dispute (disagree with) the demand

An individual or business that receives a statutory demand has 21 days to:

  • settle the debt
  • secure the debt - reach an agreement for payment

You can take out a bankruptcy or winding up petition if the debt remains unpaid from a statutory demand. The total debt must be more than £750. A number of creditors for smaller amounts can put their claims together to reach this minimum.

If you want to ask the court to ‘set aside’ (dismiss) the demand you must do this within 18 days of the date it was served.

Part 3: How to serve a statutory demand

You must do everything you can to bring the statutory demand to the debtor’s attention. This is called ‘serving’ the statutory demand.

To serve an individual or sole trader

You can serve it personally or employ a process server to do it for you.

If the debtor is avoiding service, you can:

  • send the demand by first class post or put it through their letterbox

  • advertise the statutory demand in a newspaper if the money owed is under a judgment or order of court

To serve a registered limited company

You can serve a statutory demand by:

  • delivering it to the company’s registered office
  • sending it by registered post

To serve an unregistered limited company

You can serve the statutory demand by:

  • leaving it at the company’s main place of business
  • delivering it to the company secretary, manager or principal officer of the company
  • serving it in a way directed or approved by the court

Proof of service forms

Part 4: How to challenge a statutory demand

If you don’t agree with a statutory demand, you can apply to the court to have it set aside (dismissed). You must do this within 18 days of receiving it or when was advertised in a newspaper. If you are abroad, the time limit is 22 days.

  1. Complete Form 6.4 – application to set aside a statutory demand (DOC, 40KB) and Form 6.5 – statement of truth (DOC, 40KB) to confirm that your application is true and correct.

  2. The forms need to be filed (presented) at a court. Your solicitor or local county court can tell you which one to use.

  3. The court will consider your application and either dismiss it immediately or fix a date for hearing your case. If accepted, the time detailed in the demand to satisfy the debt will be suspended.

  4. At the hearing the court can agree to set aside the statutory demand or dismiss your case. If dismissed, your creditor can apply to the court to make you bankrupt and the time detailed in the demand for you to satisfy the debt will restart.

The steps for a limited company are different. You should get legal advice on how to set aside a statutory demand.
