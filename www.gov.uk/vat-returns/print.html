VAT Returns, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Usually, you send a VAT Return to HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) every 3 months. This period of time is known as your ‘accounting period.’

The VAT Return records things for the accounting period like:

  • your total sales and purchases
  • the amount of VAT you owe
  • the amount of VAT you can reclaim
  • what your VAT refund from HMRC is

You must send a VAT Return even if you have no VAT to pay or reclaim.

Final VAT Returns

You have to send a final VAT Return when you cancel your VAT registration. You can usually do this online using your VAT online account.

If your registration is cancelled because you’re insolvent, HMRC will send you a paper version to complete.

Part 2: Fill out your return

HM Revenue & Customs has guidance on:

  • filling in your VAT Return
  • Flat Rate Scheme VAT Returns
  • working out the VAT on VAT inclusive and exclusive prices
  • correcting a VAT Return

Working out what you can claim

There are different rules if your business is a charity or not-for-profit organisation.

Generally, VAT-registered businesses can reclaim the VAT that they’ve paid on business purchases and expenses. The claim must be for a business activity (you have to work out the business element if it also had a personal use).

You must keep records to support your claim and show the VAT was paid.

Exceptions

You can’t reclaim the VAT:

  • on entertainment expenses
  • on goods bought from other EU countries
  • on purchases if you use the VAT Flat Rate Scheme, (except assets worth more than £2,000)

HMRC has guidance on these exceptions and working out how to reclaim VAT for:

  • cars - eg buying, repairing, fuel costs
  • staff travel expenses - eg accommodation and transport expenses
  • reclaiming VAT if your business is partly exempt from VAT

Estimated figures

If you can’t give accurate figures on your VAT Return, ask HMRC for permission to use estimated figures.

If you’re allowed, you won’t be charged a penalty unless you miss the deadline or make a careless or deliberate error.

Bad debts

You can reclaim the VAT you’ve paid HMRC but not received from a customer if it’s a ‘bad debt’ (one you’ve written off). To qualify for the relief:

  • the debt must be between 6 months and 4 years and 6 months old
  • you mustn’t have sold the debt on
  • you mustn’t have charged more than the normal price for the item

You should reclaim them via your VAT Return (add them to your Box 4 figure) and keep records about the debt.

If the debt is paid, you must pay the relief back via your VAT Return (add the amount to your Box 1 figure).

Part 3: Send your return

All returns must be sent online.

To send your VAT Return online you need a VAT number and a VAT online account (sometimes known as a ‘Government Gateway account’). You can then send the VAT Return using HMRC’s free online service or commercial software.

Getting online

If you need:

  • a VAT number and online account - register for VAT
  • an online account - sign up for an online account and select option ‘VAT submit returns’
  • a VAT number - log in to your online account and apply for a VAT number

HMRC’s free online service

Log in to your VAT online account and complete the VAT Return or view a demonstration.

Software you can buy

HMRC has a list of software you can buy to send your VAT Return.

Keep any reference number you receive as proof you’ve sent your return.

Using accountants or agents

You’ll need to authorise them before they can send your VAT Return. To do this:

  • download form 64-8 ‘Authorising your agent’
  • authorise them through your VAT online account

Part 4: Pay your VAT

You must pay VAT to HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) electronically, eg direct debit or internet banking. Most businesses aren’t allowed to pay by cheque.

Usually, you pay the VAT at the same time as sending your VAT Return.

Contact HMRC if you can’t pay your VAT bill.

Part 5: Deadlines

Check your VAT Return and payment deadlines and sign up for email reminders in your VAT online account.

Your VAT online account tells you when your VAT Returns are due and when the payment must clear HM Revenue & Custom’s (HMRC) account.

The deadline for sending the return online and paying HMRC are usually the same - 1 calendar month and 7 days after the end of an accounting period.

You can work out your payment deadline based on your payment method (eg CHAPS, direct debit or Bacs).

Exceptions

The deadlines are different if:

  • you use the VAT Annual Accounting Scheme
  • HMRC lets you send paper VAT Returns
  • HMRC lets you make payments on account (advance payments)

Part 6: Surcharges and penalties

If your VAT Return or payment is late it’s known as a ‘default’.

If you default more than once in 12 months you may enter a ‘surcharge period’. Defaulting in a surcharge period means:

  • the surcharge period is extended by 12 months
  • you may have to pay a penalty (known as a ‘surcharge’)

If you default, HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) will write explaining any penalties and what happens if you default again.

Penalties

Penalties are usually a percentage of the unpaid tax. HMRC estimate this if they don’t have your return - this is known as an ‘assessment’.

Exceptions

There’s no penalty if you send a late VAT Return and you have no tax to pay or you’re due a VAT repayment.

Penalties for incorrect VAT Returns

If your VAT Return is inaccurate HMRC can charge you a penalty (between 15% and 100% of the unpaid owed).

Download ‘Penalties for inaccurate tax returns’ (PDF, 200KB)

Interest

You may be charged interest on any tax you owe.

Penalties for sending a paper VAT Return

If you have to send your return online, you’ll be charged a penalty if you send a paper return.

Part 7: Assessments

HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) can send you a VAT ‘assessment’ for example:

  • if you’re late with a VAT Return
  • your VAT Return has an error on it

The assessment will explain why HMRC are contacting you and estimate the VAT you have to pay. It will also tell you the deadline for responding (usually within 30 days).

If the assessment is too low, pay it and tell HMRC or you’ll be charged a penalty.

You can’t appeal an assessment (eg because it’s too high), instead you must send HMRC your VAT Return showing the amount of tax due and make the payment.
