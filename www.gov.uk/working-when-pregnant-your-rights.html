Quick answer Working when pregnant: your rights

If you’re a pregnant employee you have legal rights - including paid time off for antenatal care, maternity leave and maternity pay.

Pregnant employees have 4 key rights:

  • paid time off for antenatal care
  • maternity leave
  • maternity pay
  • protection against unfair treatment, discrimination or dismissal

Your employer can’t change your terms and conditions while you’re pregnant without your agreement - they’re in breach of contract if they do.

It’s illegal for your employer to refuse to give you time off for antenatal care or refuse to pay you at your normal rate for it, but fathers don’t have a legal right to time off to accompany their partners.

Pregnancy-related illnesses

If you’re off work for a pregnancy-related illness in the 4 weeks before your baby is due, your maternity leave and Statutory Maternity Pay will start automatically - it doesn’t matter what you’ve agreed with your employer.

Compulsory maternity leave

Even if you’re not taking Statutory Maternity Leave, you must take 2 weeks off after your baby is born - or 4 weeks if you work in a factory.

Telling your employer you’re pregnant

You must tell your employer that you’re pregnant at least 15 weeks before the beginning of the week your baby is due.

If this isn’t possible - eg because you didn’t know you were pregnant - you must tell them as soon as possible.

You also need to tell your employer when you want to start your Statutory Maternity Leave and Pay.

You can use the Maternity Leave calculator to work out when you can take your leave.

You can’t take time off for antenatal appointments until you’ve told your employer you’re pregnant.

Health and safety for pregnant employees

When you tell your employer you’re pregnant, they should prevent you from being exposed to any risks to protect you and your baby.

Risks could be caused by:

  • heavy lifting or carrying
  • standing or sitting for long periods without adequate breaks
  • exposure to toxic substances
  • long working hours

If your employer can’t remove any risks (eg by offering you suitable alternative work) they should suspend you on full pay.

If you think you’re at risk and your employer disagrees, talk to your health and safety or trade union representative. If your employer still refuses to do anything, talk to your doctor or call the Health and Safety Executive.
