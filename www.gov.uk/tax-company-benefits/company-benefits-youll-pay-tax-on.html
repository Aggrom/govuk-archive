Part 2: Company benefits you'll pay tax on

You pay tax on the value of the benefit to you.

Company cars

Your company car’s value depends on things like how much it would cost to buy and the type of fuel it uses. Its value is reduced if you have the car part-time or you pay something towards its cost.

If your employer pays for fuel you use personally and not for your job, you’ll pay tax on this separately.

Use the company car fuel benefit calculator on the HM Revenue & Customs website.

Download ‘Car benefits and car fuel benefits helpsheet’ (PDF, 211KB)

Loans

You’ll pay tax on low-interest or interest-free loans from your employer if they’re worth £5,000 or more.

You pay tax on the difference between the interest rate you pay to your employer and the official rate of interest set by the Bank of England.

You may pay tax if your employer lends money to one of your relatives.

Download ‘Tax relief on loans helpsheet’ (PDF, 107KB)

Living accommodation

If you (or one of your relatives) is living in accommodation provided by your employer you may pay tax.

How the tax is worked out depends on whether the accommodation cost more than £75,000.

You may not pay tax if you get the accommodation so you can do your job, or do your job better (eg agricultural workers living on farms).

Download ‘Living accommodation helpsheet’ (PDF, 108KB)

Medical insurance

You usually pay tax on the cost of the insurance premiums if your employer pays for your medical insurance.

You can get some tax-free health benefits from your employer, eg:

  • medical insurance when you’re working abroad
  • annual check-ups

Find out how your employer works out what tax to take for expenses and benefits you get.
