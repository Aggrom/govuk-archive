Minimising farm waste: composting and recycling

How to reduce general farm waste and deal efficiently with animal waste through good waste management.

Introduction

Farm waste can have a significant environmental impact. Keeping farm waste to a minimum will reduce the risk of pollution, as well as the amount of waste going to landfill. Reducing the amount of waste you produce will reduce your costs.

In addition, farmers have environmental responsibilities under Cross Compliance regulations. These aim to protect water quality, local landscape and wildlife and are obligatory for any farmer who receives agricultural payments, though they are also good practice for all farmers and land managers.

This guide will define the different types of farm waste, and tell you what you can do to minimise the waste on your farm by reducing packaging, composting and recycling waste. It will also explain the regulations on disposing of farm waste. You may need to get an environmental permit or to register an exemption from environmental permitting with the Environment Agency for your waste activities.

Minimising waste by reducing energy use

All farmers produce waste in some form or another, and this can be divided into two types of waste - general farm waste and animal waste. Both of these types of waste can be hazardous.

You need to use the waste management hierarchy to determine how to prevent, reduce or most efficiently handle each type of waste your business produces. You must declare on your waste transfer notes and hazardous waste consignment notes that you have applied the waste management hierarchy.

General farm waste

Farm waste that falls into this category includes:

  • feed waste
  • used plastic silage wrapping
  • unused sheep dip
  • used veterinary products
  • animal carcasses

For more information, see the guides on non-hazardous waste: treatment and disposal and landspreading.

Animal waste

You can use animal manures and slurries not classed as animal waste as soil fertiliser. Using them in this way will reduce the amount of animal waste you need to deal with. For more information, see the guides on sewage sludge, slurry and silage and managing nutrients and fertilisers.

Hazardous waste

You should be especially careful how you handle, store and dispose of hazardous waste. For more information, see the guide on hazardous waste: treatment and disposal.

Why reduce farm waste?

There are several reasons for reducing energy use on your farm, including:

  • reducing carbon emissions, which helps counter the effects of global warming
  • making energy supplies more secure
  • saving money and improving competitiveness

The Carbon Trust estimates that reducing energy consumption by 20% is a realistic achievement for most farming businesses. The savings that you can make from this reduction could be the same as a 5% increase in your sales.

Find sector-specific energy use information on the Carbon Trust website.

Tax breaks on energy-saving equipment

You may be able to claim an enhanced capital allowance (ECA) if you buy plant or machinery for your farm which is energy efficient.

Read about tax breaks for energy-efficient equipment on the ECA website.

Minimising waste by preventing pollution

Good environmental management is good for business. Many pollution incidents are avoidable, such as those caused by pesticide leaks or slurry being stored or spread wrongly and running off into a watercourse or drains. You should note that the Environment Agency can take enforcement action for serious offences.

For more information on preventing pollution, read the guides on:

  • air pollution on farms
  • water pollution on farms
  • protecting farm environments: the basics
  • air water and soil quality: the basics

Pollution Prevention Guidelines (PPGs)

The Environment Agency’s pollution prevention guidance can help your business avoid pollution incidents and show you how to deal with them if they do happen. Download a general PPG on how to avoid pollution from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 96K).

Nitrate Vulnerable Zones (NVZs)

If your land falls within an NVZ, there may be additional rules that affect your waste and recycling. For more information, see the guide on Nitrate Vulnerable Zones.

Good practice guidance

The Environment Agency has also produced a best practice guidance pack to help you reduce the risk of causing environmental pollution. The guide includes advice on:

  • site drainage
  • deliveries and handling
  • waste management
  • trade effluent
  • groundwater
  • training and emergencies

You can download guidance on industrial and commercial pollution prevention from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 863K).

Cross Compliance

Any action that you or your farming business takes to prevent pollution should always be seen within the context of Cross Compliance. Cross Compliance requirements apply to you if you receive payments under Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) support schemes or certain rural development schemes.

There are currently three components to Cross Compliance:

  • specific European legal requirements, known as Statutory Management Requirements (SMRs)
  • domestic legal requirements requiring you to keep your land in good agricultural and environmental condition (GAEC)
  • the protection of permanent pasture is a standard according to which member states must ensure a certain ratio of permanent pasture to total agricultural area. If the prescribed ratio cannot be maintained, member states must act - eg by requiring farmers to apply for authorisation to convert permanent pasture or by requiring farmers claiming the single payment to reconvert land back to permanent pasture

You should be aware that your payments may be reduced or stopped if you do not comply with these requirements.

Anaerobic digestion

Anaerobic digestion (AD) is the breakdown of organic matter by bacteria in the absence of oxygen. This natural process can create:

  • biogas - renewable energy source for heat and power
  • biomethane - biogas that has had the impurities, carbon dioxide and water removed so it can be used as a fuel for vehicles
  • digestate - nutrient-rich organic fertiliser or soil conditioner

You can house an AD system on-farm or use a larger, centrally located facility known as a Centralised Anaerobic Digestion facility.

How AD works

Organic material is stored in an airless container, which acts as a digester. Bacteria take 20-40 days to break the material down and biogas is created, leaving a low-odour recyclable by-product called ‘digestate’.

There are many kinds of organic matter that can be used in AD, such as:

  • animal manure and slurries
  • silage, maize and grass leys
  • food waste and by-products
  • crop residues
  • biodegradable domestic waste
  • sewage

The benefits and risks of AD

In the UK, millions of tonnes of agricultural material and sewage sludge are produced every year. AD offers farmers the potential to use their waste to cut costs and generate income in a number of ways, for example through:

  • renewable energy production - heating farm and outbuildings
  • diversification - becoming a local waste recovery site
  • reducing greenhouse gas emissions
  • the sale of surplus energy
  • the use and sale of digestate
  • obtaining development support

There are risks associated with AD, for example the:

  • high setup costs
  • regular organic material supply needs
  • unintended gas leaks
  • complex and expensive electric generator connection
  • planning and local objection to infrastructure
  • need for controlled digestate application

Permission and permits

There are a number of steps that you will need to complete to start AD. For example you may need:

  • planning permission.
  • an environmental permit or an exemption for the operation of the AD plant. Find information about exemption T24 for AD on the Environment Agency website.
  • a waste carrier registration if you are collecting or transporting someone else’s waste. Apply for a permit for an on-farm AD facility on the Environment Agency website.
  • authorisation under the Animal By-Products regulations.

Quality protocol for AD

If your digestate meets the requirements of the Environment Agency’s quality protocol, it will be considered a product and will not be subject to waste controls. Find information about the AD quality protocol on the Environment Agency website.

Composting biodegradable waste

Most farm composts and digestates are made up of either green waste - eg hedge trimmings and tree prunings - or livestock manures and slurries. Composting farmyard manures can help concentrate nutrients, increasing efficiency.

Composting reduces the amount of biodegradable waste going to landfills and returns organic matter to the soil, reducing the need for inorganic fertilisers. Composting involves active management, such as turning the heaps and maintaining the best conditions for the waste to break down.

Registering an exemption from environmental permitting for composting waste

You may need to register an exemption from environmental permitting with the Environment Agency if you:

  • compost waste from your own farm, eg plant waste, horse and farmyard manure
  • anaerobically digest farmyard manures and slurries
  • compost off-farm waste, eg garden waste collected by your local authority

New environmental permitting regulations came into force on 6 April 2010. If you registered an exemption before 6 April 2010 (paragraph 12 exemption for composting agricultural waste), you will have time to prepare for these changes.

You must re-register your exemption under the new system by:

  • 1 October 2011, if you are importing waste (agricultural or non-agricultural) for composting on agricultural land
  • 1 October 2013, if you are composting agricultural waste on agricultural land

It is free to register all but one of the exemptions with the Environment Agency. You will however need to renew your exemption every three years.

Read guidance about exemptions for aerobic composting on your farm on the Environment Agency website.

Read guidance about exemptions for anaerobic composting on your farm on the Environment Agency website.

For more information on composting on-site call the Environment Agency Agricultural Waste Helpline on: 0845 603 3113.

Check the conditions of your composting exemption

You must meet the conditions of your exemption. The limits for composting have changed, so you need to check if you still comply. If you cannot meet the new conditions, you may be able to apply for a standard rules environmental permit.

The standard permits are made up of one or more sets of rules giving operators various options to choose from to meet defined outcomes or levels of environmental protection.

While standard permits save time and money, you can only use one if you meet all the criteria set out in that permit. If an inspection shows that you are operating under an inappropriate standard permit, your business will be deemed to be acting without a permit at all.

If there is no standard rule set available which matches your operation or if you are unable to comply with the standard rules, you will need to apply for a bespoke permit.

If you need an environmental permit, you must:

  • pay an application fee, and an annual subsistence fee
  • demonstrate appropriate planning permission from your local authority
  • prove your technical competence

See the guidance on standard permits for composting biodegradable waste on the Environment Agency website.

Follow the quality protocol for compost

If your compost meets the requirements of the Environment Agency’s quality protocol for compost, it will no longer be classified as a waste, so it can be used without waste management controls.

Find out about the compost quality protocol on the Environment Agency website.

Disposing of waste and fly-tipping

It is illegal to bury waste on your farm without an environmental permit from the Environment Agency. Getting such a permit is an impractical and expensive solution for most farmers.

Laws and a landfill tax have reduced the amount of waste being sent to landfill sites, reducing their impact on health and the environment.

Disposing of, or recycling waste legitimately

To dispose of waste legitimately, you can either:

  • take or send it for disposal or recycling at an authorised site
  • register an exemption from environmental permitting with the Environment Agency to recycle waste on-farm

To register, you can call the Environment Agency Agricultural Waste Helpline on: 0845 603 3113.

Duty of care

You have a legal responsibility to ensure your business waste is handled safely by authorised personnel only. This is known as your duty of care.

Fly-tipping

Fly-tipping is the illegal dumping of waste on land that is not a licensed disposal site. Fly-tipping poses a threat to health and the environment and is expensive to clean up.

As a farmer, you may become a victim of fly-tipping. If you see someone fly-tipping on your land:

  • don’t approach them - they may turn violent
  • don’t touch the waste - it could be hazardous
  • make a note of the date, time, location, a description of the waste and any vehicle involved
  • report the incident to your local authority and the Environment Agency Incident Hotline on: 0800 80 70 60

Find contact details for your local authority through our Contacts Directory.

As well as it being illegal to fly-tip, there are also some other rules that apply, including the following:

  • it is an offence to permit or authorise fly-tipping
  • the driver and/or owner of a vehicle used for fly-tipping can be prosecuted, and police can seize the vehicle

Both the Environment Agency and local authorities have powers to tackle fly-tipping. The Environment Agency investigates larger-scale incidents involving organised gangs of fly-tippers.

Giving or receiving waste for composting

If someone else takes your waste for composting, then you should:

  • check they can prove that they are authorised to carry waste
  • ensure that the waste is being taken to a properly authorised site
  • give them a transfer note including a description of the waste, signed by both parties, and keep copies for at least two years

If you take waste from someone else for composting on your farm, then you should:

  • make sure that you are allowed by law to take it - you can check this by contacting the Environment Agency Helpline on: 03708 506 506
  • check that the person giving you the waste has described it in writing
  • ensure that both parties have completed and signed a transfer note

You should also note that catering waste and animal by-products must not be brought onto premises where livestock are kept.

Storing and managing waste for composting

The Environmental Protection (Duty of Care) Regulations state that compost material must be stored securely and kept safe against accidental spilling or leaking. You should not allow liquids to escape into watercourses, and you must keep stored liquid waste in a sealed container for recycling or disposal.

Dealing with packaging waste and other plastics

Waste from packaging and other plastics can be a pollution risk and can add to the amount of material for landfill. Packaging materials include plastics, paper, glass and metals. Plastic waste includes silage wrap, crop cover and tree guards.

Packaging waste intended for composting must be biodegradable and must not interfere with the collection of waste or the composting process itself. To be biodegradable, the packaging must decompose into carbon dioxide, biomass and water.

The key issues regarding packaging waste you must be aware of include:

  • reducing packaging - you can save money and prevent waste by reducing the amount of packaging you use, eg by ordering supplies in larger containers. Download Defra waste minimisation tips from the ADLib website (PDF, 725K).
  • reusing materials - you can reuse plastics and packaging. For example, you can use last year’s silage sheet for lining the clamp shoulder or as a groundsheet.
  • recycling of materials
  • disposing of packaging - the Duty of Care applies to everyone involved in handling the waste your farm produces

Packaging regulations

The Producer Responsibility Obligations (Packaging Waste) Regulations apply to you if your business handles more than 50 tonnes of packaging in a year and has a turnover of more than £2 million. These Regulations mean you must:

  • register with the Environment Agency
  • pay to recover and recycle specified tonnages of packaging waste
  • supply evidence that you have met your obligations

If you think you may have to comply with the Producer Responsibility Obligations, you can call the Environment Agency National Waste Registration Unit on: 020 7091 4036.

Recycling farm waste

By recycling farm waste, you can:

  • reduce the risk of pollution
  • reduce the amount of waste going to landfill
  • save money
  • comply with legislation

On-farm waste recycling

Many materials used on the farm can be recycled. For example:

  • scrap metal can be collected from the farm - provided the contractor is registered to carry waste
  • demolition rubble can be useful for building farm tracks - as long as it is free of asbestos
  • most tyre dealers and fitters will take back used tyres and tyre cases
  • you may be able to take batteries, waste oil and paper to a recycling facility, or have it collected by a registered waste contractor
  • waste oil can be used for protecting machinery stored over the winter
  • clean silage sheet off-cuts should be stored separately, and your supplier may provide a take-back scheme
  • waste plastics, such as used silage wrap, can be recycled
  • water from dairy processing may be recycled for other farm uses
  • some unwanted vegetable and crop produce can be used for livestock or pet food
  • your veterinary surgeon may take back used needles and syringes

You can also find contact details for local waste recycling centres on the NetRegs Waste Directory website.

It is also very important that waste for recycling is handled correctly. The correct procedure includes:

  • separating waste by type - eg plastics and metals
  • separating different plastics - eg polyethylene and polypropylene
  • separating different metals - eg ferrous and non-ferrous
  • storing waste securely and protecting it from potential contaminants
  • ensuring that the waste stays clean

Nitrate Vulnerable Zones

If your land falls within an NVZ, there may be additional rules that affect your waste and recycling. For more information, see the guide on NVZ.

Sewage sludge

Sewage sludge is a controlled waste. It can be used as a fertiliser and soil conditioner, but may also be harmful to the environment if incorrectly applied. See the guide on sewage sludge, slurry and silage.

Environmental permits and registering waste exemptions

You may need an environmental permit for your waste activities. Although some activities do not need a permit, they still need to be registered with the Environment Agency. These are called exempt waste activities. If you have a registered exemption, you must comply with specific conditions for that exemption.

Examples of activities that require an exemption include composting plant waste, using wood chips for animal bedding and burning plant matter in the open.

Changes to environmental permitting and exemptions

New Environmental Permitting Regulations came into force across England and Wales on 6 April 2010 replaced the list of exempt activities with a new one, which groups exemptions into the following categories:

  • ‘U’ - use of waste - eg spreading compost on land to improve soil
  • ‘T’ - treatment of waste - eg shredding vegetation and branches to make a mulch
  • ‘D’ - disposal of waste - eg burning vegetation and branches on a bonfire
  • ‘S’ - storage of waste - eg storing it according to prescribed requirements until it is required for spreading

Some of the new exemptions are almost identical to exemptions in the old system and others are similar but have different limits and conditions. You are advised to check, as there are some completely new exemptions. Once your exemptions have been registered, you must renew them every three years. You can download a leaflet on how changes to the waste exemption system affect farmers from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 133K).

If you have an exemption that you registered before 6 April 2010, you will need to re-register it under the new system within a transition period extending until 1 October 2013, although the actual date is determined by the type of waste operation. Throughout this period, you will still need to comply with the limits and conditions of the exemptions you hold under the old system.

Find out how the new regulations affect farmers on the Environment Agency website.

Find out what standard permits are available on the Environment Agency website.

If you think you may need a standard permit for an existing activity, you can download a basic introduction to permitting from the Environment Agency website (PDF, 203K).

Re-registering your exemptions

It is free to register exemptions and you can re-register under the new system at any time.

Register your waste exemptions on the Environment Agency website or by calling the Environment Agency Agricultural Waste Helpline on: 0845 603 3113.

There are different dates by which you must have re-registered or applied for a standard rules permit under the new system.

Further information on minimising waste and composting

Further information on minimising waste and composting is available in other guides on this website, and from the following organisations.

The Carbon Trust is an organisation set up by the UK government to help businesses improve their energy efficiency and reduce their carbon emissions. They can help you to identify where you can save energy and money in your agricultural business. Find sector-specific information on the Carbon Trust website or call the Carbon Trust Advice Line on: 0800 085 2005.

Envirowise offers free, independent support to your business on waste management and reduction, and can help you to become more resource efficient and save money.

They can also advise on ways to increase your profits, minimise waste and reduce your farm’s overall environmental impact. For more information, call the Envirowise Advice Line on: 0800 585 794.

The Environment Agency is responsible for issuing environmental licences and exemptions. They can also offer advice on environmental legislation and compliance.

You can call the Environment Agency Helpline on 03708 506 506.

One of the major roles of Defra is to help the farming industry operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies that provide around £3 billion to UK agriculture. They also oversee a number of agencies that work with arable farmers, imports and exports of crops and implement pest and disease controls.

You can call the Defra Helpline on: 08459 33 55 77. You can also contact Defra regarding energy efficiency activities by email at energy.efficiency@defra.gsi.gov.uk.

The Rural Payments Agency (RPA) is responsible for licences and schemes for growers as well as for running the Single Payment Scheme (SPS). For more information about SPS and how it can help your farming business, you can call the RPA Helpline on: 0845 603 7777. You can also read the guide on the SPS.

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about Cross Compliance. For further information, call the Cross Compliance Helpline on: 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, find information on Cross Compliance requirements on the Cross Compliance website.

WRAP (Waste & Resources Action Programme) works in the UK to help businesses and individuals reap the benefits of reducing waste, developing sustainable products, and using resources in an efficient way. WRAP is a not-for-profit company backed by government funding from England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. You can find composting and digestate product news and support on the WRAP website.

The National Farmers Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities. You can read about the work of the NFU on the NFU website.

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities over a number of farming, land use, food standards and environmental regulations. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources.
