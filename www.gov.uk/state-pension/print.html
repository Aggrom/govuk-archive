Benefits & credits: The basic State Pension

Notes

Part 1: Overview

State Pension is a regular payment from the government. You may get it when you reach your State Pension age.

The State Pension is made up of 2 parts:

  • basic State Pension
  • Additional State Pension

You can claim up to £107.45 a week for basic State Pension. The amount you get depends on the number of years you’ve been paying National Insurance contributions.

The amount of additional State Pension you can get isn’t fixed - it depends on your circumstances.

Part 2: What you'll get

Get a detailed State Pension statement

If you’re approaching State Pension age, a State Pension statement can give you detailed information about what you’d get based on your National Insurance contributions so far.

Basic State Pension

You can get the maximum amount of basic State Pension if you have paid enough National Insurance contributions (NICs). If you don’t have enough contributions, you may get less.

If you’re married or in a civil partnership, your spouse or civil partner’s NICs may also make a difference.

Circumstances Maximum weekly payment (2012 to 2013)
Single person £107.45
Person in a marriage or civil partnership using their own NICs £107.45
Person using their husband, wife or civil partner’s NICs £64.40

Get an estimate for basic State Pension

You can use the State Pension Profiler to see if you’re eligible and estimate how much basic State Pension you may get.

Additional State Pension

The amount of additional State Pension you can get depends on your NICs and whether or not you choose to opt out - there isn’t a flat rate.

State Pension increases

The basic State Pension currently goes up each year by whichever is the highest:

  • the average percentage growth in wages (in Great Britain)
  • average price increases (measured by the Consumer Price Index or CPI)
  • 2.5%

The additional State Pension goes up each year in line with the CPI.

How it’s paid

State Pension is paid directly into an account of your choice.

Part 3: Eligibility

You must have reached your State Pension age before you can claim your State Pension.

Basic State Pension

The amount you get is based on ‘qualifying years’ - the number of years you’ve been paying National Insurance (NI) contributions.

You can use the State Pension Profiler to see how many qualifying years you have for basic State Pension.

The table shows how many years you need, depending on your birthdate and whether you’re a man or woman.

Date of birth Number of years
Men born before 6 April 1945 44
Men born on or after 6 April 1945 30
Women born before 6 April 1950 39
Women born on or after 6 April 1950 30

If you don’t have enough qualifying years

You may still get between £26.86 and £107.45 (2012 to 2013).

If you reached State Pension age before 6 April 2010

If you have fewer than 25% of the qualifying years, you can’t get any basic State Pension using your NI contributions record.

You may be able to boost your State Pension.

State Pension through marriage or civil partnership

You may be able to get some basic State Pension through the NI record of your:

  • spouse or civil partner
  • late spouse or civil partner

Additional State Pension

The rules about eligibility are different for additional State Pension.

Part 4: How to claim

You should receive a claim form 4 months before you reach State Pension age.

You can use this form to claim basic and additional State Pension.

If you haven’t heard from The Pension Service when you’re 3 months from your State Pension age, phone the State Pension claim line.

State Pension claim line
Telephone: 0800 731 7898
Textphone: 0800 731 7339
Monday to Friday 8am to 8pm (except public holidays)

You can also download the State Pension claim form, print it out, then complete and send it to your local pension centre. This may take longer than using the phone.

If you’re living abroad

Find out how to claim if you’re abroad.

What you need when you make a claim

You’ll need to give the following:

  • your National Insurance (NI) number
  • your current address and postcode, plus your last 2 addresses
  • your tax reference number - you can find this on your P45, P60 or any letter from HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC)
  • details for your spouse or civil partner (including their NI number and the date of your marriage or civil partnership)
  • relevant dates if your spouse or civil partner has died, or if you’re divorced or your civil partnership has been dissolved
  • details of any social security benefits that you, your spouse or civil partner are getting or waiting to hear about
  • the address of any employer you’ve worked for in the last 2 years, and dates of employment
  • dates and social security numbers if you’ve ever lived or worked outside the UK
  • the number for the account you want your State Pension paid into

All the information you give will be treated confidentially.

Put off getting your State Pension

You may be able to get more money if you choose to put off your pension payments until later - this is called deferring your State Pension.

Part 5: Further information

What to do if your circumstances change

You should tell The Pension Service if you:

  • move home
  • go outside the UK to live or for a long visit
  • go into a care home
  • change bank accounts

You should also report a change in your marital status, or if your spouse or civil partner dies.

Going into hospital

Your State Pension isn’t affected by a stay in hospital, but if you claim Pension Credit, it might go down if you’re in hospital for more than 4 weeks.

Inheriting State Pension

You may be able to use your late spouse or civil partner’s National Insurance (NI) contributions to top up your basic State Pension. You can’t do this if you:

  • already have enough NI contributions for a full basic State Pension
  • remarry or form a new civil partnership before you reach State Pension age

Your late spouse or civil partner died after deferring their State Pension

Your State Pension payments may be increased, unless you remarry or form a new civil partnership before you reach State Pension age.

You’re single and you die before you claim your State Pension

If you’re not married or in a civil partnership when you die, your estate may claim up to 3 months of your State Pension, as long as you:

  • had reached State Pension age
  • hadn’t yet claimed your State Pension

Nominate someone to collect your pension

You can nominate someone to collect your State Pension if you’re unable to.

Kindertransport refugees

If you came to the UK as part of the Kindertransport, you may be eligible for more German state pension because of changes to UK law in 2008.
