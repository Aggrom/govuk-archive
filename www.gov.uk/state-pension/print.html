Benefits & credits: The basic State Pension

Notes

Part 1: Overview

The basic State Pension is a weekly or monthly payment from the government that you can get when you reach State Pension age (usually between 65 and 68, depending on when you were born).

The most you can currently get is £107.45 per week. The basic State Pension increases every year by at least 2.5%.

Part 2: What you'll get

You get up to £107.45 per week.

If you’re a married or in a civil partnership and not working or claiming certain benefits you can get up to £64.40 per week.

Use the State Pension calculator to get an estimate.

You might also qualify for the Additional State Pension or, if you’re on a low income, Pension Credit.

How it’s paid

The basic State Pension is paid weekly or monthly into an account of your choice.

There are different rules if you live abroad.

You’re close to State Pension age

You can get a State Pension forecast that tells you exactly how much you’ll get.

Part 3: Eligibility

You get the basic State Pension once you reached State Pension age.

Your basic State Pension depends on the number of years you’ve paid National Insurance or got National Insurance credits while claiming certain benefits.

To qualify for a basic State Pension at least one of the following must apply to you:

  • you were working and paying National Insurance with your tax
  • you were getting certain benefits, eg for unemployment, sickness, disability
  • you were a parent or carer and claiming certain benefits
  • you have a spouse or partner whose National Insurance contributions cover you
  • you were paying voluntary National Insurance contributions

To get the full basic State Pension you need 30 years worth of contributions. These are your ‘qualifying years’. If you have fewer than 30 years your State Pension will be less than £107.45 per week.

Use the State Pension calculator to find out how many qualifying years you have and what your State Pension would be now.

You’re working

When you’re working you pay National Insurance with your tax and get qualifying years if:

  • you’re employed and earning over £5,564
  • you’re self-employed and earning over £5,595

You’re not working

In some circumstances when you’re not working the government will give you ‘National Insurance credits’ that count as qualifying years towards your basic State Pension.

Since April 2010 - you get credits if any of the following apply:

  • you care for a child under 12
  • you care for someone sick or disabled
  • you are a registered foster carer
  • you get Carer’s Allowance

Before April 2010 - you got credits if any of the following applied:

  • you claimed Child Benefit for a child under 16
  • you cared for someone sick or disabled
  • you worked as a registered carer
  • you got Carer’s Allowance

Married or in a civil partnership

If you’re not eligible for a State Pension or not getting the full amount, you might be able to qualify or ‘top up’ through your spouse’s or partner’s National Insurance contributions if:

  • you were born after 6 April 1950
  • you have both reached State Pension age
  • your spouse or partner qualifies for some basic State Pension

You do this through claiming your State Pension.

You don’t qualify for a full State Pension

If you’re not covered by any of these groups but want a State Pension you can pay voluntary National Insurance contributions. You can also do this to top up if you don’t have 30 qualifying years and want the full amount.

Men born before 1945 and women born before 1950

You need more qualifying years to get a full State Pension and a certain minimum number of years to get any State Pension at all.

Who Number of years needed for a full State Pension Number of years needed for any State Pension
Men born before 6 April 1945 44 11
Women born before 6 April 1950 39 9.75

Part 4: How to claim

You won’t get your State Pension automatically, you have to claim it. You should get a claim form in the post 4 months before you reach State Pension age.

If you don’t, phone the claim line.

State Pension claim line
Telephone: 0800 731 7898
Textphone: 0800 731 7339
Monday to Friday 8am to 8pm (except public holidays)

You can also claim your State Pension online.

You can also download the claim form and send it to your local pension centre.

You want to keep working

If you want to claim it later you defer your State Pension and get a slightly higher amount.

Part 5: Further information

Your circumstances change

You must tell the Pension Service if you:

  • move home
  • stay in hospital
  • move abroad or go abroad for a longer time
  • go into a care home

You’ll still get State Pension when you go into hospital or a care home.

What happens to your State Pension when you die

Your spouse or civil partner can use your qualifying years to top up their State Pension if they don’t get the full amount. However, to do this they have to be over State Pension age and can’t have remarried or formed a new civil partnership.

Your spouse or civil partner can’t inherit payments from your State Pension.

If you’re single and die after you’ve reached State Pension age, your estate can claim up to 3 months of your basic State Pension.
