Poultry farms: general regulations

How to register and comply with legislation for keeping broiler chickens, laying hens and other poultry species

Introduction

Maintaining high standards of animal health and welfare on the farm is essential for establishing consumer confidence, and helping to limit the risk of passing disease to both humans and animals.

If you farm or intend to farm poultry, there are rules and regulations that you must be aware of. You must follow specific legislation in order to maintain quality and to ensure that animal and consumer health is protected.

This guide gives you information on when you must have an environmental permit to operate flocks, why you must register poultry and how to do so, the rules and regulations that you must comply with as a poultry farmer and regulations for marketing eggs.

Poultry registration

The Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra), the Scottish Executive and the Welsh Government - backed by the poultry industry - set up the Great Britain (GB) Poultry Register to gather information about species of birds held on premises in Great Britain.

To help national veterinary surveillance, the following species of poultry must be registered on the GB Poultry Register:

  • chickens - including bantams
  • turkeys
  • ducks
  • geese
  • partridges
  • quail
  • pheasants
  • pigeons - reared for meat
  • guinea fowl
  • ostriches
  • emus, rheas and cassowaries (usually kept in zoos)

Who has to register?

You must register if you own - or are responsible for - a poultry premises with 50 or more birds. This applies even if your premises are only stocked for part of the year. Premises with fewer than 50 birds do not need to register, but Defra encourages keepers to do so voluntarily.

You must register if you have 50 or more birds in total. They do not have to be all of the same species.

To keep the register up to date, you must notify Defra of any significant changes to information you have already supplied. You should do this within one month of the changes happening. You should call the GB Poultry Register Helpline on Telephone: 0800 634 1112.

What are the benefits?

If Defra knows how many birds you have and where you keep them, they will be able to contact you easily and help manage any potential disease outbreak by targeting resources where they are needed most.

What information do you have to provide?

You will be asked for:

  • your name
  • your premises address
  • your County Parish Holding number, if you have one - this is for administration purposes to make it easier to trace livestock owners in event of disease outbreak
  • the number of poultry usually on the premises - this is the number present when the premises are stocked
  • the type of poultry housing
  • why you are rearing poultry

You will also be asked risk-assessment questions, eg whether the poultry have access to the open air and whether there are any bodies of water close by that may attract wild birds.

What is the Alerts System?

The GB Poultry Register allows government and its agencies to contact you about important developments regarding disease by email, post and text. If you change your contact details, you should call the GB Poultry Register Helpline on Telephone: 0800 634 1112.

You can also register for disease alerts - Animal Health and Veterinary Laboratories Agency (AHVLA) channels providing news of current disease situations on the Defra website.

If you operate large flocks, you may separately have to apply to the Environment Agency for an environmental permit.

How to register your birds on the Great Britain Poultry Register

You can register your birds on the Great Britain (GB) Poultry Register by telephone, post or online.

By telephone

You can register your birds by telephone by calling the GB Poultry Register Helpline on Telephone: 0800 634 1112 and requesting a registration form - also available in Welsh. You must then send the completed form - in the pre-paid envelope provided - to:

Poultry Register
PO Box 1109
Warrington
WA55 1EH

You can also call the Defra Helpline on Telephone: 08459 33 55 77, where an advisor will fill in the form for you over the phone. You will then be sent the completed form to check, with a confirmation letter.

By post

You can download a copy of the GB poultry registration form from the Defra website (PDF, 92KB). You should then send your completed form to:

Poultry Register
PO Box 1109
Warrington
WA55 1EH

A copy of the completed registration form and a confirmation letter will be sent for you to check.

Online

You can register for the GB Poultry Register on the GB Poultry Register website.

Environmental protection and pollution control

Poultry farms affect the environment through the release of pollutants, such as:

  • ammonia
  • nutrients from manure, litter and slurry
  • effluent discharges
  • dust
  • odour
  • noise

The Environment Agency regulates intensive poultry farms and poultry production facilities to monitor and control the above pollutants, including the proper removal, disposal and/or treatment of waste matter.

Environmental permits - what you must do

You must apply to the Environment Agency for a bespoke Environmental Permit to operate if your livestock capacity exceeds 40,000 poultry. This includes:

  • chickens
  • layers
  • pullet
  • turkeys
  • ducks
  • guinea fowl

If your farm currently operates below the permit threshold but you have expansion plans that will take you above it, you must apply for one. You can apply online for an Environmental Permit. Find environmental permit application forms on the Environment Agency website.

If you already have an environmental permit and want to make changes to your farm, you may need to apply for a permit variation.

Use of on-farm mixing animal feed and the safety of consumers

The authorisation, manufacture, distribution and use of ingredients, feedingstuffs and additives are strictly controlled throughout the European Union. Periodically, amending regulations update changes in undesirable substances and/or permitted residue levels in light of new scientific research.

You can find overviews of the regulations, guidance and the latest animal feed news on the Food Standards Agency website.

Medicated feedingstuffs (MFS)

Antibiotics, coccidiostats, growth promoters and other medicinal substances are all zootechnical products. Their manufacture, marketing, labelling and use - in the form of additives, premixes and when incorporated into feedingstuffs - are closely controlled by the Veterinary Medicines Directorate (VMD). As a poultry farmer, you must also follow a range of requirements, including:

  • having a medication feedingstuffs prescription for in-feed medication
  • initiating medication only with formal veterinary approval, or under verbal direction from a veterinarian under whose care the poultry are placed
  • carefully monitoring that the correct dosage is consumed
  • keeping accurate records relating to the birds, the time and manner of treatment

You can access current legislation and guidance documents on the VMD website.

Veterinary medicinal products

If you store or supply veterinary medicinal products (VMPs) on - or from - your premises for poultry or other livestock, you are required to be listed on a register maintained by the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons on behalf of the VMD.

The VMD has the right to enter and inspect any premises where VMPs are held. For more information, you can read guidance on premises licensing and inspections on the British Small Animal Veterinary Association website.

Imported and processed meat and animal products

The VMD’s non-statutory programme supplements the National Surveillance Scheme (NSS), and carries out residue analysis on imported and processed foods to ensure that authorised medicines are not present in foodstuffs above the permitted level - the Maximum Residue Limit (MRL) - and that banned or unauthorised substances are not being used. It also gives information on foods that do not fall within the NSS. Samples are taken from popular meat and animal products collected from shops and at border inspection posts for official analysis.

All medicines come with detailed manufacturers’ instructions on how to use them. Young birds are more at risk from some diseases, so medicines may only need to be given at the start of a bird’s life. You must keep records of all medicine given to your birds. These should show the date and details of any treatment, and any required withdrawal period before the birds may be slaughtered or their products - such as eggs - are permitted to enter the food chain.

All birds intended for human consumption are examined by slaughterhouse inspectors for disease or injury. Random samples of poultry meat - labelled with the farm of origin - are taken for residue testing to check that it is within permitted MRLs.

Withdrawal periods for poultry medicines

The use of animal medicines on farm animals is controlled by European law. You must follow the rules on the withdrawal period. This is the time between giving the animal its last dose of medicine and the level of residues in the tissues or products falling below the MRL. Until the withdrawal period has run out, the animal or its products must not be used for human consumption.

The withdrawal period is included in the medicine’s data sheet and the instructions for use - which should be part of the product packaging. You are required by law to record all uses of animal medicine.

If a Veterinary Medicinal Product (VMP) is to be included - in accordance with the Marketing Authorisations (MA) - the minimum withdrawal period shown on the Medicated Feedingstuffs Prescription must be the same as on the product label. A vet giving a VMP to a food-producing animal must give an appropriate withdrawal period.

Withdrawal periods stop medicines getting into the food chain and ensure that MRLs are not exceeded. Safety is essential when both MRLs and withdrawal periods are set up. Even residues above the MRL present little risk to the consumer because of the large safety margins used in setting the MRLs.

The statutory withdrawal period for VMPs must not be less than:

  • seven days for eggs
  • seven days for milk
  • 28 days for meat from poultry and mammals - including fat and offal
  • 500 degree days for fish meat

The Secretary of State can specify - in writing - a different withdrawal period for a particular VMP.

Where more than one VMP is prescribed - and all are used under the terms of the MA - the longest withdrawal period will apply. Where one or more are used outside the terms of the MA, the statutory withdrawal periods will apply.

Marketing rules and regulations for eggs and poultry

The Poultrymeat Marketing Standards regulate standards throughout the EU for the marketing of poultrymeat intended for human consumption.

They control the classification of poultrymeat by:

  • quality
  • weight
  • packaging and labelling
  • water content
  • storage

They also specify the standards that must be met before claims about farming methods can be made. The regulations protect the consumer by setting the same high standards and informative labelling throughout the EU. They also protect the producer against unfair competition.

The Poultrymeat Marketing Standards apply to fresh, chilled, frozen or quick frozen whole carcasses, parts and offal of:

  • domestic fowl - gallus domesticus, eg meat chickens and laying hens
  • ducks
  • geese
  • turkeys
  • guinea fowl

The legislation applies to these species only.

The regulations do not apply to:

  • prepared or preserved poultry - cooked, processed, treated, or value-added products or recipe dishes
  • poultrymeat intended for export from the European Community (EC)
  • sales from farms with an annual production of under 10,000 birds - providing you supply fresh poultrymeat from holdings in the same - or neighbouring - locality
  • New York Dressed poultry - ie delayed evisceration poultry, such as ‘traditional farm fresh turkeys’

Eggs and Chicks (England) Regulations 2009

The Eggs and Chicks Regulations implement directly applicable European Community marketing standards relating to:

  • eggs for hatching and farmyard poultry chicks
  • eggs in shell for human consumption
  • Salmonella controls under the related National Control Programme (NCP)

These regulations cover most aspects of egg production, marking, transport, grading, packing and their onward marketing, including eggs intended for human consumption as Class A eggs.

The Regulations include requirements for the marketing of eggs for human consumption. It is an offence to market eggs as Class A if they are produced by laying flocks infected with Salmonella or which are of unknown health status (due to failure to test for Salmonella). Such eggs must be marketed as Class B and treated to guarantee that all Salmonella serotypes of significance to public health have been eliminated (ie heat treated or pasteurised).

Find information on the national control programmes for the reduction of Salmonella on the Defra website.

The Hatching Eggs and Chicks Regulations cover:

  • domestic fowl
  • turkeys
  • ducks
  • geese
  • guinea fowl

The Egg (Marketing Standards) Regulations do not apply to eggs:

  • from poultry other than laying hens
  • sold directly to the consumer for their own use
  • sold by the producer on their own farm
  • sold by the producer in a local public market - with the exception of farm gate sales, at local public or auction markets, or by door-to-door selling

All Class A eggs sold at retail - and ungraded eggs sold at local public markets within the EU - must be marked with a code.

This must identify the:

  • establishment where they were produced
  • country of origin
  • method of production - eg organic, free-range, barn or cage

It is also compulsory to stamp and label all third-country eggs sold at retail. Download Defra’s information on egg markings from the Agricultural Document Library (ADLib) website (PDF, 1.37MB).

Financial support for poultry farmers

Direct financial support for the poultry industry is limited. However, there are some organisations that provide help and support to poultry farmers.

Poultry and international trade incentives

If you engage in the import or export of poultry or poultrymeat products, the Common Agricultural Policy ensures that EC producers receive fair prices for these and other goods. This could mean that import licences might be available under various product-based preferential import quota schemes. Similarly, some products may attract an export refund. You can find out about import quotas and export refund schemes for poultry on the Rural Payments Agency website.

The Rural Development Programme England (RDPE) is jointly funded by the EU, through the European Agricultural Fund for Rural Development, and the government. You can find details of the full range of RDPE schemes on the Defra website.

There may be opportunities to obtain grants under the agri-environment schemes. These schemes can provide grants towards making environmental improvements to farm holdings. For more details, you can contact the Defra Helpline on Telephone: 08459 33 55 77.

The Home Grown Cereals Authority operates an Enterprise Awards scheme for users of cereals. This can include cereals used in feed rations or cereal ingredients in value-added production.

Organic poultry farmers may benefit from Organic Entry Level Stewardship (OELS). This voluntary, non-competitive scheme allows you to choose from a wide range of options to cover all farming types. See the guide on OELS.

The land to be entered into the scheme must be farmed organically and registered with an approved Organic Inspection Body before an application to OELS is made. Farmers with a mix of organic and conventional land can apply for OELS on their OELS-eligible land and Entry Level Stewardship (ELS) on the remainder - at the applicable ELS payment rates - as part of one, whole farm, OELS agreement. For more information, see the guide on Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

You can also find information on the different types of Environmental Stewardship and OELS on the Natural England website.
