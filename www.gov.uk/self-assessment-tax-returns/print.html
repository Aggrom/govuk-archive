Self Assessment tax returns , a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If you’re registered for Self Assessment, you’ll usually get a letter in April from HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) telling you to send a tax return. You should send your tax return, even if you don’t have any tax to pay.

How to send a tax return

You can send your tax return online or using paper forms but you must be registered for Self Assessment.

There are deadlines for sending your return and penalties if you miss them.

Part 2: Who must send a tax return

You can send your tax return online or using paper forms but you must be registered for Self Assessment.

Examples of who must send a tax return

You must send a tax return if you’re:

  • a sole trader
  • self-employed
  • a partner in a business partnership
  • a company director
  • the director of a charity who gets paid or receives ‘benefits’ - eg travel expenses or a company car

You must also send a tax return if any of your income:

  • from savings and investments is £10,000 or more
  • from untaxed savings or investments is £2,500 or more
  • from property is more than £10,000 before allowable expenses or £2,500 after allowable expenses
  • from selling assets like shares or property and you need to pay Capital Gains Tax on the profit
  • is from overseas and UK tax must be paid on it
  • income is more than £100,000 a year
  • is taxable but HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) can’t collect the tax through your tax code - eg you pay a higher rate of Income Tax and have savings or investments

Other reasons

You may have to send a tax return if you’re:

  • finalising the affairs of someone who’s died
  • a trustee of a trust or a registered pension scheme
  • a religious minister of any kind
  • paying tax through PAYE but want to pay tax you owe without changing your tax code
  • employed and want to claim expenses or professional subscriptions of more than £2,500
  • claiming Enterprise Investment Scheme or Venture Capital Trust relief
  • a name or member of Lloyd’s of London insurance market
  • over 65 and your income is over the limit for an age-related allowance
  • you’ve lived or worked abroad or you aren’t domiciled in the UK

Check with HMRC if you don’t think you have to send a tax return.

Part 3: Deadlines

Filing your tax return

The 2011 to 2012 tax year ended 5 April 2012. The main deadlines for are:

  • paper tax returns - midnight 31 October 2012
  • online tax returns - midnight 31 January 2013
  • final payment of any tax due - midnight 31 January 2013

If you’ve missed the paper deadline, send your return online instead to avoid a penalty.

Exceptions

The deadline for sending the tax return can be different.

Example
Deadline: 30 December - if you send your return online, owe less than £3,000, and want HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) to try and collect the tax through your tax code.

Example
Deadline: 31 January - if you’re a trustee of a registered pension scheme or a non-resident company (you must send a paper return because you can’t do it online).

Example
HMRC writes telling you a different deadline.

There are additional payment deadlines if you make ‘payments on account’.

You can pay HM Revenue & Customs in different ways, including direct debit and internet banking.

Part 4: Penalties

Penalties - late tax return

You should send your tax return, even if you don’t have any tax to pay or you’ve already paid it.

Delay Penalty
1 day late £100
3 months The penalty above plus £10 for each day it’s late (up to £900)
6 months All the penalties above plus £300 or 5% of the tax due (whichever is higher)
12 months All the penalties above plus £300 or 5% of the tax due (whichever is higher) - in serious cases you may have to pay 100% of the tax due

You can pay HM Revenue & Customs in different ways, including direct debit and internet banking.

Part 5: Corrections

You can correct your tax return online or send HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) the corrected pages of your paper tax return (mark them ‘amendment’).

The deadline for this is usually 12 months after the tax return deadline (eg 31 January).

If you’ve missed this deadline, write to HMRC and:

  • confirm the tax year you’re correcting
  • explain why you’ve paid too much tax
  • include proof you’ve paid the tax in that year
  • confirm how you want the refund

The deadline for writing to HMRC is 4 years after the end of the tax year you’re correcting.

Refunds

HMRC will let you know how much your refund is (including interest). Refunds can be:

  • paid electronically into your bank account
  • paid by cheque
  • deducted from your next Self Assessment tax bill - if it’s less than £10 or a tax bill is due shortly

If you owe more tax

HMRC will let you know how much tax you owe (including interest) and when to pay them.

Where to send your corrections

Check your Self Assessment paperwork for the address to send your corrections to. If you can’t find the address, use the details below.
