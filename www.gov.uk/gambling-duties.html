Gambling duties

Duties you may need to register for and pay if you manage any gambling activities, and when you need to have an additional licence.

Introduction

There are seven different duties payable on gambling activities in the UK. These are:

  • Bingo Duty
  • Gaming Duty
  • Remote Gaming Duty
  • General Betting Duty
  • Pool Betting Duty
  • Lottery Duty
  • Amusement Machine Licence Duty

This guide explains when you have to register for and pay these duties, and where you can find further information. It is important to remember that even if you register with HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) and pay any of these duties, you may still need to hold a licence or permit from another authority under gambling legislation.

This guide also explains how you may be able to run a lottery, bingo or other gambling activity that’s exempt from the need to register for and pay duty.

Bingo Duty

Bingo Duty is charged on the profits you make by organising and promoting bingo games.

The duty is charged at 20 per cent of profits. Profits are defined as all of your ‘bingo receipts’, less ‘bingo winnings’ within an accounting period.

Bingo receipts include all payments you receive as a bingo promoter that entitles a player to play bingo, including payments you receive for the cost of cards and participation fees. Bingo receipts don’t include:

  • payments for admission into the club
  • bingo club membership
  • incidental benefits, such as catering

Bingo winnings include the total value of the prizes. If you pay cash prizes, you use the actual total paid out as your bingo winnings. If you provide non-cash prizes, this is the actual cost to you of the prize.

If you use vouchers to pay out as prizes, there are special rules covering how you calculate the total value. Read about accounting for vouchers as bingo prizes in Notice 457 on the HMRC website.

Exemptions from Bingo Duty

All bingo played in the UK is liable to Bingo Duty, except:

  • bingo played in a private home or on a domestic occasion
  • bingo on a machine that is covered by Amusement Machine Licence Duty
  • non-profit making bingo, where you don’t charge a player a fee to participate in a game of bingo - either directly or through a levy on stakes or winnings
  • bingo played at certain amusement arcades and travelling fairs, if the stakes and prizes are below specific limits

‘Small-scale’ bingo is also exempt from Bingo Duty if it’s promoted by a club, organisation or other association of people that doesn’t have a bingo operating licence, provided that either:

  • only members of a club or organisation or their guests can play
  • winnings or stakes do not exceed £500 on any one day, or £7,500 within any period of four or five weeks, ending on the last Sunday of each calendar month

Find information on Bingo Duty exemptions in Notice 457 on the HMRC website.

How to register for Bingo Duty

If you decide to promote bingo that is liable to Bingo Duty, you must register with HMRC at least 14 days before you start your games.

If you operate small-scale bingo but exceed the limits for exemption, then you must tell HMRC within five days of the date you exceeded the limit.

In both cases, you will need to register with HMRC for Bingo Duty by completing form EX 250.

Find form EX 250 - application for registration as a bingo promoter on the HMRC website.

Read guidance about registering for Bingo Duty in Notice 457 on the HMRC website.

Bingo Duty returns and payments

If you are registered for Bingo Duty, you will be sent a Bingo Duty return form BD 510, which you must complete and return - this is usually sent monthly. The completed form BD 510 must be received by HMRC on the due date shown on the form. This is calculated as the 15th day following the end of the accounting period that the return covers - or the previous working day if that day is a weekend or public holiday.

Read guidance on completing your Bingo Duty return on the HMRC website.

Any Bingo Duty payments you have to make must be received by HMRC on the same date as your Bingo Duty return.

How to pay Bingo Duty

Gaming Duty

If your business runs premises where any form of gaming takes place in the UK - eg a casino - you will need to pay Gaming Duty.

Gaming Duty is paid on the money gambled, minus any winnings paid. The rates of duty are based on a sliding scale from 15 per cent to 50 per cent, based on four different bands.

The duty applies to:

  • games of chance, where players play against the bank - known as casino games
  • games of equal chance, where players play against each other

Exemptions from Gaming Duty

The following are exempt from Gaming Duty:

  • bingo games that are covered by Bingo Duty
  • lotteries that are covered by Lottery Duty
  • machines classed as amusement machines and covered by Amusement Machine Licence Duty
  • private gaming
  • ‘small-scale’ gaming - for example, at members’ clubs and pubs
  • non-commercial gaming - where the proceeds are not used for private gain, eg for a charity

Who should register for Gaming Duty

You must register with HMRC for Gaming Duty if either:

  • you hold a casino premises licence under the Gambling Act 2005 and gaming that is liable to Gaming Duty takes place when the licence is in force
  • you provide the premises for, or are involved in the management or organisation of, unlicensed gaming which is liable for Gaming Duty

How to register for Gaming Duty

To register, you need to complete both form GD 56 - Application for Registration for Gaming Duty and form GD 58 - Notification of Premises and return them to the National Registration Unit no later than 14 days before you start to provide gaming.

Find form GD 56 - Application for Registration for Gaming Duty on the HMRC website.

Find form GD 58 - Notification of Premises on the HMRC website.

Read about who needs to register and how to register for Gaming Duty in Notice 453 on the HMRC website.

Gaming Duty returns and payment

HMRC will send you forms GD 94 and GD 95 in each six-month accounting period, usually beginning 1 April and 1 October each year. If you do not receive the relevant return form or lose it you can ask HMRC for a replacement. Alternatively you can download forms GD 94 and GD 95 from the HMRC website.

After the first three months of the period, you must complete form GD 94 and send it back with a payment on account to cover the Gaming Duty likely to be due for the first three months. This must be received by HMRC by the due date shown on the form, or the previous working day if that day is a public holiday or weekend.

At the end of the period, you must calculate the Gaming Duty due for the whole period using form GD 95, deduct any payment made on account and send the form and balance to HMRC within one month of the end of the period.

Find specimen form GD 94 for paying Gaming Duty on account on the HMRC website.

Find specimen form GD 95 - Gaming Duty Return on the HMRC website.

Find out about making returns and calculating payments for Gaming Duty in Notice 453 on the HMRC website.

How to pay Gaming Duty

Remote Gaming Duty

If you provide facilities for people to play games of chance for prizes through any kind of remote communication - such as the telephone, internet or interactive TV - you will have to register for, manage and pay Remote Gaming Duty.

Remote Gaming Duty is charged at 15 per cent of profits.

Registration for Remote Gaming Duty

You will need to register with HMRC for Remote Gaming Duty if all of the following apply:

  • you hold a UK remote gaming operating licence
  • you provide facilities for remote gaming
  • at least one piece of your equipment is used in the UK
  • the gaming is not already covered by, or specifically exempt from, another UK gambling duty

To register for Remote Gaming Duty, you will need to complete form GD 120 and return it to HMRC. You must return this form no later than 14 days before you start to provide remote gaming.

Find form GD 120 - Application for Registration for Remote Gaming Duty on the HMRC website.

Read about registration requirements for Remote Gaming Duty in Notice 455 on the HMRC website.

Returns and payments for Remote Gaming Duty

HMRC will send you form GD 121 for each three-month accounting period - usually starting 1 January and every three months afterwards. The completed return must be received by HMRC, along with payment of any Remote Gaming Duty due, no later than the 15th day after the end of the accounting period - or the previous working day if that day is a weekend or public holiday.

If you haven’t carried out any activities related to Remote Gaming Duty in a return period, you must still complete a NIL return.

You can download a Remote Gaming Duty return form GD 121 from the HMRC website. However, it’s best to use the original form that HMRC sends you and not the specimen form.

Download specimen form GD 121 - Remote Gaming Duty Return from the HMRC website (PDF, 44KB).

How to pay Remote Gaming Duty

Record keeping for Remote Gaming Duty

You must keep records to show that you are accounting for and paying any Remote Gaming Duty that you are liable for. There are no strict rules covering the format of your records, but you must allow HMRC to review them upon request.

Find guidance on record keeping and Remote Gaming Duty in Notice 455 on the HMRC website.

General Betting Duty

General Betting Duty is charged on the gross profits you make through bookmaking. It applies to all bets made with any of the following:

  • a UK-based bookmaker
  • the totalisator operator in the UK
  • a bet broker
  • a betting exchange
  • a pools promoter on horse or dog racing

The General Betting Duty rates are:

  • 15 per cent for fixed odds and totalisator bets
  • 3 per cent for financial spread bets
  • 10 per cent for all other spread bets

Exemptions from General Betting Duty

General Betting Duty is not due on on-course bets taken, provided that either of the following apply:

  • the person making the bets and the bookmaker or totalisator operator accepting the bets are both present at a meeting
  • the bets are taken from a bookmaker who is not at the meeting, but who makes hedged bets with a bookmaker who is present at the meeting

If you use a Fixed Odds Betting Terminal, you will be liable to pay Amusement Machine Licence Duty rather than General Betting Duty.

Amusement Machine Licence Duty

Registering for General Betting Duty

You need to notify HMRC if you want to start a business as a bookmaker or bet-broker, even if you only take on-course bets that fulfil all the conditions for being exempt from General Betting Duty. To notify HMRC, you should fill in form BD 75 to carry on a general betting business and return it to the address on the form to arrive at least one week before you start your business.

Find form BD 75 - General Betting Duty on the HMRC website.

Find out about notifying for General Betting Duty in Notice 451 on the HMRC website.

Returns and payments

HMRC will send you a return form for each accounting period, usually for every month. The return form you receive will depend on which type of betting business you run. You will be sent:

  • form BD 211 if you receive off-course bets as a bookmaker
  • form BD 211A if you receive spread bets
  • form BD 211B if you are a betting exchange

The completed return must be received by HMRC, along with payment of any General Betting Duty due, no later than the 15th day after the end of the accounting period, or the previous working day before that if it’s a weekend or public holiday.

How to pay General Betting Duty

Record keeping for General Betting Duty

You must keep detailed records to manage your General Betting Duty, including daily records of bets taken, winnings paid out and copies of betting slips you issue. You must keep these for at least three years.

Find the record-keeping requirements for General Betting Duty in Notice 451 on the HMRC website.

Pool Betting Duty

Pool Betting Duty is paid by UK pool betting promoters, or operators of a totalisator in the UK.

Pool betting is generally where the odds are not fixed - for example, because the winners will share the stakes or some other amount, or the winnings are paid out at someone’s discretion.

The duty is charged at 15 per cent of pool betting receipts plus expenses and profits not met out of stake money, less winnings.

Find information on pool betting in Notice 147 on the HMRC website.

Exemptions from Pool Betting Duty

Pool Betting Duty is not due on:

  • bets made on horse or dog racing
  • bets made with a promoter who qualifies for community benefit - but, you will still need a permit
  • fixed odds bets as these are generally liable to General Betting Duty
  • lotteries
  • certain private, not-for-profit and other pool betting such as office sweepstakes

Applying for a permit

Before you can start a pool betting business - even if the pool betting is made for community benefit - you need to apply for a permit, using form BD 2.

If you’re a private trader, you’ll need to complete form EX 103. If you’re a limited company or other incorporated body, you’ll need to complete a form EX 103A.

You must complete and return the form to HMRC at the address below at least a week before you start your business. If your pool betting is for community benefit, you must also send a copy of the competition’s rules and any supporting documents you have to prove this.

HMRC National Registration Unit
Portcullis House
21 India Street
Glasgow
G2 4PZ

Find form EX 103 - Excise Entry: Private Trader on the HMRC website

Find form EX 103A - Excise Entry: Incorporated Company on the HMRC website.

Find form BD 2 - Application for a Pool Betting Permit on HMRC website.

Find out about permits needed for pool betting in Notice 147 on the HMRC website.

Returns and payments

HMRC will send you a Pool Betting Duty return - form BD 1 - for each accounting period. This is usually a four or five-week period ending on the last Saturday of a calendar month. You can also now apply for quarterly accounting periods and duty returns if you meet certain conditions.

Find out about quarterly accounting conditions for pool betting duty in Notice 147 on the HMRC website.

You must make sure your completed return form including your payment is received by HMRC no later than the 15th day of the month following the end of the accounting period - or if that day is a weekend or public holiday, the previous working day. You must complete and return the form even if you didn’t carry out any business in the period.

You can download form BD 1 from the HMRC website.

Find form BD 1 on the HMRC website.

How to pay Pool Betting Duty

Record keeping for Pool Betting Duty

You must keep specific records to manage your Pool Betting Duty. This includes details of stake money, your expenses and any winnings paid out - you’ll need to keep these for at least six years. You’ll also need to keep coupons, entry forms and tickets for at least six months.

Read advice on record keeping for Pool Betting Duty in Notice 147 on the HMRC website.

Lottery Duty

Lottery Duty only applies to the National Lottery. Any other lawful lottery is automatically exempt from Lottery Duty.

The duty is charged at 12 per cent of gross stakes on the taking of a National Lottery ticket.

Exemptions from Lottery Duty

Apart from the National Lottery, there are only a few lawful types of lottery, all of which are exempt from Lottery Duty:

  • lotteries promoted by an ‘exempt entertainment’ - for example, a bazaar, a fete or a sporting or athletic event
  • private lotteries where tickets are only sold to members of a society, people on the society’s premises or people who all work or live on the same premises - such as office sweepstakes and office party raffles
  • lotteries promoted by a charitable society supporting a specific purpose which isn’t for private gain or a commercial undertaking - this must comply with conditions set out in the Gambling Act 2005
  • lotteries promoted by local authorities that comply with the conditions set out in the Gambling Act 2005

All other lotteries in the UK are unlawful. Unlawful lotteries are not exempt from Lottery Duty.

Read guidance on Lottery Duty in Notice 458 on the HMRC website.
