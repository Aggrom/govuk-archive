Taking your pet abroad, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Before you leave the UK

Under the PETS Travel Scheme, you can take your pet cat, dog or ferret abroad and return to the UK.

Before travelling, you must take your animal to a vet so it can be:

  • fitted with a microchip
  • vaccinated against rabies
  • issued with a Pet Passport

Once these steps are carried out, you can travel to (and return from) most EU countries and some non-EU countries.

To see a full list of countries, follow the links below.

EU countries
Non-EU countries

You must wait 21 days from the date of the vaccination before travelling.

Unlisted countries

An ‘unlisted’ country is any country not included in the list of EU or non-EU countries. If you return from an unlisted country, extra steps need to be taken.

Booking the return journey before you leave

You must book your return journey with a PETS-approved sea, air or rail route.

Most major ferry companies, airlines and rail operators are approved.

Returning to the UK

When you return to the UK, your pet’s microchip will be scanned and its passport inspected.

Before you travel

Before travelling, take your animal to a vet so it can be:

  • fitted with a microchip
  • vaccinated against rabies
  • issued with a Pet Passport

Your pet must also:

  • have a blood sample taken by a vet at least 30 days after it was vaccinated
  • have passed the blood test (the vet will give you a compliance certificate)

If you don’t take these extra steps

If you plan to return from an unlisted country and didn’t take these extra steps before leaving, your pet must:

  • have a blood sample taken at least 30 days after it was vaccinated
  • have passed the blood test (the vet will give you a compliance certificate)
  • have waited at least 3 months since the blood test was taken before it can return to the UK

Returning to the UK

When you return to the UK, your pet’s microchip will be scanned and its passport inspected.

If you don’t have the correct paperwork, your pet will be put into quarantine or sent back to the country it has travelled from.

Part 3: Travelling with registered assistance dogs

If you have an assistant dog, you must follow the same rules as for non-assistant dogs.

The Guide Dogs for the Blind Association has written guidelines about taking registered assistance dogs abroad.

Pets entering the UK on airlines under the PETS scheme must normally be carried in the hold. However, assistance dogs are normally allowed to travel in the cabin with their owner on approved PETS routes and carriers for assistance dogs.
