Fishing vessels: licence upgrades after re-engining

If you modify your fishing vessel by increasing its engine size, you need to get extra licence cover to avoid invalidating your current licence.

Introduction

The fishing vessel licensing system helps control UK fishing opportunities so that the EU regulations for sustainable fisheries management can be met. Without a licence, UK registered fishing vessels cannot fish anywhere for sea fish that will be sold.

As part of the licensing system, all vessels must have their specifications correctly recorded, which includes the engine power. Your licence will become invalid if this is not correctly recorded.

Why is engine power important?

All licensed fishing vessels in the UK must be registered with the UK Ship Register - Registry of Shipping and Seamen.

Under EU regulations the engine power in kilowatts must be recorded as part of the vessel’s registration. This allows the government to know the overall capacity of the UK fishing fleet.

The recorded power can be either:

  • the vessel’s maximum continuous engine power (MCEP)
  • the permanently de-rated engine power

Where the power of an engine is de-rated, the de-rated power should not normally be less than 75% of the MCEP.

Failure to record the engine power properly

If your vessel is inspected and it is found that the engine power is greater than is recorded on the licence, the licence will immediately become invalid and will be converted to an entitlement. You will not be issued with another licence until the engine power has been corrected and you have produced a corrected certificate of registry.

See the guides on applying for a fishing vessel licence and types of fishing vessel licences.

Increasing your vessel’s engine power

You can increase the engine power of an existing vessel by either:

  • replacing your engine with a more powerful model
  • increasing the power output of your existing engine

All increases require you to obtain additional licence cover.

Using components from a disaggregated licence entitlement

A licence entitlement belonging to someone else can be split or ‘disaggregated’ and from that, you can acquire the engine power components that you need. You can add these components to your current vessel licence to cover the increase in engine power. But you must make sure that the vessel receiving the components is within the same fleet band as the donor entitlement - over 10 metres, or 10 metres and under.

Capacity penalties

When you use components from a disaggregated licence entitlement and add them to your current vessel licence you have to pay:

  • a 5% capacity penalty on the tonnage and kilowatts of your main licence
  • a 10% capacity penalty on the kilowatts you acquire

Example of fishing vessel re-engining

Re-engining Kilowatts
Current licence and vessel engine power 100
Proposed vessel engine power 130
Increase in kilowatts 30
10% penalty on aggregated components 3
5% penalty on main licence 5
Kilowatts required to cover increase 38

Increasing the kilowatts may mean you have to acquire more gross registered tonnage, which may also be subject to a 10% penalty vessel licence. For more information, see the guide on changes to your fishing vessel licence.

What happens to any surpluses

It can be difficult to find an exact match in entitlements to provide licence cover for your increased engine power so you could end up with a surplus of kilowatts. Any surplus left after the transaction can be converted into an entitlement that is valid for 5 years.

If any surpluses are split in a later transaction the original validity date continues to apply to the new entitlements created.
