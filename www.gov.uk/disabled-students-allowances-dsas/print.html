Benefits & credits: Disabled Students' Allowances (DSAs)

Notes

Part 1: Overview

As a higher education student living in England, you can apply for a Disabled Students’ Allowance (DSA) if you have a:

  • disability
  • long-term health condition
  • mental health condition
  • specific learning difficulty such as dyslexia

DSAs are paid on top of student finance and you don’t have to pay them back. The amount you get depends on your individual needs and not on income.

Part 2: What you'll get

The amount of Disabled Students’ Allowances (DSAs) you can get depends on your individual needs - not your household income.

You can get help with the costs of:

  • specialist equipment, like computer software
  • non-medical helpers, like a note-taker or reader
  • extra travel costs you have to pay because of your disability
  • other costs, like photocopying

DSAs don’t cover disability-related costs you’d have if you weren’t attending a course, or costs that any student might have.

Your ‘needs assessment’

Once your eligibility for DSAs has been confirmed, Student Finance England will ask you to have a ‘needs assessment’ test to work out what help you need.

You shouldn’t book a needs assessment until Student Finance England ask you to.

The assessment is paid for through any DSAs entitlement you may have.

After the assessment, you’ll get a report listing equipment and other support you can get for your course.

Maximum amounts

The figures below are the maximum you can get - most students get less than this.

Type of student Specialist equipment allowance Non-medical helper allowance General allowance
Full-time Up to £5,161 for the whole course Up to £20,520 a year Up to £1,724 a year
Part-time Up to £5,161 for the whole course Up to £15,390 a year Up to £1,293 a year

Postgraduate students can get a single allowance of up to £10,260.

Part-time students

If you’re a part-time student the ‘course intensity’ may affect how much DSA you get. Intensity means how long your course takes to complete each year compared to an equivalent full-time course. Your course must:

  • last at least 1 year
  • not last more than 4 times as long as the equivalent full-time course

If you start a course before 1 September 2012 your course must not last more than twice as long as the equivalent full-time course.

How DSAs are paid

Money is paid directly to who provides the service or equipment or into your bank account.

Part 3: Eligibility

You can apply for Disabled Students’ Allowances (DSAs) if you have a:

  • disability or long-term health condition
  • mental health condition
  • specific learning difficulty like dyslexia or dyspraxia

You must also:

  • be an undergraduate or postgraduate student (including Open University or distance learning)
  • have a condition that affects your ability to study
  • qualify for student finance from Student Finance England
  • be studying on a course that lasts at least a year

Who isn’t eligible

You can’t get DSAs from Student Finance England if you’re:

  • an EU student
  • eligible for an NHS Disabled Students’ Allowance (this is a separate scheme)
  • getting equivalent support from another funding source - eg from your university or a social work bursary

Part-time students

For students starting courses from 1 September 2012, part-time courses must be at least 25% of the ‘intensity’ of the equivalent full-time course each year.

You can check course intensity with your university or college.

Example

If your course takes 6 years to complete but would take 3 years full-time, it has an intensity of 50%.

Proving you’re eligible

You won’t automatically get DSAs - you need proof of your eligibility, like a letter from your doctor or specialist.

If you have a specific learning difficulty such as dyslexia, you’ll need to provide a ‘diagnostic assessment’ from a psychologist or suitably qualified specialist teacher. If you had this done when you were under 16, you may need to get reassessed.

If you need a new diagnostic assessment but can’t afford to pay for it, you may be able to get financial help through the Access to Learning Fund.

Your course

Your course must be in the UK and one of the following:

  • a first degree, eg BA, BSc or BEd
  • a Foundation Degree
  • a Certificate of Higher Education
  • a Diploma of Higher Education (DipHE)
  • a Higher National Certificate (HNC)
  • a Higher National Diploma (HND)
  • a Postgraduate Certificate of Education (PGCE)
  • Initial Teacher Training
  • further training of youth and community workers

Check with your university or college that your course is recognised.

Part 4: How to claim

To apply for Disabled Students’ Allowances (DSAs) you need to fill in an application form. You should also download the guidance notes to help you complete it.

Part 5: Further information

Download ‘Bridging the Gap, a guide to Disabled Students Allowances (DSAs) 2012/13’ (PDF, 411KB)

If you need advice about financial help, contact the disability adviser at your university or college.

If your circumstances change

If your circumstances change, contact Student Finance England.

For example:

  • if your condition gets worse you may be able to get extra help
  • if you change your course you may still get DSAs but you won’t get any extra equipment allowance

Appeals

If your application is turned down, you can ask for an explanation or for Student Finance England to review your case. Contact Student Finance England for more details.
