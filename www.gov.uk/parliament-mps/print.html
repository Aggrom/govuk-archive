Parliament and MPs, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Your MP represents your interests in the House of Commons, which is part of the UK Parliament.

Parliament is based in Westminster in London.

Its role is to:

  • look at what the government is doing
  • debate and pass new laws
  • set taxes
  • debate issues

MPs can use their position to ask government ministers questions about current issues.

You can find out more about how Parliament works on the Parliament website.

House of Commons

The House of Commons has 650 elected MPs. The breakdown of areas (constituencies) they represent is as follows:

  • 533 in England
  • 59 in Scotland
  • 40 in Wales
  • 18 in Northern Ireland

House of Lords

This is the ‘upper house’ of Parliament. It works with the Commons to:

  • make laws
  • scrutinise the government
  • provide independent expertise

Members of the Lords can question government ministers to find out what they’re doing.

Part 2: Political parties

A political party is a group of people who have decided to work together because they share similar ideas about how the country should be run.

The government and the opposition

After a general election, the party with the most MPs usually forms the new government. The second largest party becomes the official opposition. They can:

  • oppose government proposals they disagree with
  • put forward their own policies to try to persuade people to vote for them at the next general election

You can find out who is in the government and the official opposition on the Parliament website.

Leaders of the government and the official opposition sit opposite each other on the front benches in the House of Commons. Their supporters are called the ‘backbenchers’ - they sit behind them.

Most candidates in elections belong to one of the main parties. If an MP doesn’t have a political party, they’re called an ‘independent’.

Read the information on voting to find out more about how to vote in elections.

Political parties represented at Parliament

The following parties have members in the House of Commons or the House of Lords:

  • Alliance Party
  • Conservative Party
  • Co-operative Party
  • Democratic Unionist Party (DUP)
  • Green Party
  • Labour Party
  • Liberal Democrat Party
  • Plaid Cymru
  • Respect
  • Scottish National Party (SNP)
  • Sinn Féin
  • Social Democratic and Labour Party (SDLP)
  • UK Independence Party (UKIP)
  • Ulster Unionist Party (UUP)

There are also independent crossbench peers in the House of Lords, who are not told how to vote by a political party.

Part 3: The work of an MP

MPs split their time between working:

  • in Parliament itself
  • in the local area (constituency) that elected them
  • for their political party

Some MPs are also government ministers with responsibilities in certain areas, like health or defence.

When Parliament is meeting, MPs often spend their time working in the House of Commons. Their work here includes:

  • raising issues that affect their constituency
  • attending debates
  • voting on new laws

MPs in their constituency

MPs often hold ‘surgeries’ in their local office where people can come and discuss things that concern them. These are advertised in local newspapers and public libraries and anyone who lives in their constituency can go along.

Select committees

These are small groups of MPs that deal with a particular area of work in detail, like new laws or wider topics like human rights.

They report their conclusions and recommendations to the House of Commons. They can question ministers, civil servants, individuals and any other interested groups.

MPs and the standards they follow

There are various things in place to make sure MPs carry out their work in line with a set of rules.

Committee on Standards in Public Life

This keeps a watch on people in public life, like MPs. It looks at areas like MPs’ expenses or how political parties raise money for campaigns.

You can’t complain to the committee about specific people but you can suggest areas they can look at.

There’s more information about the committee on the Committee on Standards in Public Life website.

Parliamentary Commissioner for Standards

The commissioner trains MPs in the rules set out by their code of conduct and helps them stick to these rules. If you think an MP has broken these rules you can make a complaint to the commissioner.

There’s more information about the Parliamentary Commissioner for Standards on the Parliament website.

Part 4: How your MP can help you

Your MP can help you if your concern is something that the government is responsible for. For example, it could relate to the NHS, taxes, benefits or pensions.

If you’re concerned about things like Council Tax, local social services or problems in schools, you should contact your local council instead.

Contacting your MP

  • writing a letter to the address below
