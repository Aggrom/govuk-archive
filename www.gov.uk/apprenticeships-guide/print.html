Apprenticeships, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

Anyone aged 16 or over and not in full-time education can apply for an apprenticeship.

Apprenticeships are work-based programmes that combine practical training with study. They take between 1 and 4 years to complete depending on the level of apprenticeship.

An apprentice:

  • works alongside experienced staff
  • gains job-specific skills
  • earns a wage
  • studies - usually 1 day a week - towards a related qualification

Visit the Skills Development Scotland website for information about apprenticeships in Scotland.

Levels of apprenticeship

There are 3 levels in England:

  • Intermediate – equivalent to 5 GCSE passes
  • Advanced – equivalent to 2 A level passes
  • Higher Apprenticeships – lead to NVQ Level 4 or Foundation Degree

Part 2: What apprentices get

Pay and holidays

Apprentices are paid from the first day of their apprenticeship. The minimum wage for an apprentice is £2.65 per hour and applies to workers aged 16 to 18 and those aged 19 and over in the first year of their apprenticeship.

But many earn more. On average apprentices earn £170 per week and some around £210.

Those aged 19 or over who have completed the first year of their apprenticeship will be paid at least the National Minimum Wage rate for their age.

Apprentices get at least 20 days of holiday per year, plus bank holidays.

Apprentices can’t be made redundant.

Qualifications

Apprenticeships can lead to:

  • National Vocational Qualifications (NVQs) at Level 2,3,4 or 5
  • Functional Skills qualifications, eg in maths, English or ICT
  • a technical certificate, such as a BTEC or City & Guilds Progression Award
  • knowledge-based qualifications, such as a Higher National Certificate (HNC), a Higher National Diploma (HND) or a Foundation degree

Types of apprenticeships

There is information about the different types of apprenticeships on the Apprenticeships website.

Apply

Candidates can get started and apply for an apprenticeship.

Part 3: Taking on an apprentice

Apprenticeships can be used to train both new and existing employees. Employers can get funding to train apprentices.

Funding

The amount of funding employers can get for apprenticeship training depends on their sector and the age of the candidate:

  • aged 16 to 18 - 100% of cost paid
  • aged 19 to 24 - 50% of cost paid
  • 25 years and older - employers may only get a contribution

This is paid directly to the organisation that provides and supports the apprenticeship - in most cases the learning provider.

Apprenticeship grant for employers

Employers may also be eligible for a £1,500 apprenticeship grant.

These grants are available to help small-medium sized employers to recruit up to 10 apprentices for the first time, and are in addition to the training costs you get for apprentices.

Taking on an apprentice

The Apprenticeships website shows the steps to get an apprenticeship started. The Skills Development Scotland website has advice if you’re in Scotland.
