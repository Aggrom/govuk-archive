Managing and conserving wildlife

Introduction

Wildlife management is an important part of agriculture. As part of cross compliance and the requirement to keep land in good agricultural and environmental condition, it is your responsibility as a farmer to take account of wildlife and the natural habitat and stay within the law. There are certain species of animals and plants that are legally protected from being harmed or disturbed. In addition, some wild mammals and birds are considered pests because they can harm livestock, damage crops and spread disease.

This guide gives information about which species are legally protected. It includes advice on how to apply for a licence to take action involving a protected species if you have a valid reason.

Finally, there’s information about where to find further advice and funding opportunities to support conservation on farms.

Protected species of wild animals and plants

Certain species of animals and plants found in the wild in the UK are legally protected from being harmed or disturbed. These species are listed in the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 or are named as European Protected Species in the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2010.

There is also legislation that legally protects certain animals - for example, the Protection of Badgers Act (1992) protects badgers and their setts, and the Deer Act (1991) places restrictions on actions that can be taken against deer species.

Find advice and licensing information on badgers on the Natural England website.

Find advice and licensing information on deer on the Natural England website.

It is possible to get licences for certain activities that would otherwise be illegal under the above Acts and Regulations.

You can find information about the legal status of specific animal and plant species on the Natural England website.

You can download a list of species protected under the EC Habitats Directive from the Natural England website (PDF, 126K).

Agricultural pest control

Some wild mammals and birds are considered pests and may need to be controlled in order to:

  • prevent the spread of disease
  • prevent damage to livestock or crops
  • prevent damage to feedstuffs
  • preserve public health and safety

You also have a duty to control the populations of rats and mice under the Prevention of Damage by Pests Act 1949. This law states that you must take reasonable steps to keep your land free of rats and mice. If you don’t, your local authority is entitled to deal with the problem and recover the costs from you.

For more advice about suitable methods of rodent control, see our guide on managing wildlife and pests.

Under the Agriculture Act 1947, you also have a duty to control rabbits and other damaging pests, and to prevent them from causing damage to your neighbours’ property.

Using spring traps

If you wish to use spring traps for controlling pests, you must only use a type that’s been approved for use in England, and in the manner permitted.

Traps must only be set in natural or artificial tunnels in accordance with the relevant conditions of use. Some traps incorporate a ‘suitable’ tunnel, which minimises the chances of injuring or killing non-target species without compromising the capture and killing of targeted pests.

Using snares

The Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) has published a best practice guide to using snares to control fox and rabbit populations. You can download Defra’s code of practice on the use of snares in fox and rabbit control from the Agricultural Document Library (ADLib) website (PDF, 47K).

Wildlife management advice and licensing

Advice on wildlife management issues is available from Natural England’s Wildlife Management and Licensing Service (WMLS).

You can find the latest news and find out more about the role of the WMLS on the Natural England website.

Natural England publishes a wide range of leaflets on how to deal with many of the different wildlife problems you may encounter as a farmer. These are available online or from Natural England offices.

Find Natural England offices and contact information on the Natural England website.

If you have a wildlife problem that is not covered by the advisory leaflets - or you are unsure which form you need to complete - you can call the Natural England WMLS Helpline on Tel 0845 601 4523 or email them at wildlife@naturalengland.org.uk.

Licences for pest control

Some species - such as rats, mice, rabbits and foxes - can be controlled without a licence. However, if you wish to take action against protected species - or use particular methods of control - you will need to apply for a licence first.

Some protected species - that are also considered to be regular pests - are covered by a General Licence, which allows action to be taken against them for certain specified purposes. You can find information on general licences on the Natural England website.

For all other protected species, an individual licence needs to be obtained before action can be taken against the species.

Natural England can issue licences in England to authorise wildlife management activities that would otherwise be illegal and where a valid justification exists. Read about wildlife management licensing on the Natural England website.

Licences are issued free of charge. Many licence applications can be completed online, but paper application forms can be submitted as an alternative.

If a licence is granted, it will usually be issued with a report form that must be completed and returned once the licensed action has been taken.

Find application forms and advice about obtaining a licence on the Natural England website.

Wildlife conservation on farms

Landscape and wildlife conservation is one of the government’s key objectives for the UK agricultural industry. Advice and funding are available to help you achieve this.

Conservation advice

All farmers in England are entitled to free on-farm advice aimed at encouraging the use of agricultural practices and techniques that promote conservation. This service is provided on behalf of the Defra by ADAS and the Farming and Wildlife Advisory Group (FWAG).

If you are interested in receiving this advice, please contact your local ADAS or FWAG office directly. You can find your nearest ADAS office on the ADAS website or find your nearest FWAG office on the FWAG website.

You can also find useful information in our guide on farming and conservation: the basics.

Hedgerows and field boundaries

Hedgerows provide an important habitat for many species of wildlife. They also provide some additional agricultural benefits, including:

  • reducing soil erosion
  • sheltering crops and livestock
  • controlling livestock

You can find information about the importance of hedges and hedgerow management advice on the Hedgelink website.

Hedgerow management is also covered by the standards of good agricultural and environmental condition.

Other boundaries, such as field margins, can be established to provide a species-rich flora and fauna habitat. These can also offer a source of food and shelter for farmland birds, and act as a buffer to water courses.

Environmental Stewardship

Environmental Stewardship is a scheme that provides financial support for land management practices which keep land in a good environmental condition. You can find more information in our guide on the Environmental Stewardship: the basics.

Further information on managing and conserving wildlife

Below are details of organisations that can offer advice and support with wildlife management and conservation issues.

One of the major roles of the Defra is to help the farming industry operate as efficiently as possible. Defra administers European support policies that provide around £3 billion to UK agriculture. They also oversee a number of agencies that work with arable farmers, imports and exports of crops and implement pest and disease controls. You can call the Defra Helpline on Tel 08459 33 55 77.

You can also find detailed information on managing and conserving wildlife on the Defra website.

The Rural Payments Agency (RPA) is responsible for licences and schemes for growers, as well as for running the Single Payment Scheme (SPS). For more information about SPS and how it can help your farming business, you can call the RPA Helpline on Tel 0845 603 7777.

You can also read our guide: the Single Payment Scheme (SPS).

In England, the Farm Advisory System advises farmers about cross compliance. For further information, call the Cross Compliance Helpline on Tel 0845 345 1302. Alternatively, find information on cross compliance requirement on the Cross Compliance website.

The National Farmers’ Union (NFU) represents the farmers and growers of England and Wales. It aims to promote successful and socially responsible agriculture and horticulture, while ensuring the long-term viability of rural communities.

You can read about the work of the NFU on the NFU website.

Farmers are likely to come into contact with local authorities over a number of farming, land use, food standards and environmental regulations. Your local authority may also be able to provide further information or resources. You can find contact details for your local authority in our Contacts Directory.
