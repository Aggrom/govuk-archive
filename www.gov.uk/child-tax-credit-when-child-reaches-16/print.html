Child Tax Credit when your child reaches 16, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If your child is aged 16 to 19, you can get Child Tax Credit if they are:

  • staying on at school or college
  • starting an approved training course
  • registered with certain careers services (maximum of 20 weeks eligibility for Child Tax Credit)

You need to tell the Tax Credit Office if any of the above apply for your child to continue to receive payments.

Part 2: Full-time education

‘Full-time’ means being taught or supervised for more than an average of 12 hours a week.

Your child needs to have started, enrolled or been accepted onto a course that counts before their 19th birthday.

Your child could be eligible if they are studying for qualifications like:

  • A levels
  • Scottish Highers
  • NVQ at Level 3

Your child is not eligible if their education is:

  • provided by their employer
  • provided through a role they hold - for example if they are a scout leader or councillor, and the education is part of that role
  • a higher education course, such as a degree

Part 3: Approved training courses

You can still get Child Tax Credit up to your child’s 20th birthday if they are on an approved course which:

  • is unpaid
  • teaches them skills needed to do a job

Your child needs to have started, enrolled or been accepted onto an approved course before their 19th birthday.

For a training course to be approved it should be one of the following:

England

  • Foundation Learning Programmes (previously known as ‘Entry to Employment’)
  • Access to Apprenticeships or unwaged Programme Led Apprenticeships

Scotland

  • Get Ready for Work (including West Lothian Council’s Skills Training Programme)
  • Skillseekers

Wales

  • Foundation Apprenticeships
  • Traineeships
  • Skillbuild/Skillbuild+ (if started before 1 August 2011)

Northern Ireland

  • Programme Led Apprenticeships (Apprenticeships NI)
  • Jobskills
  • Training for Success: Professional and Technical Training

A course that is part of a job contract doesn’t count as approved. For example, Modern Apprenticeships in Scotland are always provided as part of a job contract, so don’t count as approved.

Part 4: Careers services

Child Tax Credit payments usually stop on 31 August after your child’s 16th birthday.

You can continue getting payments for up to 20 weeks if you child is 16 or 17 and registered with any of the organisations listed below. The 20 weeks start from the date your child left education or training.

England

  • the local careers service
  • Connexions

Scotland or Wales

  • the local careers service

Northern Ireland

  • the careers service of the Department for Employment and Learning
  • Education or Library Board

Ministry of Defence

  • if your child has applied for and is waiting to join the Armed Forces

Europe

  • a similar organisation to others in this list in any European Economic Area (EEA) country

You must let the Tax Credit Office know within 3 months of your child leaving education or training that they have registered with any of these organisations. If you don’t, you won’t be able to get the extra money.

If your child stops being registered with any of the organisations above before the end of the 20 weeks, you must tell the Tax Credit Office straight away.

Part 5: Reporting changes

Changes to report within one month

Tell the Tax Credit Office within one month if your child:

  • leaves education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit
  • starts advanced education, such as a course at university level
  • starts education or training that’s provided as part of their job
  • starts education provided as part of a role - such as scout leader or councillor
  • gets a paid job and they’re usually paid to work for 24 hours or more a week - and they’ve left education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit
  • starts to receive benefits or tax credits in their own right
  • stops being registered with a careers service like Connexions - where you are getting the extra period of tax credits for them (of up to 20 weeks)
  • gets married or enters a civil partnership - or starts living with a partner as if they are

You need to report these changes to the Tax Credit Office or you could be paid too much. If you are overpaid, you’ll usually have to pay the money back. You could also be charged a penalty.

Changes to report within 3 months

Tell the Tax Credit Office within 3 months if your child:

  • stays on in education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit after they reach 16
  • is aged 16 or 17, stops education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit, but registers with a careers service - like Connexions

If you don’t, you’ll only get Child Tax Credit for them until 31 August after their 16th birthday.
