Child Tax Credit when your child reaches 16, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

If your child is aged 16 to 19, you can get Child Tax Credit if they are:

  • staying on in eligible education
  • starting an approved training course
  • leaving education but registering with the armed forces or certain careers services (maximum of 20 weeks eligibility for Child Tax Credit)

You need to tell the Tax Credit Office if any of the above apply so that your child continues to get payments.

Part 2: Full-time education

You can get Child Tax Credit until your child turns 20 if they’re in eligible education. They must be accepted onto the course before they turn 19.

Courses that count

Education must be full-time (more than an average of 12 hours’ supervised study a week) and ‘non-advanced’. This includes:

  • GCSEs
  • A levels
  • NVQ/SVQ level 1, 2 or 3
  • BTEC National Diploma, National Certificate and First Diploma
  • SCE higher grade or similar

It doesn’t include ‘advanced’ education, eg:

  • a degree
  • Diploma of Higher Education
  • NVQ level 4 or above
  • BTEC Higher National Certificate (HNC) or Higher National Diploma (HND)
  • teacher training

Courses are not eligible if they’re provided by the child’s employer or provided through an office the child holds - eg the education is part of their role as a scout leader.

Part 3: Approved training courses

You can still get Child Tax Credit up to your child’s 20th birthday if they are on an approved course that:

  • is unpaid
  • teaches them skills needed to do a job

Your child needs to have started, enrolled or been accepted onto an approved course before their 19th birthday.

Approved courses are:

  • Foundation Learning Programmes (or ‘Entry to Employment’)
  • Get Ready for Work (including West Lothian Council’s Skills Training Programme)
  • Skillbuild/Skillbuild+ (if started before 1 August 2011)
  • Skillseekers
  • Jobskills
  • Training for Success: Professional and Technical Training
  • Traineeships
  • Foundation Apprenticeships
  • Access to Apprenticeships (or unwaged Programme Led Apprenticeships)
  • Programme Led Apprenticeships (Apprenticeships NI)

A course provided by an employer as part of a job doesn’t count as approved eg Modern Apprenticeships in Scotland.

Part 4: Breaks in education or training

You may get Child Tax Credit for 6 months if the break is for a good reason eg your child is changing course or pregnant.

You may be able to get Child Tax Credit for longer than 6 months if your child’s full-time education is interrupted because of illness or disability.

Voluntary breaks

You can’t normally get Child Tax Credit if your child has taken a voluntary break in education or training, eg if they travel overseas for personal reasons.

After the break

If your child goes back to education or training after a break, you should make a new claim.

If your child doesn’t go back into eligible education or training, you may have to pay back some of the payments you got during the break.

Part 5: Your child leaves education or training

If your child leaves education or training your Child Tax Credits will stop. You can get them paid for an extra 20 weeks if they register with their local careers service or Connexions (and also the Education or Library Board in Northern Ireland).

You’ll also get the extra credits if they registered with a similar organisation in the European Economic Area or join the Armed Forces.

To claim, tell the Tax Credit Office within 3 months of the date they left their education or training.

You must report changes to your work or family life to the Tax Credit Office as they can affect how much you get.

Part 6: Extensions: careers services and the armed forces

Child Tax Credit payments usually stop on 31 August after your child’s 16th birthday.

You can continue getting payments for up to 20 weeks if your child is 16 or 17 and registered with one of the following qualifying organisations:

  • in England – the local careers service or Connexions
  • in Scotland or Wales – the local careers service
  • in Northern Ireland – the careers service of the Department for Employment and Learning, or an Education and Library Board
  • the Ministry of Defence, eg if your child is waiting to join HM Forces
  • a similar organisation to others in this list in any European Economic Area (EEA) Country

EEA countries

The EEA includes:

  • all countries in the European Union (EU)
  • Norway, Iceland and Liechtenstein

The 20 weeks start from the date your child left education or training.

Changes that affect the extension

The extension ends if your child:

  • turns 18
  • starts paid work for 24 or more hours a week
  • gets certain benefits (eg Income Support) or tax credits
  • is no longer registered with a qualifying organisation

You must let the Tax Credit Office know within 3 months of your child leaving education or training that they have registered with any of these organisations. If you don’t, you won’t be able to get the extra money.

If your child stops being registered with any of the organisations above before the end of the 20 weeks, you must tell the Tax Credit Office straight away.

Part 7: Reporting changes

Changes to report within 1 month

Tell the Tax Credit Office within 1 month if your child:

  • leaves education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit
  • starts advanced education, such as a course at university level
  • starts education or training that’s provided as part of their job
  • starts education provided as part of a role - such as scout leader or councillor
  • gets a paid job and they’re usually paid to work for 24 hours or more a week - and they’ve left education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit
  • starts to get benefits or tax credits in their own right
  • stops being registered with a careers service like Connexions - where you are getting the extra period of tax credits for them (of up to 20 weeks)
  • gets married or enters a civil partnership - or starts living with a partner as if they are

You need to report these changes to the Tax Credit Office or you could be paid too much. If you’re overpaid, you’ll usually have to pay the money back. You could also be charged a penalty.

Changes to report within 3 months

Tell the Tax Credit Office within 3 months if your child:

  • stays on in education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit after they reach 16
  • is aged 16 or 17, stops education or training that counts for Child Tax Credit, but registers with a careers service - like Connexions

If you don’t, you’ll only get Child Tax Credit for them until 31 August after their 16th birthday.
