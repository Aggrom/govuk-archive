Licences for offshore renewable energy installations

How to apply for licences to develop wind farms, wave and tidal devices, the cost of these licences, and potential environmental impact.

Introduction

The Marine Management Organisation (MMO) is responsible for promoting clean, healthy, safe, productive and biologically diverse oceans and seas.

Since 1 April 2010, the MMO has been able to grant licences for offshore energy-generating installations including wind farms, wave and tidal devices. There are currently around 274 wind turbines installed around the English and Welsh coasts, producing around 851.2 megawatts of electricity.

This guide explains how to apply for licences for developing energy-generating installations and outlines the cost of these licences. It also looks at the potential environmental impact of using renewable energy.

Licence applications for renewable energy developments

Consent for the construction of renewable energy installations is given by the Secretary of State for the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra).

As a wind farm developer you will need:

  • a licence under the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 (FEPA)
  • consent under the Coast Protection Act 1949 (CPA)
  • consent under section 36 of the Electricity Act 1989 - or under the Transport and Works Act if the project is based in territorial waters - find information on electricity development consents on the Department of Energy and Climate Change (DECC) website.
  • a wildlife licence for European protected species

Safety zones for offshore renewable energy developments

You must apply to the Secretary of State if you want to place a safety zone around an offshore renewable energy installation. Read the Electricity (Offshore Generating Stations) (Safety Zones) (Application Procedures and Control of Access) Regulations 2007 on the Legislation.gov.uk website.

A FEPA licence

You will need a FEPA licence if your renewable energy development involves depositing substances in the sea or under the seabed.

A FEPA licence sets out conditions for developers, eg monitoring requirements, which validate environmental assessments.

A year to get a licence

FEPA aims to issue a licence and CPA consent for a wind farm, wave or tidal device within 12 months of receiving the application. Find licensing information on the MMO website.

You can also find an online application form to undertake marine energy works on the MMO website.

Licence fees for renewable energy developments

Application charges apply for most categories of renewable energy installation licence, covering administrative fees and scientific assessment and enforcement:

  • band 1 (less than 1 megawatt (MW)) - £2,000
  • band 2 (1-4.99MW) - £6,000
  • band 3 (5-100MW) - £26,222

Variations are charged as follows:

  • band 1 (less than 1 MW) - £600
  • band 2 (1 to 4.99 MW) - £1,100
  • band 3 (5 to 100 MW) - £2,100
  • band 4 (more than 100 MW) - £2,590

Monitoring and licence management (formerly extensions/longer term instalments) are charged as follows:

  • band 1 (less than 1 MW) - £1,100
  • band 2 (1 to 4.99 MW) - £2,250
  • band 3 (5 to 100 MW) - £8,270

There is also an environment sensitivity supplement for marine works proposed within a 2 kilometre radius of nature conservations, sites of specific scientific interest or conservation areas. These are:

  • band 1 (less than 1 MW) - £950
  • band 2 (1 to 4.99 MW) - £1,350
  • band 3 (5 to 100 MW) - £1,720

Application fees for Electricity Act 1989 consents are set out in The Electricity (Offshore Generating Stations) (Applications for Consent) Regulations 2006. They are:

  • generating station of less than 200 MW capacity - £5,000
  • generating station of between 200 and 500 MW capacity - £12,000
  • generating station more than 500 MW capacity - £20,000

Environmental impact of renewable energy developments

Under the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) Directive, an EIA must be carried out to support an application for the development of renewable energy works.

An EIA investigates a project’s environmental impact so that the public and relevant authorities are aware of possible effects and how to reduce them. You can find information on construction on the MMO website.

Environmental statement

An Environmental statement (ES) is the main document that supports an application for a licence or consent. It must contain certain information on coastal processes, bathymetric surveys, benthic communities and fish surveys.

If you are a developer, an ES can provide several benefits, including:

  • providing you with a framework for environmental considerations and design development
  • encouraging you to avoid or reduce harmful effects of the development
  • allowing a quicker decision from consenting authorities

Wildlife and conservation issues for renewable energy installations

Natural England nature conservation advisers view and advise the MMO about applications and more general conservation issues. Natural England is responsible for conservation issues in England that are of up to 12 nautical miles, any area larger than this is dealt with by the Joint Nature Conservation Committee.

Nature conservation agencies can request an assessment of your proposed project to see how it will impact on European protected sites.

Wildlife licences

A wildlife licence authorises the disturbance of certain species during the construction of wind farms. It also allows scientific surveys to be carried out for specific purposes where there is no other alternative. If you apply for a wildlife licence, you will need to undergo an informal screening process to assess:

  • whether or not you need to apply for a licence
  • the most appropriate application form to use
  • what information you need to provide

Read about the screening process for wildlife licence applications on the MMO website.

Your application will be acknowledged within five working days and a decision made within six weeks. If you do not hear within six weeks, you must not assume a licence has been granted.

Marine Conservation Zones

The government is creating an ecologically safe network of national Marine Conservation Zones (MCZs). These will be established following consultation with four English regional project groups:

  • Finding Sanctuary
  • Net Gain
  • Irish Sea Conservation Zones
  • Balanced Seas

You can see an interactive map showing activities of people with an interest in the maritime environment on the MCZ website.

Nature conservation byelaws

Byelaws exist to protect marine-protected areas from activities that might harm these sites. These laws are enforced in English inshore waters by the MMO. All fisheries byelaws will be introduced by inshore fisheries and conservation authorities.

There are two types of byelaw:

  • emergency byelaw - for an urgent need to protect a feature of the site, which must be replaced by a permanent byelaw after 12 months or withdrawn if it is no longer necessary
  • interim byelaw - if there is an urgent need for protection and the area will be designated in the future as a protected site
