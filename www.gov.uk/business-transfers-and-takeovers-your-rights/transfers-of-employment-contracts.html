Part 4: Transfers of employment contracts

In a protected under the TUPE (Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment)) regulations, your new employer takes over your employment contract, including:

  • all the previous terms and conditions of your employment
  • failure of your previous employer to observe your rights (so you could make a claim for discrimination against your new employer, even if it took place before the transfer)
  • your holiday entitlement
  • your period of continuous employment - your start date is the same as before the transfer, so your continuous employment isn’t broken
  • any collective agreements previously made with employees

If the new employer doesn’t meet the terms of your contract, it’s a breach of your contract.

If you don’t want to work for the new employer

You can refuse to work for the new employer. This means you’re resigning - you won’t be able to claim unfair dismissal or redundancy pay.

If your working conditions change for the worse because of the transfer, you can resign and claim unfair dismissal.

Changing your employment contract

TUPE regulations mean you shouldn’t lose your employment rights.

Before the transfer

If your employer knows you’ll be transferring to another company they can’t change your terms and conditions to make them the same as those of the other company.

After the transfer

Your employer can’t change your terms and conditions if the reason is:

  • the transfer itself
  • a reason connected with the transfer that is not an ‘economic, technical or organisational reason’

‘Economic’ reasons are to do with how the company is performing.

‘Technical’ reasons are to do with the equipment or processes the company uses.

‘Organisational’ reasons are to do with the structure of the company.

So if your employer wanted to change your terms and conditions for a reason not connected to the transfer they could.

Positive changes

Your employer can improve your terms and conditions if you agree. For example, they might want to increase your holiday so that it’s a standard amount for everyone.

An employer can’t impose changes - they have to be agreed by the employees or their representatives.

Pension rights

Your company pension rights earned up to the time of a transfer are protected, but your new employer doesn’t have to continue an identical pension.

They should offer a minimum pension matching your contributions of up to 6% of your salary.

After the transfer

When the transfer is complete, make sure you get an up-to-date written statement of your employment, giving the name of your new employer and saying that your terms and conditions haven’t changed.

You might get a P45 if your tax records are being updated.
