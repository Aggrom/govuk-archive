Part 1: Overview

The UK is a ‘parliamentary democracy’ with a ‘constitutional monarch’.

This means:

  • a king or queen is the head of state
  • a prime minister is the head of government
  • people vote in elections for MPs to represent them in Parliament

The Prime Minister

The Prime Minister is appointed by the Queen after a general election. He or she has a number of responsibilities including:

  • overseeing the Civil Service
  • appointing members of the Cabinet
  • keeping the Queen informed about the work of government

The Prime Minister is based at Number 10 Downing Street in London. There’s more information on the Number 10 website.

The Cabinet

This is the committee at the centre of the British political system. Cabinet ministers are appointed by the Prime Minister.

Most of them are heads of government departments with the title ‘Secretary of State’. Some have other titles, like the Chancellor of the Exchequer.

The Cabinet meets every Tuesday to discuss the current issues. The Prime Minister chairs the meeting and sets the agenda.

There are also Cabinet committees that meet in smaller groups with other government ministers.

You can find out more about the Cabinet on the Number 10 website.

Parliament

Parliament is based in Westminster in London. It is made up of the House of Commons and the House of Lords.

Its role is to:

  • look at what the government is doing
  • debate and pass new laws
  • set taxes
  • debate issues

There are 650 elected MPs in the House of Commons.

You can find out more about how Parliament works on the Parliament website.

Local government

Many decisions are made and carried out locally by councils.
