The UK system of government, a guide from GOV.UK

Notes

Part 1: Overview

The UK is a ‘parliamentary democracy’ with a ‘constitutional monarch’.

This means:

  • a king or queen is the head of state
  • a prime minister is the head of government
  • people vote in elections for MPs to represent them in Parliament

The Prime Minister

The Prime Minister is appointed by the Queen after a general election. He or she has a number of responsibilities including:

  • overseeing the Civil Service
  • appointing members of the Cabinet
  • keeping the Queen informed about the work of government

The Prime Minister is based at Number 10 Downing Street in London. There’s more information on the Number 10 website.

The Cabinet

This is the committee at the centre of the British political system. Cabinet ministers are appointed by the Prime Minister.

Most of them are heads of government departments with the title ‘Secretary of State’. Some have other titles, like the Chancellor of the Exchequer.

The Cabinet meets every Tuesday to discuss the current issues. The Prime Minister chairs the meeting and sets the agenda.

There are also Cabinet committees that meet in smaller groups with other government ministers.

You can find out more about the Cabinet on the Number 10 website.

Parliament

Parliament is based in Westminster in London. It is made up of the House of Commons and the House of Lords.

Its role is to:

  • look at what the government is doing
  • debate and pass new laws
  • set taxes
  • debate issues

There are 650 elected MPs in the House of Commons.

You can find out more about how Parliament works on the Parliament website.

Local government

Many decisions are made and carried out locally by councils.

Part 2: How laws are made

Laws go through several stages before they are passed by Parliament. The House of Lords and the House of Commons work together to make them.

White and green papers

White papers outline proposals for new laws. Before a white paper is published, there might be a green paper, which asks for comments from the public.

There’s no requirement that a white paper or green paper has to be produced.

Bills

These are proposals for new laws or changes to existing ones. They get debated by Parliament and when they have been agreed, they have to be approved by the Queen before becoming law. This is called ‘Royal Assent’.

Public bills

These are the most common type of bill. They change the law that applies to the general public. Most public bills are proposed by government ministers.

If you object to a public bill you can:

  • write to your MP or a Lord
  • contact the government department responsible for the bill
  • lobby Parliament

How bills become law

A bill passes through several stages in the House of Commons and the House of Lords to become law:

  • first reading (this is where the bill is introduced without debate)
  • second reading (general debate)
  • committee stage (the bill is examined in detail - in the House of Commons this often takes place in a Public Bill Committee)
  • report stage (opportunity for further amendments)
  • third reading (the House of Lords can make amendments at this stage)

Acts of Parliament

An act is a bill that has been approved by the House of Lords, the House of Commons and the Queen. It can come into force:

  • immediately
  • at a future date
  • in stages

The relevant government department is responsible for putting the act into practice.

Part 3: Government departments and agencies

Government departments and their agencies put government policy into practice. They also advise government ministers.

Departments and agencies are staffed by politically impartial civil servants and funded by Parliament.

Government departments

Some departments, like the Ministry of Defence, cover the UK as a whole. Other departments don’t - for example, the Department for Work and Pensions covers England, Wales and Scotland, but not Northern Ireland.

Most departments are headed by ministers - there’s a list of government ministers and departments on the Parliament website.

Executive agencies

These are part of government departments and deliver government services. They don’t set policy - this is done by the department that oversees the agency.

For example, Jobcentre Plus is an executive agency and part of the Department for Work and Pensions.

Non-departmental public bodies (NDPBs)

These work independently of ministers but are accountable to them. There are 2 main types of NDPB.

Executive NDPBs

These do work for the government in specific areas. They have varying degrees of independence. Examples include:

  • Arts Council England
  • the Environment Agency
  • the Health and Safety Executive

Advisory NDPBs

These are set up by ministers to advise on particular matters. Examples include:

  • the Committee on Standards in Public Life
  • the Low Pay Commission

Part 4: The Civil Service

The Civil Service does the practical and administrative work of government. Civil servants are the politically impartial employees who work in the Civil Service.

The role of civil servants

Civil servants work under the minister in charge of the department where they work. If the minister changes, this doesn’t involve a change in civil service staff.

The roles and responsibilities of civil servants are set out in the Civil Service Code.

The Minister for the Civil Service is the Prime Minister. He is responsible for co-ordination and management of the Civil Service.

There’s more information on the Civil Service website.

Where civil servants work

About half of all civil servants provide services directly to the public, including:

  • paying benefits and pensions
  • running employment services
  • staffing prisons
  • issuing driving licences

Around 1 in 5 civil servants are employed by the Ministry of Defence and its agencies. The rest are divided between:

  • central administrative and policy duties
  • support services
  • services that support themselves financially, like the Royal Mint

About 80% of civil servants work outside London.

Recruitment

The Civil Service Commissioners make sure that recruitment to the Civil Service is based on fair and open competition. They are independent of government.

You can look for Civil Service jobs online.

Part 5: Devolved government

In Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, ‘devolved governments’ have law-making powers on day-to-day issues.

They’re responsible for many things, including:

  • health
  • education
  • economic development
  • culture
  • the environment
  • transport

Scotland

The Scottish Parliament debates and passes law on most day-to-day issues in Scotland. It’s made up of 129 Members of the Scottish Parliament (MSPs) and looks at the work of the Scottish Government.

Wales

The National Assembly for Wales has 60 elected members and debates and approves laws in Wales. It monitors the Welsh Assembly Government as well as having legislative powers and representing Wales’s interest.

Northern Ireland

The Northern Ireland Assembly is made up of 108 elected members. It debates and makes laws and monitors the Northern Ireland Executive, which is the devolved government for Northern Ireland.

The nidirect website brings together government information for people in Northern Ireland.

Part 6: Accessing law and government archives

You can get information about UK law and access government publications, archives and documents.

UK law

The UK Statute Law Database has copies of all existing laws in the UK.

Parliamentary proceedings

The Official Report (Hansard) is the edited report of what goes on in the House of Lords and the House of Commons.

Members’ words are reported with repetition and obvious mistakes removed, but nothing is left out that adds to the meaning of what’s being said.

Daily debates are available from 6am the next working day.

Bills before Parliament

You can find details of the public bills before Parliament, as well as any explanatory notes relating to them, online.

Official publications

UK Official Publications is the official catalogue of government publications - you need to be a subscriber to access it.

The National Archives

The National Archives in Kew looks after the records of central government and makes sure everyone can look at them. You can visit the National Archives - it’s free and there’s no need to book.

The National Archives also holds a collection of archived government websites.
